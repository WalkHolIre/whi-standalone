About Dublin & Wicklow

Your guide to walking in this stunning region

Dublin: City Walking and Literary Heritage. Dublin became a UNESCO City of Literature in 2010. Start at Trinity College, where Beckett, Wilde, Yeats and Swift studied; the library holds the Book of Kells. Walk south to Merrion Square, where Wilde lived and plaques mark the homes of writers and thinkers. The National Library on Kildare Street is essential for anyone interested in the city's literary history. A proper literary walking tour traces locations mentioned in Joyce's Ulysses — from Sweny's Pharmacy, now a Joyce museum, to places where Leopold Bloom walked on 16 June 1904, commemorated annually as Bloomsday. The General Post Office marks the heart of the 1916 Rising. Kilmainham Gaol, now a museum, is where the rebellion's leaders were executed, a moment that changed Irish history and resonates through the city to this day. Dublin's other cultural currency is its pub culture. These are neighbourhood institutions where writers, musicians, students and locals gather. Walk five minutes from the crowded Temple Bar and you'll find the real Dublin: quiet bars with excellent Guinness, live traditional music several nights a week, and conversations meandering from politics to literature to sport.

The Landscape

South of the city, the granite Wicklow Mountains rise abruptly: glacial valleys, ribbon lakes and jagged ridges shaped over 500 million years. The coastal stretch from Dún Laoghaire to Bray alternates sandy beaches with rocky headlands, and Killiney Bay curves in a perfect crescent. Lower mountain slopes are oak and beech woodland; mid-levels open to heather bog; upper ridges above 600 metres offer views extending to Wales. Sika deer cross the moorland at dawn, peregrine falcons hunt the cliff faces, and otters inhabit the glacial lakes.

Culture & Heritage

Dublin is a UNESCO City of Literature: Joyce, Beckett, Wilde and Yeats all walked these streets, and you can trace their footsteps on a literary walking tour. Kilmainham Gaol tells the 1916 Rising story with real power. The pub culture is central to Dublin life, with live traditional music most nights. For food, try Dublin Bay prawns, fresh oysters from the Irish Sea, and the weekend markets at Temple Bar and Dún Laoghaire. A DART trip to Howth for seafood at the harbour is one of our favourite recommendations.

The walking season runs April to October. May and June are our top picks: long evenings, wildflowers on the moorland, and lighter trail traffic. September offers golden light, quieter trails and easier booking. July and August are warmest but Glendalough gets crowded; book accommodation 2 to 3 months ahead. Winter walking is possible with proper gear, and Dublin's literary scene is excellent year-round.

Who Is It For?

First-time visitors to Ireland who want city culture and wild landscapes without a full week of trekking. City-based walkers with 3 to 5 days who want variety: urban, coastal and mountain walking combined. Couples and groups with mixed abilities, since easy coastal routes sit alongside challenging mountain trails. Literary enthusiasts tracing Joyce, Beckett and Wilde through the streets that shaped their work. We also recommend this as a perfect bookend to the Wicklow Way: a celebratory few days in Dublin after a week in the mountains.

Where You'll Stay

We book quality guesthouses and B&Bs across three zones: Dublin city centre for cultural access; coastal towns like Dún Laoghaire and Bray, served by the DART with no car needed; and mountain villages like Laragh and Roundwood near Glendalough, where family-run guesthouses understand walkers' needs. Breakfast is always included, and we arrange luggage transfers for multi-day itineraries.

Getting Here

Dublin Airport (DUB) is one of Europe's most connected airports with direct services from North America, the Middle East and across Europe. The Airlink express bus connects the airport to O'Connell Street in approximately 25 to 30 minutes. From Dublin to Wicklow: Use the DART coastal railway to access walks at Dún Laoghaire, Dalkey, Killiney and Greystones; no car needed. Buy a Leap Card on arrival at the airport (covers all Dublin buses and the DART). Wicklow Mountains are 30 to 45 minutes by bus from Dublin city centre; ideal for day walks without overnight stays.
