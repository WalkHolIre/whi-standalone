The Glens of Antrim

Nine glacial valleys carved deep into the Antrim Plateau, dropping to a wild and beautiful coastline — one of Northern Ireland's most rewarding walking destinations and one of Europe's least-crowded mountain regions.

About the Region

The Glens of Antrim are nine deep valleys carving through the Antrim Plateau to the North Atlantic coast. Unlike the crowds of Wicklow or Ben Nevis, these glens remain wonderfully quiet. Valleys are lightly visited, villages small, distances manageable. Cushendall is the natural base, a small market town with good accommodation, genuine pubs and a vibrant trad music tradition on weekend evenings. Fair Head's basalt cliffs, Glenariff's waterfall gorge, Cushendun's caves and Rathlin Island's seabirds can all shape a week of walking and wandering.

Quick Facts

A Destination, Not a Single Trail. The Glens of Antrim work best as a walking destination rather than a linear trail. There's no single "Glens of Antrim Way" — instead, multiple options radiate from villages along the coast and into valleys. Some walks are short valley strolls through wooded glens; others are long moorland hikes across the plateau. Many combine coastal cliff walking with inland glen exploration. The beauty is that you build an itinerary around your fitness and preference, mixing easy walks with demanding ones and cultural stops with wild moorland days. Glenariff — The Queen of the Glens. Glenariff Forest Park is the most celebrated of the nine glens. The valley is wider and more generous than its neighbours, and the geology is exceptional. A gorge carved by water over millennia features a forest of oak, ash and beech cloaking the steep sides and a river maintaining a series of waterfalls as it descends towards the coast. The main walking route is the Waterfall Walk, an 8-kilometre circuit descending via steep woodland steps to the gorge floor, following a timber boardwalk along the river past cascading water and moss-covered rocks, and returning via a forest track. The walk passes 22 waterfalls in the first half-mile alone, with the Ess-na-Crub and Ess-na-Larach being the most dramatic. Fair Head — The Edge of the World. If Glenariff is the green heart of the Glens, Fair Head is the wild coast at their end. This walk demands clear days, when visibility stretches across the North Channel to the Mull of Kintyre and Scotland beyond. The headland is crowned with basalt cliffs rising over 100 metres, and the columnar basalt formations alone justify the effort.

The Landscape

The Antrim Plateau is windswept moorland coloured in heather purple and sedge green. The nine glens cut through like fingers, descending to wooded valleys and sea. The contrast is dramatic: within kilometres we move from exposed, boggy moorland to deep valleys with rivers and waterfalls. Basalt columnar formations at Fair Head create geometric cliff patterns. Lower valleys hold mixed woodland of oak, ash, beech and birch, many ancient. Autumn turns the glens copper and gold; spring carpets forest floors with bluebells. Wildlife is abundant. Red deer, golden eagles and peregrine falcons inhabit the moorland and cliffs. Rivers hold brown trout; coastal sections attract grey seals and basking sharks in summer. The coastline is dramatic with cliffs, caves and hidden beaches, genuinely wild and untamed.

Walking Difficulty

Walking ranges from easy to moderate. Easy walks include the Glenariff Waterfall Walk (8km with moderate ascent), riverside strolls and coastal beach walks at Cushendun. These suit families and those with limited hill experience. Moderate walks include the Fair Head ascent (significant moorland climb on exposed terrain), Murlough Bay (moorland and coastal descent) and full glen traverses. These require good fitness and proper footwear but are achievable for walkers with reasonable experience. All walks are accessible year-round, though winter plateau sections may require navigation experience.

Culture & Heritage

The Glens have distinct Gaelic Scottish heritage, rooted in families like the MacDonnells who controlled the region through the 17th century. Layde Church preserves clan graves. The landscape holds 4,000 years of Neolithic and medieval settlement. Contemporary culture thrives in Cushendall's vibrant traditional Irish music scene. Pubs host trad sessions regularly, especially weekends, attracting musicians from across Ireland; an authentic experience of living Irish culture. Dulse — dried Atlantic seaweed with salty, mineral flavour. Yellow Man toffee — brittle honeycomb sweet from Ballycastle's Ould Lammas Fair. Fresh seafood — chowder, crab and local fish in Ballycastle restaurants. Soda farls and potato bread — Northern Irish staples, perfect fuel before walks. Local hill lamb — pasture-raised on moorlands, served in village pub stews. Bushmills Whiskey — world's oldest licensed distillery, 20 minutes from Ballycastle

Getting Here

Belfast International Airport is 70 kilometres south with regular services from North America, Europe and the UK. Buses connect Belfast to Cushendall in about 90 minutes. Car hire from Belfast is straightforward and recommended; drive 90 kilometres via the A2 coastal route. Translink NI operates regional bus services; the Ulsterbus 162 connects Ballycastle to Belfast. The Rathlin Island Ferry runs from Ballycastle harbour (25 minutes), year-round with increased summer frequency; book in advance during peak season. The Glens use Sterling (GBP) currency; this is part of the United Kingdom.
