The Mourne Mountains & County Down

Northern Ireland's finest mountain range — granite peaks, the remarkable Mourne Wall linking 15 summits, and Slieve Donard rising 852m above the Irish Sea with views to five countries on a clear day.

About the Region

County Down's secret: the Mourne Mountains. Just an hour south of Belfast, this compact granite range of 15 major peaks rises dramatically from the coast, entirely different from rolling Wicklow hills. Slieve Donard at 852m is Northern Ireland's highest peak. On clear days the summit offers views to five countries: Ireland, Scotland, England, the Isle of Man and Wales. The Mourne Wall, a 35km dry-stone structure built 1904–1922, links 15 peaks. Hundreds of stonemasons carved granite blocks to enclose Silent Valley and protect Belfast's water supply. Newcastle is the obvious base: a Victorian seaside resort with good accommodation. Beyond the high ridges lies Tollymore Forest Park's ancient woodland, Silent Valley Reservoir, Rostrevor where C.S. Lewis found Narnia inspiration, and Murlough's sand dunes. This is mountain walking where landscape connects to history, literature and engineering.

Quick Facts

The Range and Its Character. The Mourne Mountains cover approximately 570 square kilometres of County Down, a compact granite range that feels separate from the rest of Ireland. Unlike Wicklow's high plateaus or Atlantic cliffs, the Mournes are steep-sided summits with deep valleys. Ancient, hard granite has resisted erosion through millennia. You step out of a seaside resort, walk an hour into true mountain wilderness, and you're alone in granite and sky. Slieve Donard is the emotional focal point. The standard approach from Newcastle follows the Glen River path through woodland, then breaks onto open mountainside with 750m ascent. A good day takes 4–5 hours return. The summit holds two ancient burial cairns, including the oldest and highest passage tomb in Ireland. On clear days the view ranges from the Scottish coast to the Hills of Wales. The Mourne Wall: Monument to Labour. Built 1904–1922 by the Belfast Water Commissioners, the Mourne Wall stretches 35km across 15 peaks, enclosing the 9,000-acre Silent Valley catchment. Granite dry-stone built by hundreds of stonemasons working each year, the project took 18 years and became one of the great mountain engineering achievements in these islands.

The Landscape

The Mourne Mountains are ancient, hard granite that resists weathering. Summits are topped with granite tors, rocky outcrops weathered into dramatic shapes. Steep-sided valleys and sharp ridgelines differ from glacially carved Wicklow valleys. In Tollymore's woodland valleys, oak and ash dominate, with Victorian-imported redwoods and exotic firs. Higher up, heather and cross-leaved heath colour the moorland purple and orange in late summer. Alpine plants cling to exposed ground near the summits. Sika deer inhabit woodlands; peregrine falcons hunt cliff faces; seals haul out on Murlough beaches. The coast contrast is striking. Newcastle beach is flat and wide with golden sand stretching kilometres. Murlough offers rolling sand dunes. Walking from forest to mountain to coast in a single day crosses entirely different ecological zones.

Walking Difficulty

The Mourne Mountains offer walking at multiple difficulty levels, from gentle forest walks to serious mountain terrain. Easy to Moderate: Tollymore Forest Park circuits (8–12km, minimal ascent), Newcastle to Murlough coastal walk (8–10km, flat), and Silent Valley circuit (5–8km, low elevation gain). These suit families and walkers with modest fitness. Challenging: Slieve Donard summit (4–5 hours, 750m ascent) and sections of the Mourne Wall over multiple peaks. These require good fitness, sure-footedness on rocky terrain and full mountain navigation experience. Ridges are exposed; weather changes rapidly. Expert-only: The full Mourne Wall circuit (35km over 15 summits) is a serious mountain undertaking requiring full mountain experience and excellent weather.

Culture & Heritage

County Down has been shaped by 1,500 years of Irish history. Saint Patrick is central; tradition holds he died and is buried at Downpatrick Cathedral. The Mourne Wall, built 1904 to 1922, is a monument to labour: hundreds of stonemasons worked exposed ridges, cutting and carrying granite blocks. Walking alongside, you touch stonework shaped by human hands across two decades. C.S. Lewis spent childhood holidays at Rostrevor; the Mourne Mountains appear throughout his writing. The view overlooking Carlingford Lough was his "idea of Narnia." Percy French's song, The Mountains of Mourne (1896), is the most famous cultural reference. Mourne seafood from Kilkeel — fresh fish in every coastal restaurant. Mourne Seafood Bar, Newcastle — post-walk dinner spot with daily catches. Ulster fry — proper full Irish breakfast before a big day on Slieve Donard. Local lamb — pasture-raised on the mountain slopes in hearty stews

Getting Here

Belfast International Airport (BFS) is the primary arrival point; a 60 kilometre drive south via the M1 motorway reaches Newcastle in approximately 60 to 75 minutes on the scenic A24 descent toward the coast. From Belfast city centre, Translink NI operates regular bus services to Newcastle Bus Station (75 to 90 minutes). Rental cars are readily available at both Belfast airports. Northern Ireland Railways offers limited rail services; the nearest station is Bangor, but services to County Down are minimal. Most walkers arrive by car or Translink bus. Northern Ireland uses British Sterling (GBP).
