The Sperrin Mountains & Mid Ulster

Broad moorland ridges, ancient gold-bearing rock and the most open skies in Northern Ireland — the Sperrins are an upland wilderness where solitude feels like a luxury.

About the Region

The Sperrin Mountains stretch across 80 kilometres of Mid Ulster, from County Derry/Londonderry in the north to County Tyrone in the south. They are Northern Ireland's largest upland area and genuinely underrated. While the Mournes draw crowds and the Antrim coast fills with tourists, the Sperrins offer something we love: genuine solitude and wilderness. The moorland horizons stretch for miles, and peace comes from walking where almost no one else ventures. The mountains have distinctive horizontal character; ridgelines are long and rolling rather than craggy, summits are blanket bog rather than bare rock. It's a landscape that rewards slowness and reflection, where open skies reward clear days and mist creates intimate walking.

Quick Facts

The Landscape and the Gold. The Sperrin Mountains form Northern Ireland's largest upland area, shaped by ancient metamorphic rock; schist and quartzite create ridges stretching 80km. Sawel Mountain at 678m is the highest point, offering unending views across Ulster. Gold is woven into the Sperrin story. Traces have been found in streams for thousands of years. Bronze Age metalworkers knew it was here. Geological surveys in the 1970s confirmed substantial deposits, still recovered from Sperrin streams. The broad moorland ridges differ from other Irish ranges. Unlike Wicklow's granite peaks or Antrim's basalt cliffs, the Sperrins unfold in long horizontal lines. Blanket bog covers summits; deep, waterlogged peat requiring good navigation in mist. Valleys like Glenelly and Owenkillew offer sheltered walking through farmland, forestry and river corridors. Wildlife is often seen at dawn or dusk. Red grouse flush from heather. Hen harriers hunt the moorland. Red kites recently returned to Ulster. Valleys support otters, badgers in oak woodland and foxes. Spring woods fill with redstarts, wood warblers and treecreepers.

The Landscape

The Sperrin Mountains span over 1,000 square kilometres across Mid Ulster as Northern Ireland's largest upland area. Unlike the Mourne Mountains with dramatic granite cones or the Antrim plateau with basalt cliffs, the Sperrins have distinctive horizontal character with broad, rolling ridgelines and uninterrupted moorland stretching kilometres. Metamorphic rock resists the sharp peaks that characterise other Irish ranges. Mountains rise in long moorland ridges separated by deep river valleys like Glenelly and Owenkillew, offering sheltered walking through farmland, oak woodland and river flats. Upper sections are mantled in blanket bog where views are unobstructed. The light has particular quality; open moorland lets the eye travel further than in other ranges. On clear days, you see across all Northern Ireland. Mist comes fast and the landscape becomes intimate. Seasonal changes are pronounced: spring brings breeding birds, summer turns heather purple, autumn colours the bracken. Sika deer, red grouse, golden plover, hen harriers and reintroduced red kites populate the landscape. Valleys support otters, badgers and foxes.

Walking Difficulty

Walking in the Sperrins is moderate overall, varying by route. Valley trails are gentle and suitable for families. The main Sperrin Way and higher mountain routes require reasonable fitness and proper hill walking skills. The greatest challenge is navigation. Open moorland can be disorienting in mist or poor light. Blanket bog underfoot is waterlogged even in dry periods. Waterproof footwear with good ankle support is essential. A detailed 1:25,000 OS map or GPS tracks are highly recommended for mountain sections. Sperrin Way waymarking is generally good, though open moorland sections require careful attention.

Culture & Heritage

The Sperrins are layered with history. Bronze Age monuments, stone circles, cairns and standing stones suggest an ancient people who found this landscape sacred. Beaghmore Stone Circles, seven Bronze Age circles, suggest ritual or astronomical significance; walking among them means walking through genuine prehistory. The linen industry marks the medieval period. The 18th and 19th centuries saw massive emigration to America; the Ulster American Folk Park tells this story through costumed guides and authentic buildings. Wellbrook Beetling Mill connects the landscape to Ulster's industrial heritage, with machinery in seasonal operation bringing that past into the present. Sperrin lamb and mountain mutton — pasture-raised on moorland, exceptional quality in local pubs. Ulster fry — the full cooked breakfast with sausage, bacon, egg and soda bread. Tyrone farmhouse cheeses — small-scale producers creating cheeses of distinction. Potato dishes — champ, boxty and colcannon; staple Mid Ulster fare

Getting Here

Belfast International Airport (BFS) is the primary arrival point with direct services from Europe and North America. Airlink express bus connects the airport to the city centre in approximately 30 minutes. From Belfast, Omagh is approximately 90 minutes by car or bus. City of Derry Airport (LDY) serves the western Sperrins, approximately 45 minutes from western Sperrin trailheads. Translink NI operates express services from Belfast Buscentre to Omagh (approximately 2 hours) or from Dublin (approximately 3 hours). By train: Train services run from Belfast Central and Dublin Connolly to Derry; approximately 2 hours from Belfast, 3.5 hours from Dublin. By car: Omagh is on the A5 between Belfast and Dublin; Derry is on the N13 route. All Sperrin trailheads are within 30 to 45 minutes' drive of these towns.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is included in a self-guided walking holiday? expand_more
Our self-guided tours include: carefully selected family-run B&B accommodation, comfortable en-suite bedrooms (where available), a full Irish breakfast each morning, complimentary luggage transfers between accommodations, detailed maps and route notes, local advice on places to visit and eat, full pre-departure information, a waterproof map case, and 24/7 local emergency contact support. Extra nights and local transport information can be arranged on request.
What is NOT included in the tour price? expand_more
The tour price does not include flights, travel and baggage insurance, lunches, or evening meals.
Is a GPS app or digital navigation included? expand_more
Yes. Our tours include a digital route guide with GPS navigation, offline maps, and local insights via our mobile app, so you can navigate confidently even without mobile signal.
Is 24/7 emergency support really available? expand_more
Yes. Our team provides 24/7 local emergency contact support throughout your tour. If you encounter any difficulties on the trail or with your accommodation, you can reach us at any time.
Are evening meals included? expand_more
Evening meals are not included in the standard tour price. Your B&B hosts can usually recommend nearby restaurants, and many guesthouses can arrange an evening meal on request.
Are lunches included? expand_more
Lunches are not included. However, you will typically have the opportunity to purchase picnic lunches, and we provide local advice on where to eat along the route.
What is the difference between a self-guided and a guided walking tour? expand_more
On a self-guided tour, you walk independently using our detailed maps, route notes, and GPS app, with your luggage transferred each day and 24/7 support available. A guided tour provides a local expert guide who walks with you, shares local knowledge, stories and history, and ensures you stay on track and safe. Guided tours also include built-in companionship with fellow walkers.
Which type of tour is best for first-time visitors to Ireland? expand_more
For first-time visitors, a guided tour is often recommended, as it provides expert support, helps you learn the trails, and offers the chance to meet other walkers. Experienced hikers frequently prefer the freedom and flexibility of self-guided tours at their own pace.
