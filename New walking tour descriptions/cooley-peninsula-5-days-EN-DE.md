### Day 2: Ravensdale to Lumpers (Cooley) – 12 km, 4 hours

Your first proper day takes you from Ravensdale into the moody heart of Cooley. The terrain unfolds in stages: forest roads beneath ancient trees, then opening to wide heather moorland where the peninsula reveals itself. The walking feels timeless here—you're treading paths used by cattle drovers and warriors for centuries.

The physical challenge builds steadily toward Black Mountain and Clairmont Cairn, where a steep final push rewards you with horizons that seem to stretch forever. On clear days, you'll spot the jagged Mourne Mountains rising in the northeast, and the curve of Carlingford Lough beginning to reveal itself below. The walk is moderately demanding but never technical—it's about endurance and savouring the views, not scrambling over rock.

You'll finish in Lumpers, a small hamlet that feels like you've stepped outside time. Here, the real world falls away entirely.

### Day 3: Lumpers to Carlingford – 16 km, 5 hours

The longest day, but also the most rewarding. From Lumpers, you rise again through moorland and forest, crossing the spine of the peninsula on ancient droving roads. The landscape is raw and quietly dramatic—often misty in the morning, often clearing to reveal those Mourne Mountains again, still distant and mysterious across the water.

As you descend, Carlingford Lough opens before you like a promise kept. The final stretch brings you down through gentler terrain, and suddenly you're walking into the narrow, colour-washed streets of medieval Carlingford. The medieval street layout, the Norman castle ruins, the sense of arrival—it's visceral. You've earned this moment.

*Distance: 16 km | Time: 5 hours | Difficulty: Moderate-Challenging | Overnight: Carlingford B&B (en-suite)*

### Day 4: Free Day in Carlingford

This is your day to breathe, explore, and reconnect. Carlingford is a walker's reward: narrow medieval streets, a working fishing village atmosphere, the Carlingford Lough waterfront, and some of the finest oysters you'll ever taste.

Optional walks abound—a gentle loop around the lough shore, a short climb to the ruins of King John's Castle for afternoon light, or a wander through the back lanes discovering local history. The village has excellent pubs, coffee shops, and restaurants. Spend the morning walking, the afternoon in Carlingford's King John's Castle Heritage Centre learning about Viking invasions and Norman heritage, and the evening tasting fresh oysters and talking about the trails with your walking companions.

## Best Time to Walk

**May to September** is prime walking season on the Cooley Peninsula, each month offering something different.

**May** brings lengthening daylight, wild flowers dotting the moorland, and a sense of awakening. Expect cool mornings and mild afternoons; layers are essential. Fewer walkers mean quieter trails and a more intimate connection with the landscape.

**June & July** offer the longest daylight hours and warmest temperatures—perfect if you want maximum daylight for exploration and photography. These are the busiest months, so early booking is wise. The heather will be in full bloom by July, turning moorland into purple tapestries.

**August** is warm and settled, ideal for those who prefer certainty in weather. Tourist season is in full swing, so the villages buzz with energy, and restaurant reservations become important.

**September** is arguably the best month: summer warmth lingers, crowds thin, and the light takes on a golden, autumnal quality. The heather is past its peak, but the clarity of views often compensates. It's also when many locals reclaim their landscape.

**Weather Note**: The Cooley Peninsula's moorland and coastal position mean weather changes quickly. Rain is possible any month. Pack waterproof layers, and remember that walking in mist has its own moody beauty—you're not missing the "real" experience; you're getting a different, equally authentic one.
