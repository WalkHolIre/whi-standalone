About De Causeway-kust

Your guide to walking in this stunning region

A Walk Along the Edge of Time

We trace the Causeway Coast Way from east to west, from Ballycastle to Portstewart, in daily stages of 12 to 20 kilometres. The landscape is built on basalt, volcanic rock that cooled 60 million years ago into extraordinary hexagonal columns. You are walking on the rim of something ancient and powerful.

The Giant's Causeway

The Giant's Causeway sits midway along the walk. Its 40,000 interlocking basalt columns rise from the shore in a geometric pattern formed when volcanic lava cooled slowly and fractured. Northern Ireland's only UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognised for exceptional geological significance.

Visit at dawn or dusk when the light transforms the columns and the crowds thin out. The legend of Finn McCool, who supposedly built the Causeway as a bridge to Scotland, is poetic if not geological fact.

Carrick-a-Rede Rope Bridge

East of the Causeway, near Ballintoy, the Carrick-a-Rede Rope Bridge spans 20 metres, suspended 30 metres above the sea. Built by salmon fishermen in 1755, it connects the mainland to a small island. The bridge is narrow with a real drop beneath your feet. Book a timed entry online in the summer. The view from the island is outstanding.

Dunluce Castle

Perched on basalt cliffs above the Atlantic, Dunluce Castle dominates the western section of the walk. First built by the MacQuillan family around 1500, it was seized by the MacDonnell clan in the 1550s and became one of the great centres of power in mediaeval Ulster. Legend has it that one stormy night in 1639, the castle kitchens fell into the sea. Only a fiddler survived, huddled in the one corner that held. It stands today as one of the most dramatically sited mediaeval ruins in Ireland.

Bushmills Distillery

The world's oldest licensed whiskey distillery stands near Bushmills, its royal licence granted in 1608. For over 400 years, whiskey has been made here using water from the River Bush and skills passed between generations. Tours include the still house, barrel stores and tastings. Many walkers finish their day at Bushmills with an evening distillery visit. A perfectly Irish way to mark a long day on the coast.

Het Landschap

The Causeway Coast is built on basalt, ancient volcanic rock cooled 60 million years ago into iconic hexagonal columns. At the Giant's Causeway and Fair Head, the columns are regular and geometric. Elsewhere, weathering has created cliffs, sea stacks and dramatic overhangs. Sandy bays shelter between cliff sections. Whiterocks Beach gleams white against dark rock. Cliffs rise 100 meters or more, battered by constant Atlantic swells.

Guillemots, razorbills, kittiwakes and fulmars nest in the cliff faces by the thousands. Rathlin Island hosts one of Europe's largest seabird colonies. On clear days, Scotland is visible 20 kilometres away. Coastal vegetation is salt-tolerant, low-growing scrub and grasses shaped by the constant Atlantic wind.

Culture & Heritage

The Causeway Coast springs from geology, legend, clan history and 400 years of whiskey tradition.

The Giant's Causeway was studied intensively in the 18th and 19th centuries, helping establish the geological principles we understand today. Mythologically, Finn McCool built it as a bridge to Scotland. Dunluce Castle connects to the MacDonnell clan and mediaeval Ulster. Its story of the 1639 kitchen collapse is one of Ireland's most retold legends, dramatic whether or not every detail is literal.

Bushmills Distillery represents continuous whiskey-making since 1608, longer than any other licensed distillery in the world.

For food and drink along the route, look out for:

  • Bushmills whiskey, 10, 12 and 16-year single malts, difficult to find outside Northern Ireland

  • Dulse, dried seaweed from the Antrim coast, a traditional snack sold in Ballycastle market

  • Yellow Man, honeycomb toffee sold at the Ould Lammas Fair and in local shops

  • Fresh seafood, chowder, crab claws and smoked salmon from Ballycastle and Portrush harbour restaurants

  • Ulster fry, the traditional Northern Irish cooked breakfast before a long day on the coast

Bezienswaardigheden

Key highlights you'll discover in De Causeway-kust

Giant's Causeway

40,000 interlocking basalt columns formed 60 million years ago, Northern Ireland's only UNESCO World Heritage Site. Visit at dawn or dusk for the finest light and the quietest experience.

Carrick-a-Rede Rope Bridge

A 20-metre suspension bridge 30 metres above the sea, built by salmon fishermen in 1755. The view from the island on the far side is worth every step of the crossing.

Dunluce Castle

A mediaeval MacDonnell stronghold perched on basalt cliffs, with a history of clan power, Spanish Armada gold and a famous legend of a stormy night in 1639 when the kitchen fell into the sea. One of the most dramatically sited mediaeval ruins in Ireland.

Bushmills Distillery

The world's oldest licensed whiskey distillery, operating since 1608. Tours take you through the still house, barrel stores and tasting room. A fine way to end a walking day.

Things to Do in De Causeway-kust

Top activities and experiences in the area

hiking

Walking the full 53km Causeway Coast Way

East to west from Ballycastle to Portstewart over 3 to 5 days, following coastal paths and cliff-top routes past some of the most dramatic scenery in Ireland. The trail is well waymarked and manageable for walkers of reasonable fitness.

landscape

Giant's Causeway at dawn or dusk

The 40,000 basalt columns look entirely different in early morning or evening light. Building your itinerary around a quiet visit here is one of the best decisions you can make on this walk.

landscape

Carrick-a-Rede Rope Bridge crossing

Book a timed entry slot online and go early or late to avoid the midday crowds. The crossing takes nerve, not skill, and the view from the island makes it entirely worthwhile.

landscape

Rathlin Island day trip

Reachable by ferry from Ballycastle, Rathlin is Northern Ireland's only inhabited offshore island and home to 250,000 seabirds from April to July. Puffins are the headline act. Allow a full day.

Who Is It For?

The Causeway Coast Way suits walkers wanting dramatic geology and cultural history without mountain terrain. It is excellent for first-time visitors to Northern Ireland and anyone drawn to geology, mediaeval history, whiskey-making or Game of Thrones filming locations.

Couples, solo walkers and friend groups all do well here. Photographers appreciate the exceptional light on basalt at dawn and dusk. The trail suits new long-distance walkers, with manageable daily stages and clear waymarking throughout. It is also appropriate for families with teenage children.

This is not the right choice for those seeking wilderness solitude or high mountain challenge.

Where You'll Stay

We book quality guesthouses and B&Bs at each stop along the route. Ballycastle is the largest town with the widest choice of accommodation. Bushmills has real character, sitting within walking distance of both the distillery and the Giant's Causeway. Portrush is a lively seaside resort with excellent seafood restaurants and a full range of options.

All properties are selected for walkers, with hosts who understand what you need at the end of a long day on the coast. Breakfast is included at every stop. Your bags are transferred ahead each day, so you only carry a light daypack on the trail.

Please note: Northern Ireland uses sterling (GBP), not the euro.

Getting Here

By Air

  • Belfast International Airport (BFS) is the primary arrival point, approximately 80 to 90 minutes from Ballycastle by road

  • George Best Belfast City Airport (BHD) offers an alternative, also within easy reach of the Causeway Coast

By Train and Bus

  • Translink NI Railways runs from Belfast to Coleraine, with local bus connections to Ballycastle

  • Coach services connect Belfast airports to Ballycastle in approximately 1.5 to 2 hours

Reistips

lightbulb Book the Giant's Causeway in advance expand_more

Book online before you travel and plan your visit for early morning or late afternoon when the light is better and the crowds are thinner.

backpack The coast is exposed, dress accordingly expand_more

The Causeway Coast Way faces the Atlantic directly and conditions change quickly. Wind-resistant layers matter as much as waterproofs on this route, even in summer. Pack both regardless of the forecast.

checkroom Check your ETA requirements before you travel expand_more

Visitors from many countries outside the EU and UK now require an Electronic Travel Authorisation to enter the United Kingdom, which includes Northern Ireland. It is a simple online application but must be completed before you travel. Check current requirements and apply at GOV.UK Electronic Travel Authorisation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is included in a self-guided walking holiday? expand_more
Our self-guided tours include: carefully selected family-run B&B accommodation, comfortable en-suite bedrooms (where available), a full Irish breakfast each morning, complimentary luggage transfers between accommodations, detailed maps and route notes, local advice on places to visit and eat, full pre-departure information, a waterproof map case, and 24/7 local emergency contact support. Extra nights and local transport information can be arranged on request.
What is NOT included in the tour price? expand_more
The tour price does not include flights, travel and baggage insurance, lunches, or evening meals.
Is a GPS app or digital navigation included? expand_more
Yes. Our tours include a digital route guide with GPS navigation, offline maps, and local insights via our mobile app, so you can navigate confidently even without mobile signal.
Is 24/7 emergency support really available? expand_more
Yes. Our team provides 24/7 local emergency contact support throughout your tour. If you encounter any difficulties on the trail or with your accommodation, you can reach us at any time.
Are evening meals included? expand_more
Evening meals are not included in the standard tour price. Your B&B hosts can usually recommend nearby restaurants, and many guesthouses can arrange an evening meal on request.
Are lunches included? expand_more
Lunches are not included. However, you will typically have the opportunity to purchase picnic lunches, and we provide local advice on where to eat along the route.
What is the difference between a self-guided and a guided walking tour? expand_more
On a self-guided tour, you walk independently using our detailed maps, route notes, and GPS app, with your luggage transferred each day and 24/7 support available. A guided tour provides a local expert guide who walks with you, shares local knowledge, stories and history, and ensures you stay on track and safe. Guided tours also include built-in companionship with fellow walkers.
Which type of tour is best for first-time visitors to Ireland? expand_more
For first-time visitors, a guided tour is often recommended, as it provides expert support, helps you learn the trails, and offers the chance to meet other walkers. Experienced hikers frequently prefer the freedom and flexibility of self-guided tours at their own pace.
