Eight Days on the Barrow Way — Ireland's Most Relaxed Long-Distance Walk

The Barrow Way is one of the few long-distance hiking routes in Ireland where you can actually look around while you walk. No scrambling, no steep climbs, no watching your footing on a loose mountain path. Just flat, peaceful riverside trails and canal towpaths, following the River Barrow south through some of Ireland's quietest and most historic countryside.

This 8-day self-guided walking holiday takes you from Kildare Town to Graiguenamanagh, with your final day on the trail bringing you to the ancient monastic site at St Mullins. You walk approximately 114 km across six walking days, at whatever pace suits you best. Your bags travel ahead each morning. Your B&B is ready each evening. And I am always at the end of the phone if you need anything along the way.

Why Eight Days Rather Than Five?

We also offer a 5-day Barrow Way tour for walkers who want a shorter trip. But eight days gives you something the shorter option simply cannot: time to stop. Time to wander through Carlow's castle grounds without checking the clock, to linger over lunch in Borris, or to sit by the river at Graiguenamanagh and just listen. Long-distance hiking tours are at their best when the pace is honest, and eight days on the Barrow Way is an honest pace.

We Can Adjust This to Suit You

Every walking holiday we run is personal. If there is a section you would prefer to shorten, an extra night you would like to add, or a day you want to spend exploring a town rather than walking, get in touch and we will sort it. That is what makes booking directly with a small operator worth it — you are not locked into a fixed itinerary.

The Barrow Way suits walkers who are active and enjoy covering distance on foot but who would rather spend their evenings comfortable and well-fed than exhausted. It is a particularly excellent fit if you have been curious about multi-day trekking or inn-to-inn hiking in Ireland but have not been sure whether the mountain routes are right for you. The Barrow gives you the full long-distance experience without the demanding terrain.

Add a Night in Kilkenny

Kilkenny is one of Ireland's finest mediaeval cities, and it sits less than 30 minutes from the southern end of the Barrow Way. Many of our guests choose to add one or two nights in Kilkenny before or after their walk — and it is well worth considering.

The city is compact and easy to explore on foot. Kilkenny Castle, the Black Abbey, the Tholsel, the narrow lanes of the mediaeval quarter — there is enough here to fill two days comfortably. The food and pub scene is among the best in the country, and it makes an ideal way to settle in before your first walking day, or to celebrate properly at the end of your trip.

If you would like to add Kilkenny to your itinerary, just mention it when you get in touch. We will arrange the accommodation and any transfers as part of your booking.

Hoogtepunten

Voor wie is deze tour?

Fitness Level

The Barrow Way is graded easy to moderate. The terrain is flat throughout, which makes the daily distances manageable for most walkers. You will cover between 17 and 24 km on your longest days, so a reasonable level of fitness helps — but you do not need to be a seasoned hillwalker or experienced trekker to enjoy this route. If you walk regularly and are comfortable on your feet for four to six hours, you will be well prepared.

Perfect For

This tour is a particularly good fit for couples and groups of friends who want to share a week outdoors without the pressure of a demanding mountain route. Many of our guests on the Barrow Way are in their 50s, 60s, and 70s, and several have described it as their first experience of multi-day walking tours in Ireland. It also suits walkers returning after a break from hillwalking, anyone exploring Ireland's Ancient East for the first time, or those who simply want to move through the landscape at a pace that lets them actually see it. Solo walkers are very welcome — the route's quiet character makes it well suited to walking at your own rhythm.

Reisschema

Day 1

Arrival in Kildare

expand_more

Make your way to Kildare town, where your walking holiday begins. Kildare is a beautiful introduction to Ireland's Ancient East — home to the famous Curragh racecourse, the Japanese Gardens, and the Irish National Stud. Settle into your accommodation, get your walking gear ready, and enjoy a relaxed dinner. Tomorrow, the Barrow begins.

Day 2

Monasterevin to Athy

expand_more
pin_drop Monasterevin → Athyfavorite_route 22.5 kmlandscape ↑21mlandscape ↓24mschedule 4h 30m

Your first full day on the trail. You pick up the Barrow Way at Monasterevin — where the Grand Canal meets the River Barrow — and follow the towpath south to the heritage town of Athy.

The walking is flat and rhythmic. Early on, you'll see the dramatic ruins of Dunamase fortress rising from the plains to your east. On a clear day, you can spot the Wicklow Mountains in the distance. The countryside here feels unspoilt and calm — you'll pass lifting bridges, old lock-keeper's cottages, and the occasional narrowboat moored for the season.

Athy itself is worth exploring. This Anglo-Norman town was established in the 13th century to control the river crossing, and its castle, Dominican church, and heritage centre tell that story well.

Day 3

Athy to Carlow

expand_more
pin_drop Athy → Carlowfavorite_route 19.4 kmlandscape ↑60mlandscape ↓72mschedule 4h

Leaving Athy, you join the River Barrow itself — the canal towpath gives way to the riverbank, and the character of the walk shifts. The river widens and slows, flanked by reeds and willows.

Along this stretch you'll pass old mills and traditional lifting bridges, each one a small piece of industrial history. Carlow is the largest town on the Barrow, and arriving on foot along the riverside feels like the right way to arrive. Check out Carlow Castle — remarkably intact despite centuries of history — and Carlow Cathedral before dinner.

Day 4

Carlow to Leighlinsbridge/Bagenalstown

expand_more

A slightly shorter day, which gives you time to linger. About 7 km south of Carlow you reach Milford — one of the most picturesque spots on the entire Barrow. Three bridges cross the river here, surrounded by woodland and the shell of a large old mill. It's a perfect spot for lunch and a rest.

The walking continues south through increasingly pastoral countryside. Bagenalstown (locally known as Muinebheag) is a neat Georgian village with good food and genuine Irish hospitality.

Day 5

Leighlinsbridge/Bagenalstown to Borris

expand_more

Heading south from Leighlinsbridge or Bagenalstown, the Barrow Way connects briefly with the Leinster Way — Ireland's second-longest waymarked trail. Near Ballytiglea Bridge, you leave the towpath and walk the final 2 km to Borris.

Borris is a village worth savouring. A wide main street lined with stone buildings, the grand gates of Borris House visible at the far end, rolling hills rising behind. It has a quietness that feels deliberate rather than sleepy. This is a pleasant evening to sit outside with a pint and feel the satisfaction of five days of walking in your legs.

Day 6

Borris to Graiguenamanagh

expand_more

Rejoining the river south of Borris, today's walk carries you through some of the most beautiful stretches of the entire route. The Barrow Valley narrows here, and the wooded hillsides close in on both sides of the river. This is the section many guests remember most clearly.

Graiguenamanagh is a lovely base for your final two nights — same bed, no packing, no rushing. The town sits at the foot of Brandon Hill beside the river, with the magnificent Duiske Abbey at its centre — one of the best-preserved Cistercian abbeys in Ireland. Good restaurants, a warm welcome, and your bags already waiting.

Day 7

Graiguenamanagh to St. Mullins

expand_more

The looped walk from Graiguenamanagh takes you downstream along the Barrow to St. Mullins, one of Ireland's oldest and most atmospheric ecclesiastical settlements. Founded by St. Moling in the 7th century, St. Mullins sits where the River Barrow meets the Duiske, surrounded by ancient monastic ruins, a round tower stump, and a graveyard that has been in continuous use for over 1,400 years.

The peace here is real. You'll feel it.

The loop brings you back along higher ground with views over the valley before returning to Graiguenamanagh. At just 12 km, this is a day short enough to enjoy very slowly.

Day 8

Departure from Graiguenamanagh

expand_more

A leisurely morning. A last full Irish breakfast. A last look at the river.

Depart from Graiguenamanagh and make your way onward. If you're flying from Dublin, it's approximately 1.5 hours by road. Many guests add a night in Kilkenny city (just 40 minutes away) before heading home — it's well worth it.

Accommodatie

I have personally checked every guesthouse on this tour. I don't book accommodation I haven't seen — or at least spoken to the owner about directly. You'll stay in family-run B&Bs along the route, all en-suite, all serving a full Irish breakfast in the morning.

Your main bag travels ahead by luggage transfer each day, so you walk with just a small day pack—typically 20–30L. Arriving at your guesthouse to find your luggage already there is one of those small pleasures that never gets old.

Most of the owners along the Barrow have been welcoming walkers for years. They'll recommend where to eat, tell you about the local history, and make you feel genuinely at home. That kind of hospitality isn't something you can manufacture — it's just the Barrow Valley way.

May, June and September are ideal.

  • May offers long bright evenings, fresh wildflowers and bright green foliage.

  • June provides the longest daylight hours.

  • July and August are busiest; book three to four months ahead.

  • September is the finest: bracken turns gold and rust, light on the water is clear and beautiful, wildflowers remain, and accommodation books more easily.

  • October brings autumn colours and is very walkable.

The trail is accessible year-round, but winter sections can be muddy with shorter daylight.

Veelgestelde Vragen

What is included in a self-guided walking holiday? expand_more
Our self-guided tours include: carefully selected family-run B&B accommodation, comfortable en-suite bedrooms (where available), a full Irish breakfast each morning, complimentary luggage transfers between accommodations, detailed maps and route notes, local advice on places to visit and eat, full pre-departure information, a waterproof map case, and 24/7 local emergency contact support. Extra nights and local transport information can be arranged on request.
What is NOT included in the tour price? expand_more
The tour price does not include flights, travel and baggage insurance, lunches, or evening meals.
Is a GPS app or digital navigation included? expand_more
Yes. Our tours include a digital route guide with GPS navigation, offline maps, and local insights via our mobile app, so you can navigate confidently even without mobile signal.
Is 24/7 emergency support really available? expand_more
Yes. Our team provides 24/7 local emergency contact support throughout your tour. If you encounter any difficulties on the trail or with your accommodation, you can reach us at any time.
Are evening meals included? expand_more
Evening meals are not included in the standard tour price. Your B&B hosts can usually recommend nearby restaurants, and many guesthouses can arrange an evening meal on request.
Are lunches included? expand_more
Lunches are not included. However, you will typically have the opportunity to purchase picnic lunches, and we provide local advice on where to eat along the route.
What is the difference between a self-guided and a guided walking tour? expand_more
On a self-guided tour, you walk independently using our detailed maps, route notes, and GPS app, with your luggage transferred each day and 24/7 support available. A guided tour provides a local expert guide who walks with you, shares local knowledge, stories and history, and ensures you stay on track and safe. Guided tours also include built-in companionship with fellow walkers.
Which type of tour is best for first-time visitors to Ireland? expand_more
For first-time visitors, a guided tour is often recommended, as it provides expert support, helps you learn the trails, and offers the chance to meet other walkers. Experienced hikers frequently prefer the freedom and flexibility of self-guided tours at their own pace.
