# Hiking in Donegal: Slieve League, Mount Errigal and Hidden Trails

Donegal holds a special place in the hearts of Irish walkers. It's a region where mountain ridges meet wild Atlantic cliffs, where quiet valleys hide ancient paths, and where the landscape feels genuinely untamed. If you're dreaming of hiking in Donegal, you're in for something really memorable—these trails offer the kind of dramatic scenery and authentic Irish countryside that keeps walkers returning year after year.

I've walked these hills myself, and what strikes me most is how accessible the best routes remain. Whether you're tackling the vertiginous heights of Slieve League, conquering Mount Errigal's distinctive cone, or exploring the quieter gems of Glenveagh National Park, Donegal delivers in every season. The key is knowing which trail suits your pace and fitness level, what to pack for Donegal's moody weather, and where to base yourself for the most rewarding walking holiday.

In this guide, I'm sharing everything I've learned about hiking in Donegal—the classics, the hidden trails, practical logistics, and honest talk about what makes each route special. Whether you're planning a single day hike or a multi-day tour, you'll find the detail you need to make the most of these extraordinary hills.

## Why Donegal is a Walker's Dream

Donegal's reputation among serious hikers is well-earned. The county boasts some of Ireland's most dramatic terrain: U-shaped glacial valleys, towering sea cliffs, and mountain passes that seem to belong in the Alps. Yet it remains delightfully less crowded than the Wicklow Mountains, meaning you can walk for hours and genuinely feel like you've discovered something secret.

The weather here is Atlantic-facing and changeable—expect everything from crystal-clear autumn mornings to horizontal rain and dramatic cloud-lifting reveals. That unpredictability is part of the appeal. Walkers who embrace Donegal's moody character often find it the most rewarding and memorable walking holiday in Ireland.

### The Landscape and Climate

Donegal sits at Ireland's northwest shoulder, where the Atlantic has carved the coastline into spectacular cliffs and the interior mountains remain comparatively wild. The granite peaks, bog plateaus, and deep glens create enormous variety in trail character and difficulty.

Weather-wise, prepare for Irish Atlantic conditions: wind is your constant companion, rain arrives frequently but often passes quickly, and visibility can change dramatically within hours. Summer temperatures range from 14–18°C; winter drops to 4–8°C. The wind chill is real. But this is also why Donegal feels so alive—you're walking in genuinely wild landscape, not a park-managed experience.

## The Classic Routes: What Makes Each Special

### Slieve League: The Dramatic Coastal Climb

Slieve League (Sliabh Liag) isn't just Donegal's highest sea cliff—it's one of Ireland's most cinematogenic walking routes, and the reputation is completely justified. The cliffs rise 601 metres straight from the Atlantic, making them nearly twice the height of their famous Cliffs of Moher cousins. But it's not just about the vertical drama; it's the sense of exposure, the howling Atlantic wind, and those moments when you round a ridge and the landscape simply stops, dropping away into blue.

**The Classic Slieve League Ridge Walk (8–9 km, 4–5 hours, moderate to strenuous):**
This is the walker's choice. Rather than tackling it from below (a steep, boggy slog), most experienced hikers start from the Cliff Edge carpark and traverse the dramatic ridge, descending toward Carrigan Head. You'll encounter One Man's Pass—a narrow, exposed section that looks genuinely intimidating but is perfectly manageable with care and common sense. The scrambling is minimal; the exposure is maximum. Walkers with a head for heights will find it exhilarating; those prone to vertigo might prefer approaching from below.

The coastal views are astonishing: you're looking down at storm-carved caves, out toward Tory Island and the Aran Islands, and back across Donegal Bay. The ridge walk captures what makes Donegal special—genuine wildness, minimal infrastructure, and the feeling that you're walking somewhere few people venture.

**Slieve League Loop (10 km, 5–6 hours, strenuous):**
If you want the full experience without the scramble, the longer loop combines the ridge traverse with gentler moorland walking. You'll climb from Carrigan Head via open bog, cross high plateaus with 360-degree views, and descend back toward the coast. It's tougher underfoot (bog sections can be wet and slow) but equally rewarding.

**Getting to Slieve League:** Head to Carrick (a small village in southwest Donegal) and follow signs toward Teelin. The Cliff Edge carpark is the trailhead. Allow 1.5 hours' drive from Donegal town. The road narrows significantly as you approach; in peak season, carpark capacity is limited. Going early is wise.

### Mount Errigal: The Signature Cone

Mount Errigal (Portuguese: An Earagail) is Donegal's most iconic mountain—that distinctive conical peak you've probably seen in photographs, sitting alone in the northeast of the county. At 752 metres, it's not the highest in Donegal, but its isolated position and perfect cone shape make it feel grander than its altitude suggests.

**The Direct Ascent (5 km, 2.5–3.5 hours, moderate):**
From the Dunlewy carpark, a well-trodden but steep path climbs the northeast flank, gaining 700 metres of elevation. The lower slopes are grassy, but the upper mountain is scree and loose rock—watch your footing, especially on descent. The reward is a summit with views stretching across Donegal's interior mountains, westward to the Atlantic, and on clear days across to the Scottish coast.

The mountain attracts weekend crowds (especially in summer), but it's busy rather than overcrowded. Start early if you prefer solitude. The scree descent is fun and quick; many walkers actually enjoy the descent more than the climb.

**Why Errigal Matters:** It's approachable enough for confident intermediate walkers, dramatic enough to feel genuinely mountainous, and iconic enough that summiting it becomes a true memory marker. If you're spending a walking holiday in Donegal, Errigal absolutely deserves a day.

**Getting to Errigal:** Head toward Gweedore (An Gaoth Dobhair) in central Donegal. The Dunlewy carpark is well signposted. About 45 minutes from Donegal town.

## The Hidden Gems: Beyond the Classics

### Glenveagh National Park Routes

Glenveagh National Park deserves its own article (and we've written one if you want deep detail), but it's essential to mention here because it offers something different from Slieve League and Errigal. Instead of exposed peaks, Glenveagh delivers deep, green valleys, clear mountain rivers, and genuine solitude.

**The Doon Level Trail (3 km, 1.5 hours, easy):**
This is Glenveagh at its most accessible. A waymarked path follows an old estate road through native woodland, alongside the pristine Doon River. It's flat, scenic, and perfect for walkers seeking something gentler or families with children. The river views are peaceful; the woodland feels genuinely secluded. In summer, the glen is emerald green; in autumn, the colours are extraordinary.

**The Spinetail Trail (6 km, 3–4 hours, moderate):**
For something more substantial, this circular route climbs from glen floor to open moorland, offering panoramic views across the park's mountains and down to Lough Veagh. The upper sections are exposed; weather and visibility matter here. But on clear days, the 360-degree views make it feel like you're on top of the world. Walkers describe it as the route that best captures Glenveagh's scale and grandeur.

**Why Glenveagh Matters:** It's where Irish wilderness feels intimate rather than intimidating. The park has excellent infrastructure (visitor centres, facilities), but the wild landscape feels completely preserved. If you're doing a multi-day Donegal walking holiday, Glenveagh should anchor one or two days.

### The Donegal Bay Coastal Paths

The coastline west of Slieve League offers quieter coastal walking with dramatically different character. The cliffs here are lower but equally beautiful, and the paths feel more like genuine trails than the more popular routes.

**Malinbeg to Glencolmcille (10 km, 4–5 hours, moderate):**
This coastal walk winds along sea cliffs, through hidden coves, and past ancient archaeological sites. The path dips and climbs repeatedly (hence the duration for distance), but the views are magical. You're walking past sea-stacks, looking down at wild-looking coves, and crossing moorland with unobstructed Atlantic views. It's less crowded than Slieve League but equally spectacular. The village of Glencolmcille at the end has excellent pubs and restaurants—perfect for refuelling after the walk.

**Fintra Head Loop (7 km, 3–4 hours, moderate):**
West of Glencolmcille, this loop climbs toward headland with sweeping coastal views, then descends to a wild pebble beach. It's the kind of route where you'll likely meet more sheep than walkers. The weather changes dramatically out here; get this wrong, and it's surprisingly exposed. But on clear days, it's magical.

## Practical Essentials: Getting Here, When to Go, Where to Stay

### Getting to Donegal

**By Car:** The most practical option. Donegal is 3–4 hours' drive from Dublin, 2 hours from Derry. Roads are good but narrow in places; allow extra time in winter or poor visibility.

**By Bus:** Bus Éireann and GoBus operate routes from Dublin and Derry to Donegal town (the main hub). Journey times are long (5+ hours from Dublin) but affordable. From Donegal town, local buses serve main villages, though connections to trailheads can be limited. Combine with local taxis for mountain access.

**Where to Base Yourself:**
- **Donegal Town:** The obvious hub. Good accommodation range, restaurants, pubs, and bus connections. Slightly touristy but genuinely welcoming.
- **Carrick:** Closer to Slieve League (ideal if that's your focus). Smaller, quieter, excellent local accommodation and restaurants.
- **Gweedore/Dunlewy:** Central for Errigal and Glenveagh routes. More Irish-language area; authentic feel; good walking community.
- **Glencolmcille:** Perfect base for coastal routes. Remote, beautiful, simple accommodation. Genuinely feels like the edge of Ireland.

### Best Season

- **Summer (June–August):** Longest days, most reliable weather (though rain is still frequent), busiest trailheads. Midges can be annoying in valleys.
- **Autumn (September–October):** Ideal season. Weather improving, fewer crowds, spectacular colours, and clearer air. September is often the driest month.
- **Spring (April–May):** Lengthening days, wildflowers (especially bluebells and rhododendrons), greening landscape. Weather still unpredictable.
- **Winter (November–March):** Shortest days, harshest weather, but dramatic clouds and light. Beautiful for experienced walkers; challenging for others.

### What to Pack

Donegal weather is Atlantic-facing and genuinely changeable. Pack:
- Waterproof jacket and trousers (essential, not optional)
- Windproof layers
- Warm mid-layer fleece or merino
- Waterproof pack cover
- Good hiking boots with ankle support (scree and boggy sections demand them)
- Spare socks and hat
- Sunscreen and sunglasses (UV is strong at altitude, even on overcast days)
- Waterproof bag for phone and essentials
- Snacks and water bottle

Weather can change in minutes. The principle is "prepare for the worst, hope for the best."

## Practical Tips for Hiking in Donegal

### Safety Considerations

- **Weather:** Conditions change rapidly. Always check forecasts before heading out, but expect them to be partly wrong. Visibility can drop from excellent to near-zero within minutes.
- **Navigation:** Trails are generally waymarked, but in poor visibility, a map and compass (or GPS) are essential. Phone signal is patchy at altitude.
- **Bog Walking:** Much of Donegal is bog. Paths can be wet and slippery. Bog is slower than grass—allow extra time. Never try to "shortcut" across bog if the path disappears; return to your last known point and navigate carefully.
- **Mountain Streams:** After rain, streams become torrents. Crossings that are trivial in dry weather become dangerous. Don't attempt to cross fast water.

### Making the Most of Your Walking Holiday

- **Start with One Man's Pass:** If you do Slieve League, the ridge is unforgettable. It's exposed but safe; the scramble is manageable.
- **Climb Errigal Early:** It's popular, so ascending before 10 am means summit views without crowds.
- **Discover Glenveagh Slowly:** Don't rush through the park. Walk the easier Doon Level Trail one day, Spinetail the next. The park reveals itself gradually.
- **Talk to Locals:** Donegal walkers are genuinely helpful. They'll give you honest advice about conditions, hidden routes, and excellent places to eat.
