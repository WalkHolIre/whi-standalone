# The Beara Way Hiking Guide: Ireland's Wildest Peninsula Walk

If you're looking for a hiking adventure that captures the raw, untamed spirit of Ireland's southwest, the Beara Way is calling your name. Unlike its more famous cousins—Kerry's Ring of Kerry or the Dingle Way—the Beara Peninsula remains refreshingly wild, beautifully rough around the edges, and considerably less tramped by tourist boots. This is where you'll find yourself hiking through landscapes that feel genuinely remote, where the mountains are higher and steeper, where the Atlantic winds bite with real force, and where you're far more likely to encounter a wild mountain goat than a tour bus.

Our team at Walking Holiday Ireland has walked these trails extensively, and we can tell you with absolute certainty: the Beara Way is something special. It's not the easiest walk in Ireland, but it's absolutely worth every step. This guide will take you through everything you need to know about completing this magnificent 208-kilometre (129-mile) loop—from the famous Dursey Island cable car to the colourful villages of Eyeries, from the remnants of the Allihies copper mines to mountain passes that offer views stretching all the way to Skellig Michael on clear days.

## What Makes the Beara Way Different?

The Beara Peninsula juts out into the Atlantic like a weathered finger, pointing west toward the open ocean. It's a landscape sculpted by millennia of wind, rain, and Atlantic storms—and that's precisely what makes it so magnetic. The Beara Way experiences roughly one-third of the visitors that the Kerry Way receives, which means you're getting authentic Irish wilderness rather than a well-polished tourist experience.

The trail winds through some of the most dramatic mountain scenery in Ireland. You'll walk past the crumbling remnants of 19th-century copper mining operations, through villages where the houses are painted in such vivid colours that they seem to belong to a fairy tale, across mountain passes where the wind can literally knock you sideways, and along coastal sections where you can taste the salt spray on your lips and feel the raw power of the Atlantic beneath your feet.

Your legs will feel challenged—the Beara Way includes more elevation gain than many other Irish long-distance walks, and the terrain is often rough underfoot. But your soul will feel properly alive. That's the trade-off, and it's absolutely worth it.

## The Beara Way Stages: Your Complete Walking Route

The Beara Way can be walked in either direction, though most walkers begin in Kenmare and head counterclockwise. The full loop is approximately 208 kilometres (129 miles) and typically takes 8-10 days to complete. Below, we've broken down the route into daily stages, though you can adjust these distances based on your fitness level and preferences. Remember, with our luggage transfer service, you can carry just a day pack and enjoy your walk at your own pace—no heavy rucksack weighing you down.

### Stage 1: Kenmare to Castletownbere (28km)

Your adventure begins in the charming market town of Kenmare, where our handpicked accommodation partners will have you tucked up warmly the night before you start. The first day is an absolute humdinger, and we're not going to lie to you—it's steep. The trail climbs sharply out of Kenmare, ascending toward Beara Gap, where you'll gain roughly 700 metres of elevation as you head toward Purple Mountain.

This opening stage is deliberately challenging, and there's a reason for it: you're entering genuine mountain country. As you climb higher, the landscape around Kenmare falls away, and suddenly you're walking through moorland strewn with purple heather and wiry grasses. On a clear day—and you should ideally wait for one before tackling this stage—your views extend all the way across the Iveragh Peninsula toward the Macgillycuddy's Reeks.

The descent into Castletownbere is equally dramatic, dropping you steeply down toward this working fishing village that feels genuinely off the beaten track. Castletownbere has a wonderfully weathered character—it's a real place where real people work, not a polished tourist attraction. Our team loves the pubs here; they're the kind of places where locals will strike up a conversation and you'll find yourself hearing proper Irish stories told with warmth and humour.

**Stage distance:** 28km | **Elevation gain:** 1,100m | **Typical time:** 8-9 hours

### Stage 2: Castletownbere to Allihies (14km)

This is your reward day. After the intensity of Stage 1, Day 2 offers magnificent scenery with much more manageable terrain. You'll be walking through coastal sections that reveal why the Beara Peninsula captures hearts so completely. The trail winds through the Caha Mountains, descending gently toward the small village of Allihies.

But here's where it gets truly interesting: Allihies is home to the remains of one of Ireland's most significant copper mining operations. Between 1812 and the early 1900s, thousands of miners extracted copper from these mountains—copper that helped build the railways and infrastructure of the Industrial Revolution. Today, you can still see the old mine buildings, the miners' cottages, and the distinctive red-brown discoloration that the copper left in the surrounding rock and streams.

Our team finds this section particularly moving. There's something about walking through the landscape of your ancestors' labour—feeling the steep slopes where men once worked for pittance, seeing the stone cottages where mining families lived—that really connects you to Irish history. Allihies itself is a genuine gem: a tiny village strung out along a steep hillside, with colourful houses (more on those in a moment), a few welcoming pubs, and a real sense of community.

**Stage distance:** 14km | **Elevation gain:** 400m | **Typical time:** 4-5 hours

### Stage 3: Allihies to Eyeries (13km)

Day 3 is pure magic, and it's the stage where you'll understand why we get so animated about the Beara Way. The trail hugs the southern coast of the peninsula, offering you constantly changing views of the Atlantic, Dunloe Harbour, and the offshore islands. You'll be walking at a gentler pace today, which means you can really absorb the landscape.

Eyeries is absolutely unmissable. This tiny village sits on the edge of Dunloe Harbour, and its houses are painted in colours so vivid and unexpected that visiting Eyeries feels like stepping into an artist's imagination. Deep purples, ocean blues, sunshine yellows, warm oranges—it's as if the residents decided that if they were going to live this far out on a windswept peninsula, they might as well do it in colour.

The story behind Eyeries' painted houses is wonderful. Some credit the influence of artists who moved here seeking inspiration; others point to a local tradition of brightening homes against the grey Irish weather. Whatever the reason, it works. Your camera will get a proper workout here, and your heart will feel lighter just looking at the place.

This is an ideal spot to spend two nights, giving yourself a full day to explore, to sit in a harbour-view café, to wander the tiny village, and to let the pace of the peninsula settle into your bones.

**Stage distance:** 13km | **Elevation gain:** 400m | **Typical time:** 4-5 hours

### Stage 4: Eyeries to Ardagh (15km)

After your rest day in Eyeries, you're ready for another proper mountain day. This stage takes you across some of the Beara's most dramatic terrain, climbing toward Coulagh Bay and then ascending through moorland toward Ardagh. You'll encounter genuine mountain landscape here—sparse vegetation, rocky terrain, and wind that has genuinely significant force behind it.

This is where the Beara Way earns its reputation as Ireland's wildest peninsula walk. The mountains feel genuinely wild, the weather can turn seriously fast, and you need to have your wits about you, especially if visibility drops. But on a good day? The views from the high moorland extend across the entire peninsula and out toward the Skelligs.

Ardagh is a working village—a real Irish place where the post office, the pub, and the shop know everyone's name. It's the kind of spot where you'll stay in a family-run guesthouse, eat dinner cooked by someone's mother, and feel genuinely welcomed.

**Stage distance:** 15km | **Elevation gain:** 700m | **Typical time:** 5-6 hours

### Stage 5: Ardagh to Kenmare via Dursey Island (28km)

This is your final—and most exciting—stage. The day includes the Dursey Island cable car crossing, which is one of Ireland's most memorable transportation experiences. This cable car is tiny, gloriously old-fashioned, and genuinely thrilling. It's been carrying passengers and sheep between the mainland and Dursey Island since 1969, and it feels like stepping back in time.

The cable car journey itself takes about 10 minutes, and you'll spend it suspended over the water with tremendous views of the Dursey Strait. Our walkers consistently tell us that this is a highlight of their entire trip—it's quirky, it's authentically Irish, and it offers a genuine sense of adventure.

Once on Dursey Island, you'll walk the length of this small island, which offers wonderful coastal views and a real sense of being somewhere genuinely remote. The island's population is tiny, and the landscape feels properly wild and windswept. Then it's back on the cable car, and from there you walk the final mountain pass back toward Kenmare, where your journey began.

This final stage includes more elevation gain, but by this point in the walk, your legs are strong, your lungs are efficient, and your mind is properly tuned to the pace of mountain walking.

**Stage distance:** 28km | **Elevation gain:** 1,100m | **Typical time:** 8-9 hours

## Practical Information for Your Beara Way Walk

### Total Distance and Duration

The complete Beara Way loop is approximately 208 kilometres (129 miles). If you walk it at a steady pace with rest days built in, you're looking at 8-10 days of walking. Some energetic walkers complete it in 7 days, others take 12 days to really savour the experience. There's no "right" speed—your own pace is the best pace.

### Best Season to Walk the Beara Way

The Beara Peninsula experiences proper Irish weather, which means it's exposed to Atlantic storms, frequent rain, and wind that deserves respect. That said, every season has its charm:

**Summer (June-August):** This is peak season, and for good reason. The days are long, the weather is more stable (though rain is still frequent), and the accommodation is fully booked. If you're walking in summer, book well in advance. Your chances of decent weather are better, but expect to share the trails with other walkers.

**Spring (April-May) and Autumn (September-October):** These are genuinely our favourite seasons for the Beara Way. The weather is still unpredictable, but it's more dramatic than summer—you get proper mountain weather, stunning light, fewer walkers, and accommodation is easier to secure. If you can walk in May or September, you'll have a magical experience.

**Winter (November-March):** Winter walking on the Beara is genuinely tough and requires proper mountain skills. The days are short, the weather is severe, and some accommodation closes. We don't recommend winter for most walkers, though hardy adventurers who know what they're doing will find the landscape intensely beautiful and profoundly quiet.

### Fitness Level and Experience Required

Let's be honest: the Beara Way is more challenging than some other Irish long-distance walks. It includes more elevation gain than the Kerry Way, steeper descents, and more exposed mountain terrain. You'll need:

- **Basic fitness:** Ability to walk 20-30km per day over multiple consecutive days
- **Hill walking experience:** Comfort walking on steep terrain, rough ground, and exposed mountain paths
- **Weather preparedness:** Mental and physical ability to walk in rain, wind, and potentially poor visibility
- **Navigation skills:** Ability to follow waymarked trails and navigate if conditions make trails difficult to see

That said, our team has supported walkers of all abilities complete the Beara Way. Many walkers in their 60s, 70s, and beyond have finished this trail successfully. The key is training properly beforehand, building your fitness gradually, and being honest with yourself about your capabilities.

### Getting to and From the Beara Way

**By car:** Kenmare is roughly a 4-hour drive from Dublin, 2 hours from Cork City, and 1.5 hours from Dingle. If you're driving, the N71 is the main road through the region, and car parking is available in Kenmare.

**By public transport:** Bus services connect Kenmare to Cork and Tralee, though services can be infrequent. Many of our walkers fly into Cork or Dublin and rent a car for their time in southwest Ireland, which gives you maximum flexibility and allows you to explore beyond your walk.

### Weather and What to Pack

Pack for Irish mountain weather, which means packing for the possibility of everything. Bring:

- Waterproof jacket and trousers (not optional—essential)
- Warm mid-layers (fleece or merino wool)
- Hat and gloves (even in summer, high mountains are cold)
- Good hiking boots (worn in before you arrive)
- Sun protection (it does shine sometimes, and mountains intensify UV exposure)
- Blister treatment (if you're walking 28km+ per day, you need to be ready)

The weather on the Beara can turn from beautiful to brutal in minutes. We've seen walkers arrive on a day with perfect visibility and find themselves in clouds and rain by lunchtime. Pack properly, check forecasts, and be prepared to adjust your plans.
