<h2>The Antrim Coast: Northern Ireland's Coastal Crown Jewel</h2>

<p>The Antrim Coast showcases Northern Ireland's most stunning coastal scenery—dramatic cliffs plunging toward turquoise oceans, charming villages clinging to cliffsides, and walking trails delivering heart-pounding vistas. This region combines natural drama with genuine Irish hospitality, creating unforgettable experiences.</p>

<p>This comprehensive guide explores the Antrim Coast's 10 must-visit seaside towns and villages alongside practical coastal walking information, helping you plan unforgettable adventures along one of Europe's most spectacular coastlines.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Dramatic cliffs and seascapes</strong>—among Europe's most photographed coastal features</li>

<h2>The 10 Crown Jewels of Antrim Coast Towns</h2>

<h3>1. Ballycastle — Authentic Seaside Village</h3>

<p>Ballycastle epitomizes Antrim Coast charm. This working fishing village maintains authentic character despite growing tourism. The harbor bustles with boats, colorful buildings line streets, and locals outnumber visitors.</p>

<p><strong>What to experience:</strong> Harbor walks, beach exploration, traditional pubs serving excellent seafood, local artisan shops, access to dramatic Fair Head coastal path.</p>

<h3>2. Carrick-a-Rede — Rope Bridge Adventure</h3>

<p>Famous for its dramatic rope bridge spanning a 20-meter chasm to tiny island, Carrick-a-Rede combines thrilling experience with spectacular coastal vistas. The walk itself is short (30 minutes to bridge) but emotionally powerful.</p>

<p><strong>Experience includes:</strong> Rope bridge crossing (heart-pounding but safe), dramatic clifftop scenery, island exploration, seabird watching.</p>

<h3>3. Bushmills — Historic Whiskey Village</h3>

<p>Famous for its whiskey distillery, Bushmills combines heritage appeal with coastal proximity. Visitors enjoy whiskey tastings, distillery tours, and easy access to Causeway Coast hiking.</p>

<p><strong>Attractions:</strong> Old Bushmills Distillery tours, traditional village pub culture, coastal path access.</p>

<h3>4. Giant's Causeway — UNESCO World Heritage Site</h3>

<p>The Giant's Causeway features extraordinary basalt columns—geometric wonders created by ancient volcanic activity. The visitor center provides geological interpretation, while coastal paths offer dramatic clifftop walking.</p>

<p><strong>Experience highlights:</strong> Walking among basalt formations, visitor center interpretation, coastal path continuation toward Bushmills.</p>

<h3>5. Portrush — Seaside Resort Town</h3>

<p>A more developed seaside resort offering modern amenities alongside traditional appeal. Portrush features excellent beaches, quality accommodations, and restaurants alongside coastal walk access.</p>

<p><strong>Attractions:</strong> Sandy beaches, water sports, quality dining, direct Causeway Coast Way access.</p>

<h3>6. Portballintrae — Quiet Coastal Village</h3>

<p>Less touristy than neighboring communities, Portballintrae offers authentic coastal village experiences. The horseshoe-shaped beach provides contemplative walking, and the village maintains traditional charm.</p>

<p><strong>Character features:</strong> Quiet beaches, traditional pubs, minimal crowds, coastal path access.</p>

<h3>7. Dunluce Castle — Dramatic Clifftop Ruins</h3>

<p>Perched dramatically on a sea cliff, Dunluce Castle's ruins date to medieval times. The ruins dominate the landscape, and coastal paths integrate historic exploration with scenic walking.</p>

<p><strong>Experience:</strong> Castle exploration, historic interpretation, dramatic cliff vistas, seabird watching.</p>

<h3>8. Glenarm — Glens Gateway Village</h3>

<p>Glenarm village nestles where Glenarm Glen meets the coast. This transition zone between mountain glens and coastal waters creates unique character. The village serves as ideal base for combining glen hiking with coastal exploration.</p>

<p><strong>Attractions:</strong> Glen exploration access, coastal walks, traditional village character, heritage sites.</p>

<h3>9. Carnlough — Charming Harbor Village</h3>

<p>A picturesque village where mountain meets sea. Carnlough's white limestone harbor buildings reflect in calm waters, creating photographer's paradise. The village balances tourism with genuine community life.</p>

<p><strong>Features:</strong> Stunning harbor, quality accommodations, excellent restaurants, coastal path access.</p>

<h3>10. Cushendall — Quiet Beach Community</h3>

<p>The largest Glens of Antrim village, Cushendall maintains genuine character despite size. The long sandy beach invites extended walks, and the village offers excellent services without overdevelopment.</p>

<p><strong>Attractions:</strong> Sandy beaches, glen access (Glenballyemond, Glencorp), quality accommodations, traditional pubs.</p>

<h2>The Causeway Coast Way: Premier Coastal Walking Route</h2>

<p>The <strong>Causeway Coast Way</strong> stretches approximately 33 kilometers from Carrick-a-Rede to Portrush, offering one of Europe's most spectacular coastal walks. The trail combines dramatic cliffs, sandy beaches, and charming villages in a 2-3 day adventure.</p>

<p><strong>Trail Features:</strong> Cliff-edge paths with ocean vistas, seabird watching, historical sites integration, manageable daily distances (10-15 km), excellent accommodations along route.</p>

<h2>Essential Antrim Coast Walking Preparation</h2>

<h3>Coastal Weather Gear</h3>

<p>Atlantic winds are constant. Quality waterproof jacket and pants essential. Misty conditions appear suddenly—reduce visibility. Hat and gloves even in summer. Layers trump bulk.</p>

<h3>Navigation Tools</h3>

<p>Printed maps, compass, smartphone with offline maps downloaded. Coastal paths are well-marked but coastal fog demands careful navigation.</p>

<h3>Hydration Strategy</h3>

<p>Carry 2-3 liters depending on walk length. Water supplies appear regularly in villages. Natural sources exist but carry purification tablets.</p>
