# Wicklow Mountains National Park Walks: A Complete Trail Guide

Wicklow Mountains National Park delivers something rare for walkers based in Dublin—genuine mountain scenery within an hour of the city, yet feeling genuinely remote when you're within its valleys and ridges. This 40,000-hectare park encompasses dramatic mountain terrain, glaciated valleys, ancient forests, and the kind of scenery that justifies why poets and monks have sought inspiration in these mountains for centuries.

Walking in Wicklow Mountains National Park means choosing your own adventure. You can walk gentle lakeside loops accessible to families and beginners, or tackle ridge walks requiring fitness and mountain experience. You can spend a single afternoon on the park's trails, or make it the focus of a week-long walking holiday. The park's proximity to Dublin also makes it perfect for walkers combining city time with mountain walking.

This guide covers the main walking routes within Wicklow Mountains National Park, from Glendalough's iconic lakes to remote ridge traverses and hidden valley walks. ## Understanding the Park Structure

Wicklow Mountains National Park centers on Glendalough, the valley of two lakes that serves as both geographic and spiritual heart of the park. From this core, multiple valleys branch outward, each with distinct character. Glenveagh Valley, Glenmalure, and Glenasmoil each offer unique walking experiences—some accessible as day walks from Glendalough, others better as multi-day ventures incorporating park sections into longer walks.

The park's infrastructure is well-developed. Visitor centers, car parks, and trail markings make navigation straightforward. Yet despite this accessibility, the interior remains genuinely wild—venture beyond main trails and you're in genuine mountain terrain requiring proper skills and equipment.

## Glendalough: The Park's Heart

### Upper Lake and Round Tower Loop

**Distance:** 6 km (circuit)
**Duration:** 1.5-2 hours
**Difficulty:** Easy

This is the most popular Glendalough walk—and for excellent reason. The route circles Upper Lake, passing the monastic round tower, the ancient churches, and the lake itself reflecting mountain cliffs.

What makes this walk special is the combination of archaeological interest and landscape beauty. The monastic settlement dates to the 6th century; walking here means inhabiting landscapes shaped by over 1,400 years of human history. The natural setting—a glaciated valley surrounded by mountain walls—explains why monks chose this location.

The walk is entirely accessible to walkers of varying abilities. The path is well-established and marked. Water crossings are straightforward, and elevation change is minimal. This is perfect for recovering from travel, warm-up walking, or anyone seeking Glendalough's essence without sustained challenge.

Why walk it: Pure Glendalough atmosphere. The sense of place is strong. Morning or evening light creates phenomenal photography conditions. The walk combines nature and history in ways few Irish walks achieve.

### Lower Lake Loop

**Distance:** 4 km (circuit)
**Duration:** 1-1.5 hours
**Difficulty:** Easy

Following the shore of the Lower Lake through ancient oak forest, this walk offers gentle terrain and forest immersion. The lake itself is less dramatic than Upper Lake—no sheer cliffs—but the woodland experience is lovely.

This route suits walkers preferring gentle forest walking to dramatic scenery. It's family-friendly and accessible to anyone. The forest has recently been partially opened up for better views; the walk feels less enclosed while maintaining woodland character.

Why walk it: Peaceful forest walking. The lake reflections are beautiful in calm conditions. Early morning walks avoid crowds. It's an excellent recovery walk on rest days.

### Upper and Lower Lake Combined Walk

**Distance:** 10 km
**Duration:** 2.5-3.5 hours
**Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate

Combining both lake loops creates a comprehensive Glendalough circuit. Walking this full route means experiencing the dramatic contrast—the austere, cliff-backed Upper Lake followed by the forest-enclosed Lower Lake.

Why walk it: Maximum Glendalough immersion. The psychological experience of completing both lakes in a single walk feels substantial. You're not rushing—two lakes properly explored takes a full morning or afternoon.

## Ridge and Mountain Walks

### Spinc Walk

**Distance:** 7 km (return)
**Duration:** 2.5-3 hours
**Difficulty:** Moderate

This walk ascends Spinc ridge, offering expansive views from moderate effort. The route climbs gradually from Glendalough, gaining substantial elevation, to ridge-walking with panoramic views across the Wicklow Mountains and outward toward distant Wicklow coast.

The final ridge section is narrow and exposed—not technical but demanding appropriate attention. In poor visibility, navigation becomes important. In clear conditions, the views justify every step.

Why walk it: This is the walk for getting genuine mountain views without extremely difficult terrain. It's achievable for any fit walker. The sense of achievement climbing to ridge height is substantial. Photography from ridge-top is exceptional.

### Camaderry Mountain

**Distance:** 8-9 km
**Duration:** 3-4 hours
**Difficulty:** Moderate to Challenging

Camaderry (912 meters) is the park's most popular mountain summit. The route climbs from Glendalough, traversing moorland and ridge terrain to mountain top. On clear days, views extend from Dublin mountains to Wicklow coast to distant southern mountains.

The climb is sustained and challenging. Weather on the mountain can be dramatically different from the valley—sudden visibility loss is common. This requires navigation skills and appropriate equipment including waterproof jacket and hat.

Why walk it: Legitimate mountain summit achievement. The views on clear days are worth the effort. The sense of getting above the tourist zones into genuine mountain terrain is strong. This teaches you that Wicklow mountains are real mountains deserving respect.

### Tonelagee Mountain

**Distance:** 9-10 km
**Duration:** 4-5 hours
**Difficulty:** Challenging

At 817 meters, Tonelagee offers less elevation than Camaderry but more challenge. The route ascends steeper terrain, traverses narrow ridges, and delivers phenomenal views. On clear days, the mountain commands a sense of wildness and remoteness.

Why walk it: For walkers wanting genuine mountain adventure. The terrain is more technical than Camaderry. The sense of exploring relatively remote terrain is pronounced. The rewards are exceptional.

## Valley Walks and Forest Routes

### Glenveagh Valley

**Distance:** 6-8 km (depending on route)
**Duration:** 2-3 hours
**Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate

Glenveagh, less developed than Glendalough but equally beautiful, offers quieter walking through glaciated valley surrounded by mountain walls. Forest sections alternate with open moorland. The valley terminates in high moorland, with options to venture further or return via different routes.

Why walk it: Genuine sense of discovery. Fewer walkers than Glendalough. Mountain atmosphere without extreme technical challenge. The valley feel is complete—surrounded by peaks, following streams, immersed in landscape.

### Glenmalure Walk

**Distance:** 5-7 km
**Duration:** 1.5-2.5 hours
**Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate

Glenmalure, a long valley extending inland from Glendalough, offers multiple walking options. You can make brief valley visits or venture deep into the moorland.

Why walk it: Multiple route options suit different abilities. The valley feeling grows the deeper you venture. Rest days in Glenmalure allow extended exploration.

### Glencree Valley

**Distance:** Variable (day walks or multi-day options)
**Duration:** Variable
**Difficulty:** Moderate to Challenging

Glencree offers a distinct Wicklow experience—a long, narrow valley with river walking and moorland approaches. The valley is less developed than Glendalough-centered walks but suits walkers seeking less-touristy terrain.

Why walk it: Genuine remoteness. Limited facilities mean you're truly venturing into mountain terrain. The river valley walk experience is meditative and beautiful.

## Integrated Park Walking Routes

### The Complete Wicklow Mountains Circuit

For dedicated walkers, the park offers a multi-day circuit incorporating multiple valleys and ridges. A typical 3-4 day park-focused itinerary might be:

**Day 1:** Glendalough-based rest day
- Morning: Upper and Lower Lake loops
- Afternoon: Spinc walk for ridge introduction

**Day 2:** Glendalough to Glenveagh
- Walk through Glenveagh valley
- Evening accommodation in valley location or return to Glendalough

**Day 3:** Mountain adventure day
- Camaderry or Tonelagee summit walk
- Return to accommodation

**Day 4:** Final walk and departure
- Morning: Glencree valley exploration or return favorites

This gives comprehensive park experience within a weekend or 4-day visit.

## Seasonal Considerations

### Spring (March-May)

Wildflowers bloom across moorlands. Weather becomes more stable but remains unpredictable. Days lengthen substantially. Valley floors can still be muddy. Waterfalls are dramatic from snowmelt and rainfall.

**Best walks:** Glendalough loops, early-season ridge walks (with caution), valley explorations.

### Summer (June-August)

Warmest, most reliable weather. Days are longest. Trails become busiest. Mountain conditions are most stable. Thunderstorms can develop suddenly in afternoon.

**Best walks:** All routes. High ridges are most accessible. Early morning or late afternoon walks avoid crowds.

### Autumn (September-October)

Weather becomes variable. Colors are spectacular—moorland turns purple, forests turn gold. Trails quieter than summer. Waterfalls increase with autumn rainfall.

**Best walks:** Ridge walks (September), valley walks (October when weather turns), photography-focused walks.

### Winter (November-February)

Weather is unpredictable and often severe. Short daylight hours limit options. Ice and snow make high ridges dangerous without winter experience. Lower valley walks remain accessible.

**Best walks:** Valley circuits, lower lake loops only.

## Practical Information for Park Walking

### Accommodation Options

Glendalough itself offers guesthouses and hotel accommodation directly in the valley, which is highly convenient for walkers. Other villages (Laragh, Annamoe) within 15 km offer additional options. Our walking tours feature handpicked accommodation, ensuring comfort and proprietor understanding of walker needs.

### Supplies and Services

Glendalough visitor center offers information and facilities. Small shops in the village provide basic supplies. For substantial shopping, nearby Laragh offers more options. Pack out what you pack in—this is a conservation area.

### Parking and Access

Ample car parking at Glendalough visitor center and valley locations. The park is easily accessible from Dublin—approximately 45 minutes south via the N11. Public transport connects Dublin to Glendalough, making car-free visits possible.

### Maps and Navigation

The park is well-marked with trail signage. Ordnance Survey maps (1:50,000 or 1:25,000) cover the area comprehensively. Mobile navigation apps work well but shouldn't replace map and compass backup, particularly for less-developed routes.

### Weather Considerations

Mountain weather changes rapidly and dramatically. The saying goes: "If you don't like the weather in Ireland, wait five minutes." This is especially true in Wicklow mountains. Always carry waterproof jacket and hat regardless of forecast. Morning clear skies do not guarantee afternoon clarity.

## The Park's Deeper Appeal

Wicklow Mountains National Park works on multiple levels. You can enjoy gentle, accessible walks experiencing landscape beauty. You can pursue genuine mountain walking with technical challenge. You can combine archaeological interest (Glendalough's 6th-century monasticism) with natural beauty. You can spend afternoon or week immersed in terrain.

What unifies these experiences is the sense of place. Glendalough isn't just scenic—it's spiritually evocative. Walking there means inhabiting landscapes shaped by human contemplation and natural grandeur for over 1,400 years. That accumulated resonance is part of what makes the park special.

## Planning Your Park Walking

Start with the easy walks—Glendalough's Upper and Lower Lake loops. Then progress to Spinc or Camaderry for ridge views. Spend rest days exploring additional valleys. Consider integrating park walks into a longer Wicklow Way journey, using the park as intensely experienced base rather than pass-through.

Most importantly: walk at your own pace. The park rewards extended time. A weekend hurried through multiple walks delivers less than a focused 4-5 days with rest days allowing full immersion.
