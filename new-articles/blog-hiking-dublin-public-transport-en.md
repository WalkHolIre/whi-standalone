# Hiking Near Dublin with Public Transport: Bus, DART and Luas Routes

Dublin's location is genuinely brilliant if you love walking: the city sits within striking distance of some of Ireland's best trails, and you don't need a car to reach them. The Wicklow Mountains rise 40 kilometres south of the city centre; the Irish Sea offers coastal walks within the DART network; Phoenix Park and various river valleys provide excellent walks within the city itself. For walkers without a car, or those preferring not to navigate Irish roads, Dublin's public transport network opens up realistic options.

The trick is knowing which transport options work, understanding current schedules and costs (public transport changes regularly), and matching them with actually excellent walks. A route that requires three buses and takes two hours to reach isn't practical; one taking 45 minutes by direct DART is a game changer.

In this guide, I'm sharing the walking-friendly public transport routes as they exist in 2026, detailed cost information, journey times, and honest assessment of which walks are genuinely accessible by bus and DART. I'll help you understand what works for day hikes versus multi-day walking holidays, and when a car or taxi might actually be better value. By the end, you'll know exactly how to walk Ireland's best trails without needing to rent a vehicle.

## The Dublin Public Transport Network: What You're Working With

Dublin's public transport comprises several systems:

**DART (Dublin Area Rapid Transit):** Electric commuter rail serving Dublin and surrounding counties. Fast, reliable, relatively frequent. Covers both city and coastal routes extending south to Arklow and north to Drogheda. This is often your best option for reaching walking trailheads.

**Luas:** Light rail tram system in Dublin city, with two lines (Red Line and Green Line). Less useful for reaching walking trailheads, more useful for accessing city-centre walks or connecting to other transport.

**Bus Éireann and GoBus:** Long-distance buses connecting Dublin to regional towns. Variable frequency; longer journeys; useful for reaching some trailheads, particularly in County Wicklow.

**Dublin Bus:** City bus network. Most useful for reaching Phoenix Park or accessing DART/Luas stations; less useful for reaching distant trailheads.

**Local Taxis:** Not public transport, but worth considering for short, expensive routes or shared transport groups.

### Costs (2026)

Public transport costs change regularly. Here's the 2026 context:

**DART:**
- Single journeys within Dublin: €1.50–€4.50, depending on distance
- Journeys to Wicklow: €4–€6, depending on endpoint
- Weekly travel card (unlimited travel, greater Dublin area): €30–€40
- Day ticket (great value for multi-leg journeys): approximately €6–€8 for unlimited daily travel

**Luas:**
- Single journeys: €1.80–€4.50
- Similar weekly/day ticket options available

**Dublin Bus:**
- Single journeys: €2–€3.50
- Travel cards available (same ecosystem as DART, so integrated pricing)

**Bus Éireann:**
- Single journeys to County Wicklow: €5–€12, depending on distance
- Generally more expensive than DART for equivalent distance

**Money-Saving Approach:** If doing multiple public transport journeys, a daily travel card covering bus, DART, and Luas (approximately €8–€10) is often better value than paying per journey. If spending several days in Dublin, weekly cards become worthwhile.

## Hiking Near Dublin on DART: Best Options

DART is genuinely excellent for walkers. The network reaches walking-friendly destinations, runs frequently, and is relatively affordable.

### South (Wicklow Direction) DART Trails

**Bray to Greystones Coastal Walk (4 km, 1.5 hours, easy)**

Starting point: Bray DART station (35 minutes from Dublin centre, approximately €4)

One of the easiest, most accessible walks from Dublin. A cliff-top coastal path connects the seaside towns of Bray and Greystones. The path is paved, safe, and offers sea views throughout.

**The Route:** From Bray DART station, walk through town toward the seafront. The cliff-top path is obvious and well-maintained. You'll walk above shingle beaches with views across Dublin Bay. The walk is flat and undemanding—perfect for fitness building or family walks.

**Return:** Complete to Greystones DART station (where the path ends) and DART back to Dublin (approximately €4). Total: about 3.5 hours (including walking and public transport time).

**Best For:** Families, gentle walks, fitness testing, anyone wanting to combine coastal scenery with minimal planning.

**Practical Notes:** The cliff path is safe but exposed in windy weather. Carry water; there are shops in Bray and Greystones but limited facilities on the walk itself. Start early to catch return trains (frequency reduces toward evening).

### Glendalough (Valley of Two Lakes): DART plus Bus

Starting point: Connolly Station or Tara Street DART (city centre), then connecting bus.

Glendalough is genuinely one of Ireland's special places: a deep glacial valley with two pristine lakes, monastic ruins, and excellent walking. Getting there via public transport requires a bit of planning but works well.

**Getting There:**
1. DART to Rathdrum (approximately 1 hour 15 minutes from Dublin centre; €5)
2. Bus Éireann (Route 193) from Rathdrum to Glendalough (approximately 30 minutes; €3)
3. Total journey time: approximately 2 hours; total cost: approximately €8

Return follows the same route in reverse.

**The Best Glendalough Walks:**

**Glendalough Round Tower and Lakes Loop (5 km, 2–2.5 hours, easy):** A circuit around both lakes with views of the round tower and monastic buildings. Easy terrain, mostly lakeside paths, genuinely beautiful. Excellent first experience of Glendalough.

**Spinetail Trail (6 km, 3–4 hours, moderate):** This climbs from valley floor to open moorland, offering panoramic views across the valley. More challenging than the lake circuit but still manageable. Recommended for stronger walkers seeking a bit more challenge.

**Practical Considerations:** Glendalough is beautiful but the journey is lengthy (approximately 2 hours each way in public transport). Most walkers do one substantial walk and call it a day. Attempting multiple walks in one day via public transport gets tiring. However, the destination is genuinely worth the journey.

**Pacing:** If doing this as a day trip, you're looking at: Travel to Glendalough (2 hours), walk (2–4 hours), break for lunch, return travel (2 hours). A full-day itinerary works; rushing doesn't.

### Wicklow Town and Around: South DART Reach

Starting point: Wicklow DART station (1 hour from Dublin; €6)

Wicklow town is a charming seaside/inland hub with several walking options accessible by foot or short bus connections from the station.

**Wicklow Coastal Path:** A short cliff-path walk (3–4 km, easy) departing from Wicklow town. Not dramatic, but pleasant and accessible. Combine with exploring Wicklow's historical sites and restaurants.

**Return Journey:** Same DART line back to Dublin.

### Dublin-Based DART Walks (No Connection Needed)

Several walks start from DART stations within Dublin itself:

**Howth Cliff Walk (6 km, 2–3 hours, easy-moderate):** From Howth DART station (30 minutes north of Dublin city centre, €3), a cliff-top coastal walk with sea views, sandy beaches, and proper hiking feel despite being within the Dublin metro area. One of Dublin's best walks.

**Dalkey to Bray Coastal Path:** Various starting points on DART stations in the Dublin south-side suburbs, offering cliff-top coastal walking (combining multiple short transport journeys, or doing sections accessible by foot from different stations).

## Hiking near Dublin by Bus: Secondary Options

Bus works less well than DART for hiking access, but certain routes make sense:

**Bus Éireann Route 200 Series (Dublin to Wicklow):** Serves Bray, Greystones, and Wicklow destinations. Slower than DART (1.5–2 hours to reach main trailheads), more expensive than DART to equivalent points. Less practical unless you're connecting to specific routes DART doesn't serve.

**GoBus:** Some routes serve rural Wicklow villages. Useful if targeting specific secondary trailheads, but generally less convenient than DART. Journey times are longer; frequency is lower.

### When Bus Makes Sense

- **Accommodation near a specific bus stop:** If you're staying somewhere with good bus access but poor DART access, the bus becomes your practical choice.
- **Specific trailheads not on DART:** Some excellent walks require bus transport to reach. In these cases, the journey is worth it.
- **Group travel:** Buses serving smaller groups sometimes offer better value than individual DART tickets.

## Dublin City Walks: Starting from City Centre

If you're staying in central Dublin and want to walk without transport:

**Phoenix Park (8 km loop, 2–3 hours, easy):** Europe's largest enclosed park, with tree-lined avenues, open grasslands, and surprising wildness given its city location. Walk the park perimeter or various internal loops. DART or Luas to Heuston, then short walk to the park (or walk directly from city centre—it's accessible by foot).

**Grand Canal Walk (variable, easy):** A towpath walk following the Grand Canal west from Dublin city centre. Can be walked for any distance you prefer. Genuinely urban but pleasant. Starts from city centre (no transport needed).

**Dodder Valley Walks:** Following the Dodder River through south Dublin suburbs. Accessible by walking from various city locations or taking DART/bus to starting points. Urban nature walking.

These are more "urban walking" than "mountain hiking," but they offer genuine activity without needing to travel.

## Practical Tips for Dublin-Based Hiking via Public Transport

### Planning Day Trips

**Single-Day Structure:**
- Early morning transport (avoid peak commute times if possible)
- Substantial walk (3–5 hours depending on your walk choice)
- Lunch somewhere on or near the trail
- Return transport before evening (DART frequency reduces after 7 pm, some routes stop around 11:30 pm)

**Timeline Example (Wicklow):**
- Depart Dublin 8 am (DART to Rathdrum or Bray)
- Walk 9 am–1 pm (4 hours)
- Lunch 1–2 pm
- Return transport 2 pm
- Dublin by 3:30 pm

**Realistic Limitation:** Via public transport, day walks are typically single trails per day. Attempting two separate walks in one day via public transport becomes exhausting due to journey times.

### Weather Considerations

**Check forecasts before departing:** Committing to public transport means you're committed to the journey time regardless. If weather is genuinely dangerous, don't go. But if it's wet but safe, proceed—Irish rain is often manageable with proper gear.

**Have a backup walk plan:** If heading out on a specific trail that's exposed or difficult, have a secondary, easier trail in mind (in case conditions deteriorate or energy is lower than expected). Both should be accessible from your transport connection point. Check our **[weather safety guidelines](https://walkingholidayireland.com)** for more detailed information on assessing conditions.

### What to Carry

**Essentials:**
- Full water bottles (refill at transport stations before departing)
- Lunch/snacks (transport stations have shops, but limited options once on trail)
- Phone charged (for emergency use; signal is variable at altitude)
- Waterproof pack (Irish weather)
- Map (don't rely only on phone GPS)
- Basic first aid
- Extra layers (temperature changes on longer walks)

**Not Essential but Useful:**
- Travel card or transport pass (saves fumbling with exact change)
- Book or podcast for return journey (trains are calm places to rest after walking)

### Return Journey Planning

**Important:** DART frequency reduces significantly in evening hours. Last southbound trains from Dublin depart around 11:30 pm, but frequency during your walking window might be limited.

**Practical approach:**
- Check DART schedules for your destination before departing
- Plan to return by mid-to-late afternoon to ensure good transport frequency
- Avoid getting stranded with a 2-hour wait for the final train

**Timing Reality:** Many Dublin-based public transport walks involve a decent travel time both directions. A 5-hour walk plus 4 hours of public transport makes for a full, tiring day. Build this into expectations—it's still worthwhile, but it's not a casual afternoon activity.

## Multi-Day Walking Holidays: Public Transport Limitations

Here's the honest truth: multi-day walking holidays are less practical via public transport than day hikes. Multi-day walks typically move between accommodation in different villages. Getting yourself, luggage, and supplies to new accommodation each day via public transport becomes complicated and tiring.

**Practical Alternatives:**

**Dublin-Based Multi-Day Walks:** Do several days of walks based from Dublin accommodation, using public transport day trips to different trailheads. You return to the same accommodation each night.

**Combination Approach:** Use public transport for initial journey to a rural base (e.g., Bray, Wicklow, or Glendalough), then stay in one location and do day walks from there, using local buses or taxis for trailhead connections.

**Accommodation Near Transport Hubs:** Choose accommodation near DART or major bus stations, making transport planning simpler.
