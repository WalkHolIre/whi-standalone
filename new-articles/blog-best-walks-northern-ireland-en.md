# Best Walks in Northern Ireland: A Complete Guide

Northern Ireland's landscape is the kind that makes you understand why people write poetry about wild places. The mountains are dramatic, the coastline is spectacular, the forests are genuinely wild, and there's an openness to the landscape — space, sky, and stone arranged in combinations that feel slightly mythical. Walking here, you feel less like a tourist and more like an explorer discovering something secretly magnificent.

Northern Ireland often plays second fiddle to the Republic in walking tourism, but this represents a genuine gap in most walkers' experience. The walks here are exceptional. The infrastructure is excellent. The landscape diversity — dramatic mountains, accessible forest parks, stunning coastlines, pilgrimage routes — rivals anywhere in Ireland. And crucially, outside peak season, you often walk in genuine solitude on trails that would be crowded across the border.

Whether you're seeking serious mountain challenge, gentle woodland walks, or spectacular coastal routes, Northern Ireland delivers world-class walking experiences. The key is understanding which routes match your fitness and preferences, respecting the terrain, and allowing time to absorb landscape that rewards attention.

## Understanding Northern Ireland's Geography and Access

Northern Ireland covers roughly 14,000 square kilometers in the northeastern corner of the island. The landscape includes the Mourne Mountains in the south, the Glens of Antrim in the east, and extensive moorland and forestry across the north. The coastline includes both dramatic cliffs and gentler sandy beaches.

Access is straightforward. Belfast (Northern Ireland's capital) is connected by bus and train to Dublin and other major Irish cities. Most major walking destinations are reachable within 1-2 hours of Belfast. The Mourne Mountains are closer to Newry and the southern border. The Causeway Coast is in the far north near Bushmills and Portrush. Glenariff Forest Park is inland from the coast near Ballymoney.

**Transport options:** Ulsterbus operates throughout Northern Ireland with reasonable frequency. Car hire is available in Belfast. Many walkers combine Northern Ireland walks with southern Ireland routes, making multi-region walking tours realistic.

## The Mourne Mountains

The Mournes are Northern Ireland's most famous mountain range — a cluster of granite peaks offering dramatic walking and genuinely wild landscape. The range includes 15 peaks over 600 meters, with Slieve Donard (850m) being the highest mountain in Northern Ireland.

### Mourne Wall Walk

**Distance:** 35km complete circuit / typical sections 10-15km
**Ascent:** Variable by section
**Difficulty:** Moderate
**Best Section:** Hare's Gap to Slieve Donard (10km, 400m ascent)
**Terrain:** Ridge path, mountain track, occasional scrambling
**Best Season:** May-September

The Mourne Wall is one of Northern Ireland's most distinctive features — a 22-mile stone wall built in the 1920s as a watershed boundary, connecting 15 of the Mourne Mountains. Walking the wall gives you authentic mountain experience while the wall itself provides a navigational aid and a tangible artifact to follow.

Rather than walking the complete circuit, most walkers choose specific sections. The Hare's Gap to Slieve Donard section is the most popular and genuinely rewarding. The walk climbs steeply from Hare's Gap through moorland to Slieve Donard's summit. From the top, you're rewarded with views stretching to the Isle of Man, across Northern Ireland, and toward distant Scottish hills on the clearest days.

**What makes this special:** The physical act of walking a constructed feature adds an unusual dimension. The wall is substantial stone construction, built entirely without mortar, somehow still standing after a century. Walking alongside it, you're connected to human effort and intention across decades.

The Mourne landscape is genuinely wild moorland — heather, bog, and stone without tree cover. This openness means weather exposure but also spectacular light and genuinely unrestricted views.

**Seasonal variation:** Summer offers reliable daylight and warmest temperatures. Spring and autumn offer excellent conditions with fewer crowds. Winter is challenging due to short days and potential ice on higher sections.

**Getting there:** The Hare's Gap car park is accessible from Newcastle, a coastal town with accommodation and facilities. From Belfast, it's approximately 1.5 hours drive south.

### Slieve Donard (Direct Ascent)

**Distance:** 9km round trip
**Ascent:** 850m
**Duration:** 3.5-4 hours
**Difficulty:** Difficult
**Terrain:** Forest path, steep moorland, final rocky section
**Best Time:** May-September, clear days only

This is the direct ascent route from Donard Park near Newcastle. The walk climbs steeply through forest initially, then emerges onto open moorland for the final push to the summit. The terrain is well-walked and clearly marked, but the ascent is relentless.

The summit experience is genuinely worth the effort. On clear days, you're standing at Northern Ireland's highest point with 360-degree views. The contrast between the forested lower sections and the open, windswept summit is dramatic.

**Practical considerations:** This walk is steep and not suitable for beginners. Good hiking boots and comfortable fitness are essential. The final section is rocky and occasionally involves minor scrambling. Weather dramatically affects this walk — in cloud cover, the summit is exposed and disorienting. Only attempt this walk on clear days with excellent visibility.

**What to bring:** 2 liters water minimum, snacks, waterproof jacket and trousers, windproof layer (it's frequently windy at the summit), map or GPS app, and sun protection.

## The Causeway Coast

The Causeway Coast, which includes the famous Giant's Causeway, offers spectacular coastal walking combined with iconic geological features. The Antrim Coast walk extends through this region, and several shorter walks allow engagement with the coastline.

### Causeway Coast Path (Bushmills to Ballycastle section)

**Distance:** 20km / can be split into 10km sections
**Ascent:** 400m cumulative
**Difficulty:** Moderate
**Terrain:** Coastal path, cliff-top walking, beach sections
**Best Time:** May-September, but walkable year-round
**Getting there:** Ulsterbus operates from Belfast to Bushmills (approx. 1.5 hours)

This section of the Causeway Coast walk passes through some of Northern Ireland's most dramatic landscape. Starting from Bushmills (famous for its whiskey distillery), the path climbs cliffs, follows cliff-top routes with ocean views, and occasionally descends to beach level. The Giant's Causeway is the major landmark, with its distinctive hexagonal basalt columns, but the surrounding coastline is equally impressive.

The walk includes the Carrick-a-Rede rope bridge (if you choose the slightly inland section accessing it), which adds adventure — a suspension bridge spanning 20 meters above sea to a small island. The bridge sways in the wind and offers unforgettable views.

**Why walkers love this:** The combination of geological wonder, coastal beauty, historical sites (Dunluce Castle ruins), and accessible challenge makes this one of Northern Ireland's finest walks. The infrastructure is excellent, with cafes and facilities at regular intervals.

## Glenariff Forest Park

Glenariff, in County Londonderry, is Northern Ireland's most visited forest park, and for good reason. The managed woodland offers multiple trails of varying difficulty, passing waterfalls, streams, and through landscapes that feel wild despite careful management.

### Main Waterfall Trail

**Distance:** 2-3km loop
**Ascent:** Minimal, approximately 100m
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Terrain:** Forest path, stepping stones, some steps
**Best Time:** Year-round, spectacular in autumn

This is the signature Glenariff walk — a short loop that passes multiple waterfalls and creates a sense of genuine forest immersion. The path is well-maintained with helpful steps in steeper sections. The terrain is firm underfoot and non-technical, making this appropriate for all fitness levels.

The waterfall loop is genuinely beautiful in any season. Spring brings rushing water and wildflower color. Summer offers full forest coverage and cool refuge from potential heat. Autumn is extraordinary — the deciduous trees turn gold and orange, the waterfalls flow strongly, and the light quality transforms the landscape.

### Binnion Trail (Full Park Loop)

**Distance:** 14km
**Ascent:** 400m cumulative
**Difficulty:** Moderate
**Terrain:** Forest path, stream valleys, some exposed moorland sections

For walkers wanting more substantial Glenariff experience, the Binnion Trail offers a full-park loop combining forest walking, stream-side sections, and ascending to open moorland above the park. The views from the higher sections look across the Glens of Antrim and toward the distant coast.

**Getting there:** Glenariff is approximately 1.5 hours from Belfast. Car access is easiest, though bus services exist. The visitor center offers information, facilities, and can guide trail selection.

### Why Glenariff Matters

Glenariff represents the best of managed walking infrastructure — facilities, signage, and maintenance that make walking accessible and enjoyable. But it also preserves genuine wilderness qualities. The forest is real; the streams run wild; the sense of being within landscape rather than moving through a theme park is maintained.

It's an excellent choice for walkers wanting forest immersion without the full mountain challenge, or as a rest day between more demanding walking.

## The Glens of Antrim

The Antrim Glens are a series of valleys cutting toward the coast. These inland routes offer completely different character from coastal paths — woodland walking, stream valleys, pastoral farmland, and genuine solitude.

### Glenarm Walk

**Distance:** 8-10km
**Ascent:** 300m
**Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate
**Terrain:** Valley path, forest sections, some stream crossing

This walk follows Glenarm Glen, the southernmost of the major Antrim Glens, following the river valley between forested hillsides. The path passes small waterfalls, ancient trees, and through a landscape that feels unchanged for centuries.

### Why the Glens Appeal

Walking the Antrim Glens offers something distinct from the Causeway Coast or Mourne Mountains experience. Here, you're walking through intimate landscape rather than exposed heights or dramatic coastline. The walking is gentler, the pace becomes more contemplative, and you encounter fewer other walkers.

Combined with coastal walks, the Glens create a comprehensive Northern Ireland experience — showing the region's full landscape range.

## Seasonal Walking in Northern Ireland

**Spring (March-May):** Temperatures warming, daylight extending rapidly. April offers genuine spring conditions — milder weather, wildflower color, the sense of the landscape awakening. May is excellent for walking, with long daylight hours.

**Summer (June-August):** Warmest weather, longest daylight (sunset after 10:30pm in June), and most reliable conditions. Also the busiest season, particularly July-August. Early summer morning starts offer solitude on popular trails.

**Autumn (September-November):** Often ideal for walking. September maintains summer conditions while crowds diminish. October offers that particular light quality and autumn colors. November becomes wetter and can be grey, though walking is still possible.

**Winter (December-February):** Short daylight (around 7 hours in late December), cold temperatures, and unpredictable weather. Challenging but possible for experienced walkers. The quiet landscape has its own appeal, and occasional clear, crisp days are genuinely magical.

## What to Bring

For all Northern Ireland walking:
- Proper hiking boots with good grip and ankle support
- 2-3 liters water capacity
- Light snacks (nuts, energy bars, fruit)
- Waterproof jacket and, for mountain walks, waterproof trousers
- Layers (fleece or wool mid-layer for mountain walks)
- Sun protection (sunscreen, hat, sunglasses)

For mountain walks (Mourne Mountains):
- Map of the specific area
- GPS app or device
- Windproof outer layer
- Potentially hiking poles
- Headtorch in case of later-than-expected returns

For forest and easy walks:
- Comfortable hiking shoes or boots
- Insect repellent (midges can be present in summer)
- Camera for capturing woodland beauty
