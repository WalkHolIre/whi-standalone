# Glendalough Walks Guide: 5 Essential Trails in Ireland's Most Magical Valley

## A Valley Where Mountains Whisper Stories of Saints and Seekers

There's a particular kind of magic that settles in Glendalough when the morning mist rises from the lakes and the ancient stone round tower emerges like something from a Celtic legend. After years of guiding walkers through Glendalough walks and Ireland's most enchanting valleys, we've learned that Glendalough isn't just a destination—it's a conversation with history, with nature, with yourself.

Nestled deep in the Wicklow Mountains, about 40 kilometres south of Dublin, Glendalough means "Valley of Two Lakes" in Irish. For nearly 1,500 years, this remote valley has been calling people—first the monks seeking solitude, then pilgrims drawn by faith, and now walkers like you, seeking something equally profound: the restorative rhythm of footsteps on Irish soil.

We've crafted this guide to share the walks we love most, the ones that have made our walkers' hearts sing. Each trail offers something distinct, from gentle contemplative strolls around the monastic site to challenging mountain ridges that reward your effort with views that stretch to the sea.

---

## Planning Your Glendalough Walks from Dublin

The journey from Dublin to Glendalough is part of the experience. Most walkers drive (about 90 minutes via the N11 and R755), though some prefer the slower journey by bus, allowing yourself to ease into the walking mindset before arriving.

**Driving directions:** From Dublin, take the N11 south towards Arklow, then turn west onto the R755 at Rathnew. Follow the scenic road as it climbs into the Wicklow Mountains. You'll know you're close when the mountains embrace the road on both sides.

**Parking:** The main valley car park (free) can get busy during summer weekends and peak tourist season. We always recommend arriving early—say, before 9am—to secure a good spot and have the trails relatively peaceful. An alternative Upper Car Park provides access to some trails and is often quieter. There's also a smaller Village Car Park near the monastic site.

**Seasonal timing matters here.** Spring (April-May) brings wildflowers and lighter crowds. Summer (June-August) offers the longest daylight and warmest weather, though the trails are busier. Autumn (September-October) is our favourite—crisp mornings, golden light, and the valley feeling like it belongs to you alone. Winter can be beautiful but trails are muddy and daylight disappears early (sunset around 4pm).

## Before You Walk: The Monastic Site

Before heading out on the trails, spend an hour at Glendalough's early Christian monastic settlement. It's the soul of the valley. The round tower (30 metres tall, built around 1000 AD) still stands defiant. The small stone cathedral, the reconstructed hut of St Kevin, and the weathered Celtic crosses tell a story of faith, resilience, and the search for meaning that still resonates today.

The monastic site is free to explore year-round, though visitor facilities (café, shop) operate during daylight hours. Many walkers find that understanding this spiritual history deepens their connection to the landscape—these monks walked these same paths, sought shelter from these same mountains, found peace in this same sacred valley.

## The Five Essential Glendalough Walks

### Green Road Walk

**Distance:** 7 km (4.3 miles)
**Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate
**Estimated Time:** 2.5-3 hours
**Best For:** Contemplative walkers, photographers, those wanting steady effort without technical challenge

The Green Road is a walker's meditation. It's an old mountain drovers' route, the kind of path that shepherds and pilgrims have trodden for centuries. Your feet follow the same earth that ancient feet once pressed.

This walk begins at the main car park and climbs gradually alongside a mountain stream, passing stone walls that seem to grow from the landscape itself. The path is mostly gravel and packed earth, occasionally steep but never technical. As you climb, the valley behind you opens up in increasingly glorious views—the twin lakes shrinking to silver mirrors, the stone buildings of the monastic site becoming toy-like with distance.

The highlight comes as you reach the open mountain moorland after about 90 minutes of steady walking. Here, on a clear day, you'll see across the Wicklow Mountains towards the distant shape of Galtymore. The return journey is a gentle descent, allowing your mind to settle into the rhythmic peace that only hills can bring.

**Why walkers love it:** The Green Road feels remote and timeless despite being the most accessible of Glendalough's main routes. It's the perfect introduction to mountain walking—challenging enough to feel rewarding, manageable enough that you'll have energy for the views. Many walkers tell us they return to the Green Road season after season, finding something new each time.

**Seasonal notes:** Spring is magical here when mountain flowers bloom along the path. Winter requires good boots for muddy sections.

---

### Miners' Path & Village Walk

**Distance:** 4 km (2.5 miles)
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Estimated Time:** 1.5-2 hours
**Best For:** Families, leisurely walkers, those with limited time, history enthusiasts

If you have just a couple of hours or want to introduce younger walkers to the valley, the Miners' Path is pure joy. It's a relatively flat walk through the lower valley, but it's rich with history and sensory delights.

The walk traces an old mining route (lead and zinc were extracted here in the 19th century) and passes through woodland filled with the sound of running water. You'll cross streams on stepping stones, walk past the ruins of miners' cottages, and feel how humans have lived and worked in this valley for centuries.

The path winds past the Lower Lake, then climbs gently to the Miners' Village area—a collection of restored stone dwellings that give real shape to historical life here. On warm days, walkers sit on the banks of the lake and watch the light dance on water. In all seasons, there's something contemplative about this route.

The return journey retraces your steps, giving you a chance to notice what you missed on the way out—a bird call, the particular shade of lichen on stone, the way water sounds different as the light changes.

**Why walkers love it:** This walk proves that you don't need dramatic height gain for a meaningful experience. The sensory richness—forest smells, water sounds, the texture of old stone—makes this walk memorable. It's also the most accessible walk in Glendalough, suitable for most fitness levels.

**With children:** This is your best bet. It's short enough for young legs, interesting enough to keep attention, and safe with good surfaces most of the way.

### Spinc and Glenealo Valley Walk

**Distance:** 8.5 km (5.3 miles)
**Difficulty:** Moderate to Challenging
**Estimated Time:** 3.5-4 hours
**Best For:** Experienced walkers, those comfortable with height and exposure, adventurous spirits

Now we move into more serious terrain. Spinc (pronounced "Spink") is the distinctive rocky ridge that defines Glendalough's eastern side. This walk is the valley's classic challenge—a real mountain walk with proper altitude gain, but entirely worth every step.

The route climbs steeply from the valley floor, gaining about 300 metres in the first 45 minutes. Your legs will remind you they're working, but the encouragement comes in the form of ever-expanding views. As you climb, the valley becomes a map beneath you—you can trace the entire shape of Glendalough, see both lakes joined by a slender thread of river, watch the mountains roll away in every direction.

The reward is reaching Spinc's ridge itself—a dramatic rocky crest with that particular kind of exposure that makes your heart beat faster in a way that feels deeply alive. Here, the views are magnificent: south towards the distant Wicklow coastline, north across to Djouce Mountain, west into the heart of the Wicklow range. The air up here feels different—cleaner, somehow, or perhaps it's just that heights sharpen your senses.

The descent into the Glenealo Valley is dramatic and scenic. You drop into a hanging valley (a geological feature created by ice age glaciers), surrounded by high peaks. The valley floor has a wild, remote feeling—this is where walkers often pause, feeling the particular peace that comes from reaching wild places through your own effort.

**Why walkers love it:** This walk delivers the "proper mountain walk" experience. It challenges your legs and your nerve but rewards both with views that stay with you for years. Many walkers tell us that reaching Spinc's ridge is when they truly feel they've arrived in Glendalough.

**Safety notes:** This walk involves some scrambling and exposed sections. It's not technical rock climbing, but sure footedness and a head for heights help. In wet weather, the rocky sections become slippery—use walking poles for balance. Avoid this walk in poor visibility when navigation becomes difficult.

### Upper Lake Walk

**Distance:** 5 km (3.1 miles)
**Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate
**Estimated Time:** 1.5-2.5 hours
**Best For:** All walkers, nature lovers, those wanting a scenic circuit, photographers

The Upper Lake is where Glendalough reveals its most intimate face. Higher and smaller than the Lower Lake, surrounded by steep woodland, it feels tucked away, as though you've discovered a secret.

This walk begins at the upper car park and makes a gentle circuit around the lake itself. The path undulates through mixed woodland (oak, birch, and ash trees) that age-old locals say whisper stories if you listen carefully enough. You'll cross a small wooden bridge, walk past the ruins of St Kevin's Church, and visit the site of the Reefert Church, where early monks gathered.

What makes this walk special is how intimate it feels. You're never more than metres from water or woodland. The pace is leisurely enough for genuine conversation, yet the setting is beautiful enough that walkers often fall naturally into companionable silence, each lost in their own thoughts.

On misty mornings—and there are plenty in Glendalough—the Upper Lake becomes almost mystical. The water disappears into fog, the trees emerge and recede like memories. It's the kind of conditions that make you understand why monks sought spiritual enlightenment in this place.

**Why walkers love it:** This walk feels like a secret even though it's straightforward. The combination of woodland, water, history, and solitude creates something special. Many walkers describe it as meditative—the kind of walk where you finish feeling calmer than when you started.

**Bird watching:** The Upper Lake is excellent for birdwatching. You might see grey heron, dippers, and various woodland species. Bring binoculars and patience.

### Poulanass Waterfall Walk

**Distance:** 6.5 km (4 miles)
**Difficulty:** Moderate
**Estimated Time:** 2.5-3.5 hours
**Best For:** Waterfall enthusiasts, walkers wanting scenic variety, those wanting genuine views without extreme exertion

The walk to Poulanass Waterfall combines everything we love about Glendalough: water, woodland, views, and a satisfying goal. The waterfall itself isn't dramatically high (about 25 metres), but there's something about the sound and sight of water falling that speaks to something ancient in us.

The route begins at the lower car park and follows the river valley southeastward, initially through mixed woodland where you're surrounded by the sound of flowing water. The path climbs gradually, passing through areas of bracken and heather as you leave the deepest woodland behind.

The section approaching the waterfall is the most dramatic—the path becomes narrower and steeper, with the river increasingly evident as you climb. The sound of falling water grows louder, building anticipation. When you round a final bend and the waterfall appears—water pouring over dark rock, mist rising from the plunge pool below—it feels like a reward earned through effort.

Many walkers sit for an extended break at the waterfall, eating lunch while watching the water endlessly fall. There's something hypnotic about it, something that encourages a kind of peaceful mind-emptiness.

The return journey retraces your steps, which is fine because you'll see the woodland and landscape with fresh eyes, noticing what you missed on the ascent. By the time you return to the car park, you'll feel properly walked—tired in a good way, refreshed in a deeper way.

**Why walkers love it:** This walk feels like a proper hill walk without being overwhelming. The waterfall provides a clear objective, the views improve as you climb, and the entire experience feels curated for walker satisfaction. Plus, the sound of water and the approach to the waterfall makes the effort feel worth it.

**Photography:** The waterfall is particularly photogenic in autumn when the surrounding trees are golden, and in spring when water flow is highest from snowmelt.

## Seasonal Wisdom: When to Walk Glendalough

**Spring (April-May):** Mountain flowers bloom along the higher paths. Days are lengthening, the weather increasingly unpredictable. Come prepared for sun, wind, and the occasional shower.

**Summer (June-August):** Warmest, longest days. The valley is busiest. Start walks early to have trails to yourself. The Upper Lake and Miners' Path are less crowded than Spinc. Midges can be bothersome on still evenings.

**Autumn (September-October):** Our favourite season. Crisp mornings, golden light, trails peaceful. The green starts turning to bronze and russet. Weather is stable enough for planning, but layers are essential.

**Winter (November-March):** Stunning but serious. Days are short (sunset around 4pm). Trails are muddy and potentially icy. The upper trails (Spinc, Green Road) require good boots and possibly gaiters. Visibility can be poor. Only suitable for experienced hill walkers. The Miners' Path and Upper Lake remain enjoyable options.

**Our recommendation:** Visit in May or September-October for the sweet spot of accessibility, weather, and crowds. But honestly, Glendalough is beautiful in every season—you just need to respect what each season demands.

## Practical Essentials for Your Glendalough Walking

**What to wear and bring:**
- Waterproof jacket and trousers (essential—Irish weather changes fast)
- Sturdy walking boots with good ankle support (trails can be rocky and muddy)
- Layers you can add and remove (temperatures drop as you gain height)
- Hat, gloves, and a light scarf (even in summer, mountain tops are cool)
- Waterproof bag for electronics
- Walking poles (especially for Spinc, which has steep descent)
- Sun cream and sunglasses (yes, really—UV is stronger at height)
- Snacks and water (at least 1.5-2 litres)

**Fitness and experience:**
- The Miners' Path requires minimal fitness
- The Green Road and Upper Lake suit most walkers with reasonable fitness
- Poulanass Waterfall requires regular walking experience
- Spinc requires mountain walking experience and fitness
- If you're new to hill walking, start with Miners' Path or Upper Lake and build up

**Safety and navigation:**
- Use a detailed map (get one at the visitor centre or download the Glendalough Walking Map app)
- Trails are generally well-marked, but poor visibility can disorient
- Tell someone your planned route and expected return time
- Start early to ensure you're off the mountain before dark
- If weather deteriorates, turn back—the mountains will be there next time
- Mobile signal is unreliable in parts of the valley

## The Gift of Walking Glendalough

Walking in Glendalough offers something increasingly rare: time and space to think your own thoughts, feel your own feelings, without distraction or expectation. Whether you choose the gentle Miners' Path or the challenging Spinc ridge, whether you visit on a crystal-clear day or in soft mist, you're participating in something that humans have done in this valley for fifteen centuries.

You're adding your footsteps to the path of monks seeking spiritual truth, of pilgrims seeking healing, of countless ordinary people seeking something they couldn't name but recognized once they found it.

The mountains don't judge your pace or your ability. They don't care if you make it to the highest point or turn back halfway. They simply offer themselves—their beauty, their challenge, their indifference—and somehow that's exactly what we need.

**Pack your boots, bring your sense of adventure, and head to Glendalough.** The valley has been waiting for your footsteps.
