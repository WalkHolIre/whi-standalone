# Walking Festivals in Ireland 2026: Your Complete Calendar

There's something magical about walking festivals in Ireland—the collective energy of strangers becoming friends, sharing trails under the same sky, all united by the simple joy of putting one foot in front of the other. Walking festivals in Ireland 2026 are packed with extraordinary events, each offering unique experiences across our island's most beautiful landscapes.

I've guided walkers through many of these festivals. There's an atmosphere unlike everyday walking: the camaraderie, the carefully planned routes designed to showcase regional character, the local hospitality flowing freely. This is where walking festivals in Ireland become more than exercise—it becomes connection.

## Kenmare Walking Festival — April 18-20, 2026

**Location:** Kenmare, County Kerry
**What to Expect:** One of Ireland's most prestigious walking festivals, Kenmare draws walkers from across Europe. The festival showcases the stunning Kerry peninsula with routes ranging from 5km to 22km daily.

**Routes & Highlights:**
- Caha Mountains circuit (moderate, 16km)—dramatic ridge views overlooking Kenmare Bay
- Beara Peninsula day walk (challenging, 18km)—wild mountain and coastal terrain
- Gentle woodland routes (easy, 6-8km)—perfect for building confidence or casual walkers
- Kenmare Bay coastal sections (moderate, 10km)—sea views with manageable terrain

**Accommodation:** Kenmare fills quickly. Book early. We recommend small family-run guesthouses rather than hotel chains—they offer genuine Irish hospitality. Properties with views toward Kenmare Bay are worth seeking.

**What Makes It Special:** Kenmare itself is charming—colorful shop fronts, waterfront location, welcoming atmosphere. The festival integrates local guides and community knowledge. You're not just walking; you're discovering Kerry through locals' eyes.

**Registration:** Visit [www.kenmarewalkingfestival.ie](http://www.kenmarewalkingfestival.ie) for registration and detailed route descriptions. Booking opens December for this popular festival.

**Pro Tip:** Arrive a day early. The festival's momentum builds—you'll want to experience the evening gatherings, local music, and festival-day pre-walk breakfasts that fuel great days on the trail.

## Ballyhoura Walking Festival — May 9-11, 2026

**Location:** Ballyhoura, County Limerick/Cork
**What to Expect:** Ballyhoura showcases the hidden gem of the midlands—rolling hills, forest routes, and a surprisingly varied landscape that few walkers discover outside festival time.

**Routes & Highlights:**
- Galtee Mountains approach (moderate, 14km)—approaching Ireland's most westerly mountain range
- Ballyhoura Mountain forest trails (easy-moderate, 9km)—well-designed routes through ancient woodland
- River valley walking (easy, 6km)—gentle riverside terrain with rural character
- Hill fort exploration (moderate, 12km)—combining hiking with archaeological interest

**Accommodation:** Ballyhoura's villages are quiet and charming. Guesthouses offer personable hospitality. The region's slower pace is part of the appeal.

**What Makes It Special:** This festival feels less commercial than larger events. The routes showcase genuine rural Ireland—quiet villages, working farms, forests where you feel far from tourism. The local guides genuinely love their landscape and share that passion.

**Registration:** Contact Ballyhoura Development (www.ballyhoura.com) for festival details. Typically releases full schedule in February.

**Pro Tip:** Combine the festival with exploring South Limerick's food culture. The region is quietly becoming known for excellent local food. Festival evenings often feature local producers.

## Doolin Walking Festival — May 16-18, 2026

**Location:** Doolin, County Clare
**What to Expect:** Doolin is synonymous with Irish music and traditional culture. The walking festival weaves cultural experience with outdoor activity.

**Routes & Highlights:**
- Cliffs of Moher coastal paths (moderate, 8km)—iconic Irish scenery, manageable terrain
- Burren plateau exploration (moderate, 12km)—unique limestone landscape found nowhere else in Europe
- River walks (easy, 5km)—gentle riverside trails through cultural landscape
- Loop Head coastal route (moderate, 10km)—Atlantic cliffs and coastal drama

**Accommodation:** Doolin's small village character is charming but accommodation is limited. Book well ahead. Surrounding villages (Liscannor, Lahinch) offer alternatives.

**What Makes It Special:** Doolin blends festival walking with living culture. Evening traditional music sessions are genuine—locals playing for locals, with visitors welcome. It's not performed "for tourists"; it's culture happening naturally. Walking days provide the energetic balance to evening music appreciation.

**Registration:** Check [www.doolintouring.ie](http://www.doolintouring.ie) for festival details and cultural programming.

**Pro Tip:** Stay multiple nights beyond the festival. The Burren's landscape rewards slow exploration, and Doolin's music culture deepens if you experience more than festival-focused evenings.

## Glens of Antrim Walking Festival — June 6-8, 2026

**Location:** Glenariff Forest Park area, County Antrim
**What to Expect:** Northern Ireland's most dramatic scenery—deep glacial valleys, waterfalls, forest, and mountain landscape. The festival showcases why the Glens are considered Ireland's most spectacular natural landscape.

**Routes & Highlights:**
- Glenariff Waterfall Trail (moderate, 8km)—the heart-stirring sound and sight of water falling through ancient forest
- Glenballyeamon circuit (challenging, 16km)—ridge walking with views across glens to the sea
- Towey Forest walks (easy-moderate, 7km)—forest immersion with waterfall discovery
- Coastal extension routes (moderate, 10km)—connecting glens to sea cliffs at Fairhead

**Accommodation:** Glenariff Village offers simple guesthouses and farmhouse stays. The region retains traditional Irish hospitality while being well-developed for walking tourism.

**What Makes It Special:** The Glens feel wild and remote despite accessibility. The landscape is genuinely spectacular—deep valleys carved by glaciers, waterfalls dropping through forest, and a sense of scale that takes your breath away. Festival guides have deep local knowledge, sharing landscape stories most visitors never learn.

**Registration:** Visit [www.visitglenariff.com](http://www.visitglenariff.com) for festival scheduling and detailed route information.

**Pro Tip:** The Glens are best experienced over multiple days. Consider extending your visit beyond festival dates to fully explore the landscape. Waterfall routes are most impressive in spring/early summer when water volume peaks.

## Connemara Walking Festival — September 5-7, 2026

**Location:** Connemara, County Galway
**What to Expect:** The Twelve Bens, boglandi, and Atlantic coastal character define Connemara. This festival showcases Ireland's wild west at its most dramatic.

**Routes & Highlights:**
- Benlettery and Twelve Bens approaches (moderate-challenging, 15km)—mountain drama with sweeping bog views
- Coastal section from Roundstone (easy-moderate, 8km)—sea and mountain combination
- Sky Road experience (easy, 5km)—short but spectacularly scenic with panoramic views
- Quiet valley routes (easy, 6km)—discovering Connemara's gentler character

**Accommodation:** Connemara's accommodation ranges from simple village guesthouses to farmhouse stays with genuine character. Book ahead—the region fills quickly in festival season.

**What Makes It Special:** Connemara feels genuinely wild. The mountains aren't high but feel immense. The bog landscape is distinctive—found nowhere else quite like this. The Atlantic presence is constant. This is where Ireland's west reveals its authentic character.

**Registration:** Details available through [www.connemarawalkingfestival.ie](http://www.connemarawalkingfestival.ie) typically in June/July.

**Pro Tip:** September is optimal—summer crowds have cleared, weather is still reasonable, and autumn light is extraordinary. The bog's colors shift into autumn hues, creating spectacular photography and viewing.

## Wicklow Mountains Walking Festival — September 12-14, 2026

**Location:** Wicklow Mountains National Park area
**What to Expect:** Ireland's ancient mountains showcase stunning scenery, forest, waterfall, and mountain terrain all within 90 minutes of Dublin.

**Routes & Highlights:**
- Wicklow Way sections (moderate, 12-16km)—iconic long-distance route broken into festival-appropriate distances
- Glenmalure valley loop (moderate, 10km)—Ireland's longest glen with dramatic surrounding peaks
- Waterfall routes (easy-moderate, 7km)—Glenmalure waterfall and smaller cascades
- Roundwood to Glendalough (moderate, 11km)—forest to ancient monastic site transition

**Accommodation:** Wicklow town offers various options. Glendalough's village guesthouses provide authentic mountain village experience.

**What Makes It Special:** Wicklow is accessible yet feels genuinely wild. The mountains have dramatic character despite moderate heights. The forest and waterfall combinations create sensory richness. Festival guides share natural history—glacial formation, forest ecology, and landscape evolution.

**Registration:** Information available through [www.wicklowmountainsfestival.ie](http://www.wicklowmountainsfestival.ie).

**Pro Tip:** This festival is less internationally known than Kenmare, meaning smaller crowds and more intimate experience. The quality of routes rivals famous festivals while remaining more accessible and relaxed.

## Kerry Way Walking Festival — October 10-12, 2026

**Location:** Killarney area, County Kerry
**What to Expect:** The famous Kerry Way long-distance route translated into festival format. Stunning mountain and lakes scenery in Ireland's most spectacular region.

**Routes & Highlights:**
- Torc Mountain and Upper Lake (moderate, 11km)—intimate mountain experience with stunning water views
- Moll's Gap to Glencar (challenging, 16km)—dramatic mountain scenery and ridge walking
- Lauragh Peninsula coastal section (moderate, 10km)—combining mountain views with coastal access
- Kenmare Bay approach (easy-moderate, 7km)—waterside walking with mountain backdrop

**Accommodation:** Killarney is Ireland's premier mountain resort town. Accommodation ranges from simple guesthouses to hotels. Our preference: smaller properties offering personalized service.

**What Makes It Special:** Kerry is iconic for reason—the mountains are genuinely spectacular, the lakes are profound, and the landscape feels legendary. Festival brings together serious walkers and those discovering mountains for the first time.

**Registration:** Details through [www.kerryway.ie](http://www.kerryway.ie).

**Pro Tip:** October brings autumn colors and clearer air (less summer haze). Fewer crowds than summer but weather remains reasonable. The light is golden—photographers will be delighted.

**What to Expect:** One of Ireland's most beautiful peninsulas showcased through curated routes. Mountain, coastal, and cultural landscapes combined.

**Routes & Highlights:**
- Coumeenoole Beach to Slea Head (moderate, 8km)—iconic coastal scenery with dramatic cliffs
- Brandon Mountain approach (challenging, 15km)—Ireland's spiritual mountain with sweeping views

**What Makes It Special:** Dingle feels like journeying to Europe's edge. The peninsula's character is distinct—influenced by Atlantic weather, traditional Irish culture, and a landscape that feels both welcoming and wild.

## Practicals: Making the Most of Walking Festivals Ireland 2026

### What to Expect Day-to-Day

Most festivals follow a similar pattern:

**Evening before festival start:** Meet-and-greet gathering, route briefing, casual social time. This is where you meet fellow walkers and guides. Despite being strangers, shared walking interest creates immediate connection.

**Festival mornings:** Early breakfast (substantial Irish breakfasts fuel the day), route confirmation, guides gathering groups. Walks typically depart 8-9am.

**On the trail:** Groups of 8-15 walkers with a knowledgeable guide. Pace is moderate—this isn't racing but exploring together. Rest stops are built in, with guides sharing landscape knowledge.

**Festival evenings:** Dinner (usually arranged by festival, sometimes at local restaurants), social gathering, local entertainment (music, storytelling), and early nights (post-walk fatigue encourages this).

### Packing for Festival Walking

**Day pack essentials:**
- Water (minimum 1.5 liters)
- Weather-appropriate layers (Irish weather changes rapidly)
- Sun protection (cream, hat, sunglasses)
- Lunch and snacks
- First aid basics
- Phone for emergencies
- Camera (if inclined)

**Accommodation bag:**
- Extra clothes
- Toiletries
- Medications
- Comfortable evening shoes
- Any specialized items

We handle luggage transfer between accommodations, so you carry only daily necessities.

### Fitness Preparation

Walking festivals aren't casual; they're proper hiking. Preparation matters:

**Weeks before:** Establish regular walking routine. Walk weekly at a moderate pace for 1-2 hours minimum.

**Two weeks before:** Attempt longer walks—10-12km on varied terrain. Build foot conditioning; long walks reveal foot issues before festival.

**Final week:** Maintain fitness but reduce intensity. Rest is as important as training.

This isn't about being an expert mountaineer; it's about reasonable fitness for the distance involved.

### Registration Tips

- **Book early:** Popular festivals (Kenmare, Dingle, Kerry Way) fill by March/April for summer events
- **Choose appropriate difficulty:** Be honest about fitness. Festivals offer multiple levels; it's better to do an easier route comfortably than a harder route struggling
- **Ask about customization:** Some festivals can customize routes if you have specific needs
- **Confirm accommodation:** Walking festival accommodation books faster than regular tourist season

### Building Community Through Walking

Here's what many first-time festival walkers discover: the friendships formed matter as much as the trails walked. Meeting someone on a trail at 10am, walking alongside until 4pm, sharing lunch stops and route discoveries—these create real connection.

Many walkers return to the same festivals annually partly for routes but equally for reuniting with friends made in previous years. Walking festivals create communities.
