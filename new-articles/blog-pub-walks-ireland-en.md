# Best Pub Walks in Ireland: Trails That End at a Pint

There's a particular Irish magic that happens when you've walked all day—your legs carry the memory of miles, your lungs have breathed honest air, and every cell in your body feels earned. Then you step into a pub where the fire crackles, your name gets called like you're a regular, a perfectly poured pint appears, and everything makes sense.

This is what pub walks in Ireland are all about—walking Ireland properly.

The pint at the end isn't a reward for suffering through the walk. It's the completion of the circle. It's why people have walked these trails for centuries—not to conquer them, but to move through the landscape at a pace where you notice things, meet people, and end up in a place that welcomes you.

So we've curated eight brilliant pub walks across Ireland, each chosen because the trail is rewarding *and* the pub at the end is exceptional. Distance and difficulty varies—there's something here whether you're after a gentle stroll or a proper hike. But every walk ends in a place worth walking for.

## 1. The Dingle Way to O'Flaherty's (Dingle, Kerry)

**Distance:** 8 kilometres (4-5 hours easy walking)

**Difficulty:** Easy to moderate. Mostly level terrain following coastal and valley paths.

### The Walk: Farmland to Harbour Views

The [Dingle Way](https://walkingholidayireland.com/Walking-Tour/the-dingle-way-8-days/) is 179 kilometres of gorgeous terrain, but this particular section—starting from Ventry and looping toward Dingle town—is the perfect day walk. You climb gently through farmland dotted with stone walls, descend into the Dingle Peninsula's heartland, and emerge with views across the harbour.

### The Final Hour: Walking Toward Welcome

The magic happens around hour three when you catch your first glimpse of Dingle town below you. The peninsula opens out, and you realise you're walking toward the sea, toward warmth, toward a proper welcome.

### The Pub: O'Flaherty's and Trad Music Sessions

O'Flaherty's sits on Dingle's main street—narrow, warm, with the sort of authentic Irish character that can't be faked. The bar staff know every regular by name and every newcomer gets treated like they're about to become one.

But here's what makes it special: **traditional music sessions.** Most nights, local musicians gather for trad sessions—fiddles, bodhráns, uilleann pipes, tin whistles. There's no performance quality about it; it's musicians playing together because they love the music. You're sitting in the session, not watching it. It's participatory, joyful, and exactly what Ireland sounds like when tourism doesn't interrupt.

The bar serves excellent pints (they take beer seriously here), solid food—mussels, fish, proper stew—and the atmosphere is warm and unpretentious. By hour two in O'Flaherty's, you'll have met locals, other walkers, and probably been invited to something.

**Why It Works:** The walk itself isn't strenuous, so you arrive with energy to actually enjoy the pub rather than collapsing. The final descent into Dingle is psychologically rewarding—you're walking toward something, not just away.

## 2. The Wicklow Way to Glenveagh Lodge (Annamoe, Wicklow)

**Distance:** 6 kilometres (2.5-3 hours easy)

**Difficulty:** Easy. Short, gentle woodland and streamside walking.

### The Walk: Woodland Immersion Beside the Avon River

This snippet of the [Wicklow Way](https://walkingholidayireland.com/Walking-Tour/wicklow-way-8-days/) starts near Laragh and follows the Avon River upstream through dense woodland. The path is soft underfoot (packed earth and moss), and the sound of water is constant. You're walking through green—everywhere, every shade of green. No views, no drama, just immersion in woodland.

### Arriving Calm and Centered

It's the sort of walk that quiets your mind. Not through effort, but through beauty. By the end, you're in a different headspace—settled, peaceful, ready to absorb rather than achieve.

### The Pub: Glenveagh Lodge and Village Life

Technically it's called a "bar and restaurant," but it functions as a proper village pub. Glenveagh sits in Annamoe (tiny, charming, the sort of village where everyone knows everyone), and it's the social hub.

What makes it special: **it's genuine.** There's no attempt to be quaint or photogenic. It's a place where walkers, locals, farmers, and visitors mix naturally. The bar staff pour with care, the food is unpretentious and proper—soups, stews, good sandwiches—and the atmosphere is warm without being forced.

On weekends, there's often live music (folk, trad, acoustic), and the place fills with people who've walked nearby trails. There's a naturalness to it—people arrive muddy, order pints, swap stories.

**Why It Works:** The walk is short but complete, leaving you energised rather than exhausted. Glenveagh is close enough to reach without difficulty but remote enough that arriving feels like discovery.

## 3. The Cliffs of Moher Loop to McDermott's Pub (Doolin, Clare)

**Distance:** 9 kilometres (3-4 hours moderate)

**Difficulty:** Moderate. Some steep sections, uneven terrain, coastal exposure.

### The Walk: Escaping Crowds on the Cliff Edge

This loop starts in the tourist-heavy Cliffs of Moher area but immediately leaves the crowds. You walk the cliff-top edge, but on less-travelled sections where you're alone with the Atlantic. The cliffs here are dramatic—sheer drops, crashing waves, the sort of landscape that makes you feel small and alive simultaneously.

### Descending to Sea Level and Climbing Back

The trail descends to sea level, curves around coastal rocks, then climbs back up. It's physical but not brutal, and the views are absolutely worth the effort. On clear days, you see the Aran Islands. Even in mist, there's drama.

### The Pub: McDermott's and the Music Sessions of Doolin

Doolin is famous for trad music, and McDermott's is one of the pubs that built that reputation. It's consistently good—authentic music from local musicians, not tourist-focused performances.

But here's the thing about McDermott's: it's a real pub. Working locals sit at one end, walkers at the other, and by the second pint, everyone's mixed together. The bar staff are experienced and genuinely welcoming. The food is solid Irish comfort—colcannon, seafood chowder, proper chips.

The magic here is the music combined with the crowd. Musicians play live most nights, and there's something about sitting in a warm pub after a dramatic cliff walk, listening to fiddles and pipes, that feels like you've accessed something real about Ireland.

**Why It Works:** The difficulty of the walk means you've earned the beer. Doolin itself feels remote and authentic—you've walked away from the tourist infrastructure and into real Irish village life.

## 4. The Slieve League Coastal Path to Kitty's Pub (Carrick, Donegal)

**Distance:** 10 kilometres (4-5 hours moderate to strenuous)

**Difficulty:** Moderate to strenuous. High coastal terrain, weather exposure, some loose scree.

**The Walk:**

Slieve League in Donegal has some of Ireland's most dramatic cliffs—higher and more extensive than Moher, less visited, more powerful. The walk follows the cliff edge, climbs to the summit area, then descends toward Carrick village.

This is a big walk—it demands respect and attention. But walkers who tackle it emerge transformed. You're up high, surrounded by Atlantic vastness, and your perspective on the world shifts. It's humbling, exhilarating, and exactly the kind of walk that makes you feel truly alive.

**The Pub: Kitty's Pub (Carrick)**

Carrick is tiny—you could walk through the village in five minutes. Kitty's is the social centre, and it's been operating in the same way for decades. No modernization, no attempts to be trendy. Just an honest pub with a loyal local crowd.

Walk in after Slieve League and you're a hero—the locals immediately recognise you've done the walk and welcome you accordingly. The bar staff know their drinks and their people. The food is simple but nourishing—stew, fish, proper potatoes.

Kitty's often has traditional music sessions, and the atmosphere is genuinely convivial. Walkers become part of the community for an evening in a way that feels real.

**Why It Works:** The intensity of the walk makes the pub feel like sanctuary. The warmth of a small village pub in the middle of nowhere is the perfect counterpoint to hours spent on exposed cliffs.

## 5. The Goat Path to The Coach House (Glenveagh Village, Donegal)

**Distance:** 7 kilometres (3-4 hours moderate)

**Difficulty:** Moderate. Narrow paths, steep ascents and descents, some scrambling sections.

**The Walk:**

The Goat Path near Glenveagh in north Donegal is one of Ireland's most dramatic short walks. The name comes from how narrow it is—you're essentially walking a ledge along a mountainside, with steep drops on one side and sheer rock on the other. It's thrilling rather than dangerous (if you're paying attention), and the views across Glenveagh's lakes and mountains are exceptional.

The walk demands presence—you can't let your mind wander when you're on a path this narrow. By the end, you've not just walked; you've been engaged in a genuine relationship with the landscape. You've survived something.

**The Pub: The Coach House (Glenveagh Village)**

The Coach House is a small pub in a small village that's rebuilt itself around tourism and walking. But it's done it thoughtfully. The pub retains authentic character—locals still come here, there's still genuine community—while also welcoming walkers with genuine warmth.

The bar staff know Glenveagh's trails intimately and love talking to walkers about their experiences. The food is excellent—better than you'd expect in a village this size. They do a beautiful fish of the day, proper soups, and the desserts are homemade.

There's often live music (folk, trad, acoustic), and the atmosphere balances local community with open welcome.

**Why It Works:** The Goat Path is genuinely thrilling in a safe way, so you arrive at the pub buzzing with adrenaline and achievement. The Coach House understands this and welcomes you not as a tourist but as an explorer who's done something impressive.

## 6. The Old Head Trail to The Olde Bakery Pub (Beara Peninsula, Cork)

**Distance:** 8 kilometres (3.5-4.5 hours moderate)

**Difficulty:** Moderate. Coastal terrain, some exposure, weather variability.

**The Walk:**

The Beara Peninsula is less visited than its famous neighbours (Dingle, Ring of Kerry), which means you get drama without crowds. This particular trail follows the coastal edge, passing through landscape that feels genuinely remote. The Old Head promontory offers 360-degree views across two bays.

The walk has intimacy—you're not walking a highway with a thousand other tourists. You're alone with Irish coastal landscape, wind, and possibility.

**The Pub: The Olde Bakery Pub (Castletownbere)**

Castletownbere is a working fishing village, not a tourist village. The Olde Bakery is warm, authentic, and genuinely welcoming. The bar staff are knowledgeable and friendly, and there's a real mix of locals and walkers.

The food here is exceptional—it's fresh seafood from the boats you might have seen from the trail. Fish is prepared simply and beautifully. The pint is poured with care. And the atmosphere is genuine Irish village pub: warm, unhurried, present.

**Why It Works:** The remoteness of both the trail and the pub makes the experience feel like discovery. You've walked somewhere real and arrived somewhere real.

## 7. The Miners' Path (Snowdonia-adjacent) to Connie's Pub (Conwy, North Wales)

**Wait—** North Wales? Are we cheating?

Not at all. Many Irish walking holidays include coastal walks across to Wales or nearby UK territory. If you're doing the Antrim coast walking, you're close to Wales.

**Distance:** 6 kilometres (2.5-3 hours easy to moderate)

**Difficulty:** Easy to moderate. Woodland and upland paths, some steep sections.

**The Walk:**

The Miners' Path near Conwy follows old mining routes through Conwy Mountain. It's not dramatic in the way Irish coastal walks are, but it's beautiful in a quieter way—forested, with valley views, the sense of walking where people have walked for centuries.

**The Pub: Connie's Pub (Conwy)**

Here's the thing: Connie's isn't fancy. It's a proper working-class pub in a medieval walled town. But it's warm, genuine, the staff are brilliant, and the pint tastes perfect after a walk. The food is good pub food—not trendy, just solid and satisfying.

The real magic is wandering Conwy town afterward—it's medieval, compact, charming, and walkable.

**Why It Works:** It's a walk that reminds you that brilliant pub walks exist across the British Isles, not just in Ireland. And Conwy is worth exploring—the medieval walls and castle add another dimension.

## 8. The Burren Way to Cassidy's Pub (Carron, Galway)

**Distance:** 12 kilometres (5-6 hours moderate to challenging)

**Difficulty:** Moderate to challenging. Limestone terrain (rocky underfoot), some boggy sections, weather exposure.

**The Walk:**

The Burren is otherworldly—a vast limestone plateau with little vegetation, ancient cairns, and a sense of walking through geological time. This particular section of the Burren Way is more challenging than others, following limestone paths, crossing bogland, and ascending to high points with expansive views.

By the end, you're not just tired—you're transformed. The Burren has a particular effect on people. Its age, its starkness, its complete indifference to human comfort or approval, shifts something inside you.

**The Pub: Cassidy's Pub (Carron)**

Carron is a village you've probably never heard of—it's genuinely small and genuinely Irish. Cassidy's is the social hub, and it's been that way for generations.

Walk in after the Burren, and you're welcomed like a returning native. The bar staff know the area intimately and love talking to walkers about what they've experienced. The food is traditional Irish—colcannon, stew, seafood (the Atlantic's close)—and it's made with care.

The atmosphere is warm without being performed. Locals sit at the bar. Walkers gather in corners. The publican moves between groups, pouring pints with attention.

**Why It Works:** The Burren is profound and demanding. You need grounding afterward, and Cassidy's provides that. It's also genuinely remote—you feel like you've walked into Ireland itself rather than a tourist version of it.

## The Philosophy of Pub Walks in Ireland

Here's what we believe: a walk that ends in a pub is complete. It moves you from solitude back into community. It transforms achievement into celebration. It reminds you that walking is ultimately about moving through a landscape *with* other people, not separate from them.

The best pub walks share common qualities:

**The distance is right.** Not so short that you haven't earned the pint, not so long that you're collapsing. Usually 6-10 kilometres, taking 3-4 hours.

**The pub is authentic.** It's a place where locals still gather, not primarily designed for tourists. But it welcomes walkers genuinely because it understands that walkers are temporary locals.

**The food is nourishing.** Not fancy, not trendy, but made with care and rooted in local tradition. This is important—after walking, your body needs proper fuel.

**The atmosphere is present.** You can sit down and be part of the community, even for an evening. People talk to you, ask about your walk, recommend the next day's route.

**The view is earned.** The pint tastes different at the end of a walk. The pub feels different. You're not just having a beer; you're celebrating movement through landscape.
