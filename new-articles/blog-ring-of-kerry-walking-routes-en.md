# Ring of Kerry Walking Routes: Not Just a Drive

The Ring of Kerry is famous. Incredibly, famously, almost overwhelmingly famous. If you've ever been to Ireland, you've probably heard about the Ring of Kerry—that stunning 179-kilometer coastal drive in County Kerry that's appeared in a thousand travel blogs and postcards. Tour buses pack the roads daily, especially in summer. Visitors queue at the same viewpoints, stop at the same gift shops, photograph the same sunsets.

But here's what most people don't know: the Ring of Kerry isn't actually meant to be driven. Well, it is now, but it wasn't originally. The Ring of Kerry route follows old droving roads, ancient pilgrim paths, and traditional routes that people walked for centuries before cars were invented. Hidden within the famous drive are genuine walking routes that offer the same spectacular scenery but without the tour buses and crowds.

This guide is for walkers, not drivers. We'll explore how to walk sections of the Ring of Kerry, how to combine them with bus services, and how to experience this spectacular landscape at your own pace—which is exactly how it was meant to be experienced.

## What Is the Ring of Kerry, Really?

The Ring of Kerry is a 179-kilometer loop road in southwestern Ireland that circles the Iveragh Peninsula, a long, dramatic spit of land that juts out into the Atlantic. The route connects several small towns—Killarney, Kenmare, Sneem, Cahersiveen, Waterville, and Glenbeigh—and passes through some of the most visually spectacular landscape in Ireland.

The drive takes roughly 4-5 hours without stops, or a full day with stops at viewpoints and villages. Most visitors do it as a day trip, sitting in tour buses or driving rental cars, collecting Instagram photos and moving on.

For walkers, though, the Ring of Kerry is something entirely different. The landscape is the same—the mountains, the coast, the valleys—but experienced from ground level, at human pace, with time to absorb the sensory detail. And far beneath the modern road, the ancient paths that created the Ring of Kerry are still there, walkable by anyone willing to look.

## The Kerry Way: The Multiday Option

If you want to walk the entire Ring of Kerry (or most of it), the Kerry Way is your answer. This is a 215-kilometer long-distance trail that traces much of the Ring of Kerry loop, though it's not exactly identical—the walking route sometimes diverges from the driving route to explore valleys, climbing mountains, or following more interesting historical paths.

The Kerry Way typically takes 9-10 days to complete and is graded as moderate—there are climbs, but nothing extreme, and the variety of terrain makes every day different. The route passes through or very near every major town on the Ring, allowing for comfortable accommodation and good food.

## Day Walks and Partial Routes

### Section 1: Killarney to Kenmare

**Distance:** 35 km (Kerry Way) | **Duration:** 1-2 days walking

The eastern side of the Ring, from Killarney to Kenmare, follows the Kerry Way inland through valleys and lower mountains rather than hugging the coast. This section is less dramatic than western sections but offers beautiful mountain scenery and passes through charming villages.

**Walking Options:**

**Full Section (35 km):** Properly a 2-day walk with an overnight stop. Starts in Killarney, climbs to Molls Gap (a scenic mountain pass), descends through the Ardeagh Valley, and drops toward Kenmare. This is genuine mountain walking through quiet landscape.

**Day Walk from Killarney:** Starting from Killarney, walk toward Molls Gap (about 12 km, 3-4 hours). The views from the pass are spectacular—you can see the Macgillycuddy's Reeks mountains to the north and the Kenmare valley to the south. Many walkers do this as a day trip, then return to Killarney or drive to Kenmare.

**Day Walk from Kenmare:** Starting from Kenmare, reverse the route and hike toward Molls Gap. The climbing is gentle and the valley scenery is lovely. 6-8 hours gives a substantial hike without committing to an overnight.

**Difficulty:** Moderate. There's genuine climbing, particularly on the Killarney side of Molls Gap, but the paths are well-established and the terrain is on mountain tracks rather than technical climbing.

**Highlights:** Molls Gap viewpoint, wild mountain landscape, isolation and quiet, traditional villages.

### Section 2: Kenmare Waterfront Walks

**Distance:** 5-15 km depending on route | **Duration:** 2-5 hours

Kenmare sits at the head of a long, scenic inlet (Kenmare River) where inland mountains meet coastal landscape. Several walking options branch from Kenmare, allowing you to explore different facets of this region.

**The Waterfront Loop:** A gentle 5-kilometer loop around Kenmare town itself and along the waterfront. Perfect for arriving tired or wanting to stretch legs without serious effort. 1.5-2 hours of easy walking.

**Kenmare to Sneem (Coastal):** A longer day walk of about 13 kilometers along roads and paths that follow the coastline south toward Sneem. The walking is moderate (some climbs), the scenery is coastal and lush, and you finish in Sneem village with access to good food and accommodation. 4-5 hours walking.

**Caha Mountains Loop:** Various circular walks start from Kenmare and climb into the Caha Mountains (also called the Slieve Mish range). These are proper mountain walks offering dramatic views over the Iveragh Peninsula. 4-8 hours depending on route and fitness. More challenging terrain.

**Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate depending on route chosen.

**Highlights:** Coastal scenery, mountain views, charming villages, opportunity to meet locals in pubs and restaurants.

### Section 3: Sneem

**Distance:** Multiple options, 5-12 km | **Duration:** 2-4 hours

Sneem is an utterly charming village on the southern coast of the Iveragh Peninsula. It's small enough to feel genuine but established enough to have good accommodation and restaurants. It's also a perfect base for exploring this section of the Ring.

**Sneem Circular Walks:** Several loops branch from Sneem village:
- **River Walk (5 km, 1.5 hours):** Easy walk along the Sneem River valley
- **Coastal Path toward Parknasilla (8 km, 2.5-3 hours):** More challenging walk along cliffs and beaches toward the Parknasilla Hotel (a landmark visible from kilometers away)
- **Mountain Loop toward Coomakista (12 km, 4 hours):** Climbing walk with views over Kenmare River and Dingle Peninsula

**Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate depending on route.

**Highlights:** Genuine Irish village experience, small-scale coastal scenery, mountain views, excellent local restaurants and pubs.

**Why Stay Here:** Sneem feels like stepping into a different Ireland than tourists normally see. The village has character and authenticity. The people are genuinely warm. The food (particularly seafood) is excellent. If you're doing the Ring of Kerry at a relaxed pace, Sneem is worth at least a night, ideally two.

### Section 4: Cahersiveen and Skellig Ring

**Distance:** Multiple options, 8-25 km | **Duration:** 2-7 hours

Cahersiveen is the largest town on the western Ring of Kerry and serves as a gateway to some of the most dramatic coastal scenery in Ireland—including the Skellig Islands (visible offshore) and the narrow Skellig Ring peninsula.

**Cahersiveen Village Walk:** A modest 3-4 km walk around town and along the waterfront. The town itself has historical interest (the birthplace of Irish patriot Daniel Foley) and good cafés and pubs. An hour or so of gentle walking.

**Skellig Ring Coastal Path:** This is genuinely special. The Skellig Ring is a small peninsula that juts out into the Atlantic between Cahersiveen and Waterville. Walking the coastal path around its edges is dramatic, wild, and less touristy than much of the Ring of Kerry.

The Skellig Ring walk is roughly 12-15 kilometers depending on exact route, and takes 4-5 hours. The terrain is rocky, the scenery is Atlantic-facing and dramatic, and the isolated quality feels worlds away from the tour buses on the main Ring road.

The walk passes Ballinskelligs Beach (where you can see the Skellig Islands) and Cahersiveen Ring Fort (an ancient stone structure). The loop can be done from Cahersiveen or from Waterville.

**Difficulty:** Moderate to Challenging. The terrain is rocky and exposed. Fitness required.

**Highlights:** Dramatic Atlantic coastline, complete isolation, ancient monuments, sense of being at the edge of the world.

**Practical Note:** The Skellig Ring is where most of the film "The Last Jedi" was shot. If you're a Star Wars fan, walking here offers a different perspective on those famous locations.

### Section 5: Waterville and Lough Currane

**Distance:** 5-15 km | **Duration:** 2-4 hours

Waterville is a small seaside village with a long beach and views toward the Skellig Islands. The landscape here transitions from mountain to coast, and several walking options allow exploration.

**Waterville Beach Walk:** An easy 2-3 km walk along Waterville's long beach. Flat, accessible, good for tide-watching and spotting seabirds. 1 hour easy walking.

**Lough Currane Loop:** Ireland's western coastline has numerous coastal lakes (loughs). Lough Currane, near Waterville, can be circled via a 10-12 km walking route that combines lake views, mountain scenery, and access to small villages. 3-4 hours moderate walking.

**Degree Loop Mountain Walk:** A more challenging mountain walk climbing toward higher ground with panoramic views over the Iveragh Peninsula and toward Dingle Peninsula. 12-15 km, 4-5 hours, moderate to challenging terrain.

**Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate depending on route.

**Highlights:** Long sandy beaches, mountain-to-coast scenery, dramatic light on Atlantic waters, sense of isolation.

### Section 6: Glenbeigh and the Dingle Peninsula Views

**Distance:** 8-18 km | **Duration:** 3-5 hours

Glenbeigh is the northeastern gateway to the Ring of Kerry, where the Iveragh Peninsula begins to give way toward Dingle Peninsula. Several walks explore this transition landscape.

**Glenbeigh Beach and Village Walk:** A gentle 4-5 km walk around Glenbeigh village and along its beach. Easy and accessible. 1.5-2 hours.

**Seefin Mountain Loop:** A more substantial walk (12-15 km, 4-5 hours) climbing Seefin Mountain for views over both the Iveragh and Dingle Peninsulas. The ascent is steady and the views are comprehensive. Moderate terrain, good fitness required.

**Rossbeigh Beach Long Walk:** A 10 km walk along one of Ireland's longest beaches, with mountain backdrop. Flat but exposed to wind. 3 hours easy walking.

**Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate depending on route.

**Highlights:** Transition landscape between two peninsulas, long sandy beaches, mountain views, sense of arrival or departure.

## Combining Walking with Bus Travel

One of the joys of the Ring of Kerry is that Bus Éireann runs regular bus services around the loop, allowing walkers to do sections at their own pace and use buses to connect between sections or to return to starting points.

**Example Itinerary (5 days, combining walking and bus):**

**Day 1:** Arrive Killarney. Walk Molls Gap section or explore Killarney valley. (6-8 hours)

**Day 2:** Bus from Killarney to Kenmare. Walk Kenmare Waterfront Loop or shorter Kenmare to Sneem coastal section. (4-5 hours walking)

**Day 3:** Bus from Kenmare to Cahersiveen. Walk Skellig Ring. (5 hours walking)

**Day 4:** Bus to Waterville or Glenbeigh. Walk chosen local section. (3-5 hours walking)

**Day 5:** Return to Killarney via bus or private transport.

This approach allows you to walk 20-30 kilometers over 5 days, experiencing different sections of the Ring, without the demands of multi-day backpacking.

## Seasonal Considerations

**May-June:** Excellent season. Days are very long (it's light until 10 PM in mid-June at this latitude), wildflowers bloom, weather is warming. The Ring of Kerry is beautiful but not yet desperately crowded.

**July-August:** Peak season. Warmest weather, fully long daylight, but busiest with tour buses. Book accommodation well in advance. Walking early in the day helps avoid crowds.

**September:** Beautiful month. Weather often excellent, light has golden quality, crowds have diminished. Frequently voted the best time to visit.

**April and October:** Shoulder seasons. Weather changeable, days shorter, but accommodations still open and prices reasonable. Good for experienced walkers.

**November-March:** Many accommodations close seasonally. Days are very short. Weather can be severe. Only walk if you're an experienced winter mountain walker.

## Why the Ring of Kerry Matters

The Ring of Kerry isn't special because tour buses use it or because it's famous. It's special because it's genuinely beautiful and geologically diverse. Within one 179-kilometer loop, you experience:

- **Mountain scenery:** The Macgillycuddy's Reeks and Caha Mountains
- **Coastal drama:** Cliffs, beaches, and Atlantic views
- **Valleys and valleys:** Lush, green, pastoral landscape
- **Ancient history:** Stone monuments, ring forts, standing stones
- **Contemporary culture:** Charming villages, excellent restaurants, genuine Irish hospitality
- **Wild landscape:** Isolated sections that feel genuinely remote

For walkers willing to slow down and explore sections, the Ring of Kerry offers something that the drive-through experience can never provide: intimacy with a landscape, understanding of how the land is used and lived in, appreciation for the subtle changes that happen between one village and the next.

## Practical Tips for Ring of Kerry Walking

**Navigation:** While the Ring of Kerry is marked on maps and partly waymarked, it's easy to get confused on small roads and paths. Carry good maps or use a walking app. OSI 1:50,000 maps are excellent.

**Supplies:** Bring more water and snacks than you think you'll need. Rural villages may not have shops. Convenience stores in larger towns (Killarney, Kenmare, Cahersiveen) are good for stocking up.

**Weather:** Atlantic weather changes rapidly. Pack waterproofs even on clear mornings. Sunscreen is essential—reflection off sea and stone intensifies sun effects.

**Footwear:** Proper hiking boots are essential, particularly on mountain sections and rough coastal paths.

**Courtesy:** Close gates behind you (farmers depend on them for livestock management). Stay on established paths. Respect private land.

**Timing:** These walks are best done at a slow, exploratory pace. Rush through sections and you'll miss details that make the Ring of Kerry genuinely special.

## The Real Ring of Kerry Experience

The famous Ring of Kerry drive takes 4-5 hours. Walkers who explore sections of the Ring take days or weeks, and they see something entirely different. They notice:

- The way light falls on stone at different times of day
- The sound of the Atlantic on different shores
- Wildflowers that aren't visible from cars
- Local people and genuine village life
- The immensity of mountains approached on foot rather than seen in glimpses from roads
- The texture of landscape that reveals itself only to those moving slowly enough to notice

This is why the Ring of Kerry, for walkers, is something far richer than a tour bus route. It's a landscape that asks to be walked, explored, and understood through experience.

Whether you walk one section or many, whether you combine walking with bus travel or commit to multi-day backpacking, the Ring of Kerry offers some of Ireland's finest walking and most dramatic scenery. Go slowly, look carefully, talk to locals, eat well, and let the landscape work its particular magic.

The Ring of Kerry has waited centuries for walkers to return to it. Your walking steps are following paths that generations have walked before. You're participating in a tradition as old as human movement itself.
