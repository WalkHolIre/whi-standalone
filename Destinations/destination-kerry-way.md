## Overview

County Kerry sits in the far southwest of Ireland, where rocky peninsulas reach into the Atlantic. The **Iveragh Peninsula** holds ancient oak woods, glacial lakes, open bog and wave-battered cliffs.

The **Kerry Way** circles this peninsula for 215km, making it Ireland's longest waymarked trail. We run the route anti-clockwise from **Killarney**, south through the national park, west along the Atlantic, and back east through *Kenmare*. This direction puts the wind at your back and saves the **Old Kenmare Road** mountain crossing for the final stretch.

The peninsula holds *Skellig Michael*, a UNESCO site 12km offshore; *Derrynane House*, home of Daniel O'Connell; and fishing villages where fresh seafood reaches your plate by evening.

## Full Description

### A Walk Around the Edge of Europe

Our Kerry Way tours begin in **Killarney**, a market town at the head of three famous lakes surrounded by Ireland's largest area of native woodland. The first stage crosses **Killarney National Park**, passing *Torc Waterfall* and the ancient yew and oak woods around *Muckross Lake*. The air smells of damp moss and wet bark. Red deer move through the trees at dawn — descendants of Ireland's only surviving native herd.

From the national park, the trail climbs into the **Black Valley** — one of the last places in Ireland to receive electricity, in 1976. The landscape here is raw and quiet: stone-walled fields, turf stacks, a handful of farmhouses beneath mountain ridges. The silence is thick enough to notice.

The route then turns west towards the Atlantic. The terrain opens into high bogland, and the coast appears first as a distant shimmer, then as a crashing presence. **Cahersiveen** and **Waterville** are the main overnight stops on the western leg — small towns with strong characters. *Waterville* was the favourite holiday retreat of *Charlie Chaplin*, who returned every summer through the 1960s. A life-size bronze statue of The Tramp stands on the seafront, hat tipped to the bay.

South of *Waterville*, the trail passes **Derrynane House** and the exquisite beach at *Derrynane Bay*, then the massive drystone walls of **Staigue Fort** — a 2,000-year-old ringfort standing on a hillside with views to the Kenmare River. The fort is 27 metres in diameter, its walls nearly four metres thick at the base, built without a drop of mortar. It is one of the finest prehistoric structures in Ireland.

The eastern leg moves through **Sneem** — a village of painted houses on the Kenmare River estuary — and on to **Kenmare**, a Georgian market town with the best restaurant scene on the entire trail. From Kenmare, the final stage follows the **Old Kenmare Road** back to Killarney, an ancient track over the mountain saddle between *Peakeen* and *Mangerton*. The views from the summit ridge stretch across the three Killarney lakes, all the way to *MacGillycuddy's Reeks* — the highest mountain range in Ireland.

### County Kerry as a Destination

The trail corridor passes through a landscape layered with history, geology and extraordinary wildlife. These are the places worth building time around:

**[Killarney National Park](https://www.nationalparks.ie/killarney/)** covers over 10,000 hectares and contains Ireland's most important surviving native oak and yew woodland. The park is home to Ireland's only wild herd of native red deer and one of the last remaining stands of ancient *sessile oak forest* in Europe. **Muckross House** — a Victorian mansion on the lakeshore — and the ruins of *Muckross Abbey* sit within the park grounds. **Ross Castle**, a 15th-century tower house on the shore of *Lough Leane*, was the last stronghold in Munster to fall to *Cromwell's* forces.

**[Skellig Michael](https://heritageireland.ie/places-to-visit/sceilg-mhichil/)** rises 218 metres from the Atlantic, 12km off the Iveragh coast. Between the 6th and 8th centuries, a community of monks built a monastery on its summit — six corbelled beehive huts, two oratories, a graveyard and a network of dry-stone terraces clinging to the rock above the sea. It became a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1996. Boat trips to *Skellig Michael* depart from *Portmagee* in season and should be booked well in advance.

**[Derrynane House](https://heritageireland.ie/places-to-visit/daniel-oconnell-house-derrynane-house/)** near *Caherdaniel* was the home of *Daniel O'Connell* (1775–1847), known as **The Liberator** for his campaign to secure Catholic Emancipation in 1829. The house is now a museum set within 120 hectares of national parkland, with a spectacular 1.5km shoreline of sandy beaches and dune grassland.

**Ladies View** — named for the ladies-in-waiting of *Queen Victoria* who admired the panorama during a visit in 1861 — is one of the most photographed viewpoints in Ireland. The lookout sits above the Upper Lake on the road between Killarney and Kenmare, and the view reaches across the full chain of Killarney's three lakes into the mountains beyond.

**The Gap of Dunloe** is a narrow glacial pass carved through the mountains north of the trail. The valley floor holds five lakes connected by the River Loe, with a stone *Wishing Bridge* at its midpoint. It can be walked or cycled as a rest-day excursion from Killarney.

## Landscape and Scenery

The **Killarney lakeland** opens with ancient oaks draped in moss and ferns, still water reflecting ridges, and waterfalls around every bend. Climb out and the landscape shifts to **blanket bog** — vast brown-gold peatland with cloud shadows racing across ridges.

The western coast drops in sea cliffs and rocky headlands; gannets dive and spray reaches the trail on rough days. Southern and eastern legs soften into green farmland and river valleys. The **Kenmare River** inlet shifts grey to blue to silver with the sky. *Sika deer* browse in woodlands; *choughs* wheel above cliffs; hedgerows blaze with wild fuchsia from May through October.

## Walking Difficulty

The Kerry Way is **moderate** for walkers with reasonable fitness. Our 9-day itinerary averages 18–24km daily with ascents of 400–700m. Terrain ranges from forest tracks to mountain paths and bogland; exposed ground can be wet.

Most demanding sections are the **mountain crossing above the Black Valley**, the coastal stage between *Cahersiveen* and *Waterville*, and the **Old Kenmare Road** ascent. Opening stages through the national park are gentler. Not ideal for walkers with serious knee issues or those new to multi-day walking.

## Who Is This For?

The Kerry Way suits walkers seeking genuine long-distance trail experience in wild Atlantic Ireland. Nine days of walking changes your rhythm, while overnight stops in villages with pubs and restaurants keep it sociable.

It works well for couples, solo walkers and small groups. Most guests are in their 40s to 60s with some experience. The cultural depth — *Skellig Michael*, *Derrynane*, Gaeltacht villages — appeals to walkers wanting more than scenery.

---

## Best Time to Visit

**May, June and September** are optimal. May brings wildflowers to the bogland, long evenings and light trail traffic. June offers the longest daylight for tackling the western cliff stages at a comfortable pace. September is arguably finest: the heather turns purple on hillsides, the light is clear and golden, and accommodation is significantly easier to book than summer months.

July and August are busiest; *Skellig Michael* boat trips are heavily booked and accommodation needs securing three to four months ahead. The trail is walkable April through October, but mountain sections above 400m require good waterproofs and confident navigation in poor visibility.

## Culture and Cuisine Highlights

County Kerry's culture runs deep. Irish is spoken daily in Gaeltacht villages. **Daniel O'Connell** — *The Liberator* — was born at *Cahersiveen* and raised at *Derrynane House*; his campaign for Catholic Emancipation changed Irish politics. **Skellig Michael's** monastic community survived 600 years on an Atlantic rock. **Charlie Chaplin** visited *Waterville* from 1959 onwards; the town's annual Comedy Film Festival keeps the connection alive.

### Local Food & Drink

- **Kerry mountain lamb** — grazed on Iveragh uplands with exceptional flavour
- **Fresh Atlantic seafood** — brown crab, lobster and chowder from *Portmagee* and *Cahersiveen*; seafood chowder is the signature lunch
- **Kenmare dining** — best restaurant scene on the trail: *The Lime Tree*, *No. 35* and *Mulcahy's* use local producers and Kerry beef, lamb and fish
- **Craft beverages** — Killarney Brewing & Distilling Co. (beer and whiskey) and Tom Crean Brewery (craft ales with taproom and tours)

## Accommodation Types

The Kerry Way corridor is well-served by family-run B&Bs, guesthouses and small hotels. **Killarney** has the widest choice at the start and finish. **Kenmare** is the most rewarding overnight town on the route, with Georgian streets, independent shops and excellent restaurants within walking distance. **Waterville** and **Sneem** are quieter stops with genuine warmth and character.

We pre-book quality accommodation with breakfast included at every stop, selecting properties that understand what walkers need at the end of a long day. Luggage is transferred between accommodations each morning — you carry only what you need for the day.

## How to Get Here

**Kerry Airport (KIR)** is 20 minutes from Killarney; **Cork Airport (ORK)** is 90 minutes away. Irish Rail operates direct services from Dublin Heuston to Killarney in 3.5 hours. Bus Éireann runs express coaches from Dublin, Cork and Limerick. Killarney is well-positioned as a starting point; arrive and walk the next morning.

The route is a circuit finishing where you started. Many walkers add a day or two to visit *Muckross House* or rest in town.
