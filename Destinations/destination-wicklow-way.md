## Overview

The Wicklow Way was Ireland's first long-distance walking trail and remains one of its finest. Running 127km from Clonegal in County Carlow north through the Wicklow Mountains to Marlay Park on Dublin's southern edge, the trail crosses granite ridges, glacial valleys, blanket bog and ancient monastic landscapes. The Wicklow Mountains are the backbone of this walk: Ireland's largest upland area, carved by ice into deep valleys and ribbon lakes that feel genuinely remote despite sitting less than an hour from Dublin. We walk this trail regularly and it never loses its hold on us.

## Full Description

### The Wicklow Way: South to North

We run the Wicklow Way from south to north, starting in Clonegal where the trail begins at the edge of County Carlow. This direction builds beautifully: early days wind through the gentle Carlow and south Wicklow foothills, open farmland and quiet lanes that ease you into the rhythm of multi-day walking.

As the trail moves north, the terrain changes decisively. You climb into the Wicklow Mountains proper, crossing blanket bog and exposed granite ridges that feel wild and elemental. The descent into Glenmalure, one of Ireland's deepest glacial valleys, is a powerful moment: a steep, wooded ravine where the 1798 rebels held out against British forces, and where the landscape still carries a sense of fierce independence.

From Glenmalure, the trail climbs again to Glendalough, the emotional and historical heart of the walk. The 6th-century monastic settlement founded by St Kevin sits in a steep-sided valley between two glacial lakes, ringed by granite peaks. We always recommend spending extra time here. The trail continues north over Djouce Mountain (725m) with views across the Irish Sea to Wales on clear days, then descends through Crone Wood and the Glencree Valley. The final stage drops through Enniskerry and suburban parkland to Marlay Park on Dublin's southern edge.

Most walkers complete the full trail in 6 to 8 days, covering 16 to 22km daily. We arrange accommodation in Tinahely, Glenmalure, Laragh, Roundwood and Enniskerry, with luggage transfers between each stop so you walk with just a daypack.

### The Wicklow Mountains

The Wicklow Mountains are Ireland's largest continuous upland area, stretching 60km from the Dublin suburbs south to the Carlow border. These are granite mountains, pushed up through the earth's crust over 400 million years and carved by glacial ice into the valleys and corrie lakes that define the landscape today.

Lugnaquilla at 925m is the highest peak in the range and the highest point in Ireland outside County Kerry. The summit is a broad plateau with views extending to the Blackstairs Mountains, the Mourne Mountains in Northern Ireland and, on clear days, across the Irish Sea to Snowdonia. The ascent is a full mountain day requiring proper navigation and fitness.

Lough Tay, often called the Guinness Lake for the dark colour of its peat-stained water and the white sand beach at its head, sits below Luggala Mountain in one of the most photographed glacial valleys in Ireland. The Sally Gap crossroads on the Military Road offers wide views across blanket bog in every direction; this is one of the few places in eastern Ireland where you can stand and see nothing but open mountain in every direction.

The Great Military Road, built after the 1798 Rebellion to control the mountains, runs 60km from Rathfarnham to Aghavannagh. Today it is one of Ireland's finest scenic drives and gives access to walking routes across the high ground: Three Rock, Kippure, Tonelagee and the Liffey Head Bog where the River Liffey rises.

The Dublin Mountains at the northern end of the range, peaks like Three Rock and Ticknock at around 500m, are accessible from the city suburbs in 30 to 45 minutes by public transport, ideal for walkers with a single day who want genuine mountain terrain.

### Wicklow Mountains National Park

Wicklow Mountains National Park covers 23,000 hectares of mountain, bog and woodland, making it one of the largest national parks in Europe's upland areas. The park is centred on Glendalough, where nine waymarked trails radiate from the visitor centre.

The Spinc ridge loop (9.5km, 3 to 4 hours) is the standout walk: a high-level trail along the spine of a glacial ridge above the Upper Lake, with views down into the valley and across to Camaderry and Mullacor. This is one of Ireland's great day walks and we recommend it to every walker who visits Glendalough.

Powerscourt Estate near Enniskerry sits within 47 acres of formal gardens ranked by National Geographic among the world's top three. The nearby Powerscourt Waterfall at 121m is Ireland's highest and makes a fine short hike through native woodland. The park supports red deer, sika deer, peregrine falcons and a recovering population of native red squirrels.

We build custom day-walk itineraries from Laragh, the village closest to Glendalough, combining the Spinc ridge, lakeside monastery walks and summit routes on Camaderry or Mullacor depending on fitness and weather.

### Linking to the Barrow Way

Walkers starting the Wicklow Way in Clonegal can extend their trip by connecting to the Barrow Way via the South Leinster Way, part of European long-distance route E8. From Clonegal, the South Leinster Way runs south through the Blackstairs foothills to Borris, where it meets the Barrow Way along the River Barrow. This link creates a combined itinerary of 10 to 14 days: riverside walking on the Barrow followed by mountain walking on the Wicklow Way, covering two very different Irish landscapes in a single trip. We can build this as a seamless package with accommodation and luggage transfers across both trails.

### Beyond the Wicklow Way

The Wicklow Mountains offer more than the long-distance trail. St Kevin's Way (30km) follows the medieval pilgrim route from Hollywood to Glendalough across the mountains. The Bray to Greystones Cliff Walk (8km) is a moderate coastal loop with views at every turn. Bray Head rises 241m directly above the town with panoramic views of Dublin Bay and the Wicklow Mountains. The Dún Laoghaire to Bray coastal walk (13 to 14km) is among the finest half-day walks near any European capital, passing through Sandycove, Dalkey and over Killiney Hill.

### Travel Tips

- The Wicklow Way is well waymarked throughout; no advanced navigation skills needed on the main trail
- Mountain routes off the main trail (Lugnaquilla, Tonelagee) require proper navigation, full waterproofs and mountain experience
- We handle luggage transfers between every overnight stop, so you walk with just a daypack
- Book accommodation 2 to 3 months ahead for May to September, particularly in Laragh and Roundwood
- Download offline maps: the Wicklow Mountains National Park app or 1:25,000 OS maps for higher routes
- Glendalough is best visited early morning or late afternoon when day-tripper crowds have cleared
- Weather changes rapidly above 500m; carry full waterproofs and layered clothing even in summer
- The trail can be walked in sections; a 3 to 4 day option from Glendalough to Dublin is popular for limited time

## Landscape and Scenery

Granite mountains rise from deep glacial valleys carved over 10,000 years of ice retreat. Upper ridges above 600m open to blanket bog and exposed rock with views extending to Wales. Lower slopes are oak, beech and birch woodland that turns golden in autumn. Ribbon lakes fill the valley floors: dark, peat-stained water reflecting mountain walls. Sika deer cross the moorland at dawn, peregrine falcons hunt the cliff faces, and red squirrels are returning to the native woodlands.

## Who Is This For?

Walkers who want a multi-day trail through genuine mountain scenery with the convenience of Dublin on the doorstep. The Wicklow Way suits first-time long-distance walkers as much as experienced trekkers. Couples and small groups with mixed abilities do well here: the main trail is moderate while side routes offer serious mountain days. Literary and history enthusiasts will find depth at Glendalough and in the 1798 Rebellion landscapes.

---

## Best Time to Visit

The walking season runs April to October. **May and June** are our top picks: long evenings, wildflowers on the moorland and lighter trail traffic. **September** offers golden light, quieter trails and easier booking. July and August are warmest but Glendalough gets crowded; book accommodation well ahead.

## Culture and Cuisine Highlights

Glendalough's 6th-century monastic settlement is one of Ireland's most important early Christian sites: a round tower, seven churches and two glacial lakes in a setting that explains why monks chose this valley. The 1798 Rebellion left its mark across these mountains, particularly in Glenmalure. For food, try Wicklow lamb from the mountain farms, artisan cheese from the Wicklow Way area, and visit Avoca Handweavers, Ireland's oldest working mill. Powerscourt Distillery near Enniskerry produces excellent Irish whiskey.
