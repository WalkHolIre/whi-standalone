## Overview

The Glens of Antrim are nine deep valleys carving through the Antrim Plateau to the North Atlantic coast. Unlike the crowds of Wicklow or Ben Nevis, these glens remain wonderfully quiet. Valleys are lightly visited, villages small, distances manageable.

Cushendall is the natural base, a small market town with good accommodation, genuine pubs and a vibrant trad music tradition on weekend evenings. Fair Head's basalt cliffs, Glenariff's waterfall gorge, Cushendun's caves and Rathlin Island's seabirds can all shape a week of walking and wandering.

## Full Description

### A Destination, Not a Single Trail

The Glens of Antrim work best as a walking destination rather than a linear trail. There's no single "Glens of Antrim Way" — instead, multiple options radiate from villages along the coast and into valleys. Some walks are short valley strolls through wooded glens; others are long moorland hikes across the plateau. Many combine coastal cliff walking with inland glen exploration. The beauty is that you build an itinerary around your fitness and preference, mixing easy walks with demanding ones and cultural stops with wild moorland days.

### Glenariff — The Queen of the Glens

Glenariff Forest Park is the most celebrated of the nine glens. The valley is wider and more generous than its neighbours, and the geology is exceptional. A gorge carved by water over millennia features a forest of oak, ash and beech cloaking the steep sides and a river maintaining a series of waterfalls as it descends towards the coast.

The main walking route is the Waterfall Walk, an 8-kilometre circuit descending via steep woodland steps to the gorge floor, following a timber boardwalk along the river past cascading water and moss-covered rocks, and returning via a forest track. The walk passes 22 waterfalls in the first half-mile alone, with the Ess-na-Crub and Ess-na-Larach being the most dramatic. The boardwalk was built about 100 years ago and has been carefully maintained; it remains one of the most memorable woodland walks in Ireland.

The Forest Park also offers easier riverside strolls for gentler terrain, with small restaurants and picnic areas at the car park.

### Fair Head — The Edge of the World

If Glenariff is the green heart of the Glens, Fair Head is the wild coast at their end. This walk demands clear days, when visibility stretches across the North Channel to the Mull of Kintyre and Scotland beyond. The headland is crowned with basalt cliffs rising over 100 metres, and the columnar basalt formations alone justify the effort.

The walk ascends steeply from Ballycastle and crosses moorland grazing before reaching the cliff edge. The views are vast. Rathlin Island sits close offshore, and on clear days the Scottish mainland is visible. This is where local legend places the Children of Lir looking back towards Ireland as they flew away; there's something otherworldly about standing here with the wind in your face and the cliffs falling away beneath your feet.

At Fair Head's base lies Murlough Bay, a wild sheltered beach accessible on foot, perfect for a longer walk combining moorland plateau and coastal descent.

### Cushendun and the Caves

Cushendun sits at the mouth of Glendun and has become famous as a filming location for HBO's Game of Thrones. The Cushendun Caves are sea caves carved into the cliff base by wave action and featured in the show, becoming a pilgrimage site for fans. But these caves held significance for centuries before television. They're genuinely atmospheric, dark and damp with the sound of water and seabirds echoing through them.

The village itself is charming with good accommodation and food. The coastline offers excellent coastal walking through cliff paths, beach sections and access into lower Glendun valley.

### Rathlin Island and the Seabirds

Rathlin Island lies just 6 kilometres offshore, accessible by ferry from Ballycastle in about 25 minutes. The island is tiny, approximately 6 kilometres long and 1.5 kilometres wide, but home to one of the largest seabird colonies in the United Kingdom. In summer (April to July, with peak numbers in May and June), over 250,000 seabirds nest on the island's cliffs: puffins, guillemots, razorbills, kittiwakes, fulmars and occasional corncrakes.

The RSPB Rathlin West Light Seabird Centre, built beside a clifftop lighthouse, offers telescopes and viewing platforms for close-range bird watching. Puffins are the stars with their bright orange feet and beaks, comical behaviour and vulnerability making them beloved by visitors. Bring binoculars and plan at least a half-day for the island.

### Other Glens and Heritage

Ossian's Grave, a neolithic court tomb dating back over 4,000 years, sits on a hillside above Glenariff and is accessible on foot. Local legend associates it with the mythological warrior-poet Oisín, though the truth of its origins is lost to time. The walk up offers excellent views across the glen and towards the coast.

Layde Church, standing near Cushendall, is a ruined medieval church with particular significance to the local MacDonnell clan, a powerful Gaelic Scottish family who controlled the Glens through the 16th and 17th centuries. The graves of clan members can still be seen in the churchyard; the church itself reminds us that this landscape is layered with history.

### Travel Tips

- **Base yourself in Cushendall** — a small market town centrally located to all the main glens with good accommodation and food options
- **September and October** bring exceptional light and autumn colour to the lower wooded valleys; we consider these the best months for photography
- **May is spectacular** — gorse blooms yellow on the coastal clifftops and bluebells carpet the forest floors
- **Carry cash** — some of the smaller B&Bs and traditional pubs in the villages don't reliably accept card payments
- **Plan for Rathlin Island** — book ferry tickets in advance during summer; allow a full half-day for the trip including the seabird centre
- **Fair Head is essential** — don't miss it, especially on clear days when views to Scotland are possible
- **The Moyle Way** is a 41km multi-day trail connecting the villages through the Glens; we can build itineraries around sections of this route

## Landscape and Scenery

The Antrim Plateau is windswept moorland coloured in heather purple and sedge green. The nine glens cut through like fingers, descending to wooded valleys and sea. The contrast is dramatic: within kilometres we move from exposed, boggy moorland to deep valleys with rivers and waterfalls.

Basalt columnar formations at Fair Head create geometric cliff patterns. Lower valleys hold mixed woodland of oak, ash, beech and birch, many ancient. Autumn turns the glens copper and gold; spring carpets forest floors with bluebells.

Wildlife is abundant. Red deer, golden eagles and peregrine falcons inhabit the moorland and cliffs. Rivers hold brown trout; coastal sections attract grey seals and basking sharks in summer. The coastline is dramatic with cliffs, caves and hidden beaches, genuinely wild and untamed.

## Walking Difficulty

Walking ranges from easy to moderate. We accommodate walkers of different abilities.

Easy walks include the Glenariff Waterfall Walk (8km with moderate ascent), riverside strolls and coastal beach walks at Cushendun. These suit families and those with limited hill experience.

Moderate walks include the Fair Head ascent (significant moorland climb on exposed terrain), Murlough Bay (moorland and coastal descent) and full glen traverses. These require good fitness and proper footwear but are achievable for walkers with reasonable experience. All walks are accessible year-round, though winter plateau sections may require navigation experience.

| Walk | Terrain | Distance | Difficulty |
|---|---|---|---|
| Glenariff Waterfall Walk | Forested gorge with steps and boardwalk | 8km | Easy–Moderate |
| Fair Head Cliff Walk | Moorland plateau to basalt cliffs | 6–8km | Moderate |

All walks are accessible year-round, though winter sections on the plateau may require navigation experience if visibility is poor.

## Who Is This For?

The Glens suit walkers seeking quieter, authentic Northern Ireland away from heavily-touristed trails. We recommend them for first-time visitors wanting to blend walking with cultural exploration and seeking good value accommodation.

Groups with mixed abilities thrive here; combine easy waterfall walks with demanding moorland routes. Solo walkers and couples enjoy welcoming villages and genuine pubs. The trad music scene in Cushendall adds cultural depth.

The Glens aren't ideal for those seeking high mountain altitude; the plateau tops at 550 metres and focuses on wild coastal and wooded valley walking.

---

## Best Time to Visit

May, June and September are optimal. May brings wildflower displays and gorse blooms; June offers longest daylight for longer walks. September is finest: bracken turns gold, light is clear, trails quieter, accommodation easier to book.

July and August are busy. April to July brings puffins to Rathlin Island. October offers autumn colour and excellent photography light. The Glens are walkable year-round, but winter brings reduced daylight and unpredictable weather on exposed plateaus.

## Culture and Cuisine Highlights

The Glens have distinct Gaelic Scottish heritage, rooted in families like the MacDonnells who controlled the region through the 17th century. Layde Church preserves clan graves. The landscape holds 4,000 years of Neolithic and medieval settlement.

Contemporary culture thrives in Cushendall's vibrant traditional Irish music scene. Pubs host trad sessions regularly, especially weekends, attracting musicians from across Ireland; an authentic experience of living Irish culture.

### Local Food & Drink

- **Dulse** — dried Atlantic seaweed with salty, mineral flavour
- **Yellow Man toffee** — brittle honeycomb sweet from Ballycastle's Ould Lammas Fair
- **Fresh seafood** — chowder, crab and local fish in Ballycastle restaurants
- **Soda farls and potato bread** — Northern Irish staples, perfect fuel before walks
- **Local hill lamb** — pasture-raised on moorlands, served in village pub stews
- **Bushmills Whiskey** — world's oldest licensed distillery, 20 minutes from Ballycastle

## Accommodation Types

Cushendall is the best base, a small market town with guesthouses, family-run B&Bs and small hotels within easy reach of all main glens. Hospitality is genuine rather than commercial. Ballycastle offers more facilities.

Traditional B&Bs throughout villages offer hearty breakfasts and genuine local knowledge. We pre-book at properties understanding what walkers need at day's end.

## How to Get Here

Belfast International Airport is 70 kilometres south with regular services from North America, Europe and the UK. Buses connect Belfast to Cushendall in about 90 minutes. Car hire from Belfast is straightforward and recommended; drive 90 kilometres via the A2 coastal route.

Translink NI operates regional bus services; the Ulsterbus 162 connects Ballycastle to Belfast. The Rathlin Island Ferry runs from Ballycastle harbour (25 minutes), year-round with increased summer frequency; book in advance during peak season.

The Glens use Sterling (GBP) currency; this is part of the United Kingdom.
