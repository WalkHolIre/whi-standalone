## Overview

The Beara Peninsula straddles the Cork-Kerry border as one of southwest Ireland's most remote places: isolated mountains, villages rooted in their pasts, and Atlantic coast that arrives without warning. The 196km loop circles the peninsula in nine to ten days, winding through ancient stone circles, 19th-century copper mine ruins, and cliff-edge paths where gannets wheel overhead. We run the route anti-clockwise from Glengarriff, starting on the sheltered north coast and building through more demanding mountain terrain. The Beara demands experience and respect for its weather, but rewards you with something increasingly rare: genuine remoteness and solitude.

## Full Description

### A Peninsula Shaped by Mining, Famine and Perseverance

The Beara Way begins at Glengarriff on the eastern end. The opening stages trace the northern coast along the sheltered Kenmare River through working farms and small settlements. Garnish Island sits offshore, its Italian gardens designed by Harold Peto now home to a 250-seal colony accessible by ferry. This eastern section opens gently, letting walkers settle into rhythm before the peninsula's character becomes wilder.

As the trail moves west, landscape and history shift completely. The Allihies Copper Mines rise above the trail, their shafts, spoil heaps and ruined engine houses telling the story of industrial boom and collapse. Mining began in 1812; by mid-century it employed over 1,600 workers, mixing local labour with Cornish expertise brought across the Irish Sea. The technology was extraordinary for its time: steam engines powering crushers, a network of tramways carrying ore, entire communities built around the extraction of red metal from mountain rock. The boom ended by 1884, when many emigrated to Butte, Montana, replicating the mining communities they'd left behind. Allihies now, with its colourful cottages and ruined houses crumbling slowly back into the landscape, is a ghost of industrial Ireland. We have walked the old mine roads, visited the museum, and talked with locals who remember when this was work, not heritage.

The Dursey Island cable car, opened in 1969, is Ireland's only cable car crossing open sea. At 250 metres across, it dazzles: a moment of genuine exposure over the Atlantic, descending 300 metres to the water below. Dursey Island itself is quiet and sparse, marking the official start of the E8 long-distance path running to Istanbul. Few walkers venture here; fewer still understand what that isolation means.

The southern coast is more exposed and dramatic. The trail climbs towards Hungry Hill (685 metres) and the Mare's Tail waterfall, pouring straight down the mountainside for nearly 200 metres. Clear days reveal views across Bantry Bay and back over the peninsula towards Kenmare. This section of the walk demands respect for weather and navigation; the exposure is real.

The Healy Pass, built in 1847 as a famine relief project, switchbacks up through moorland at steep gradients. Walking alongside it, you're constantly aware of the peninsula's recent past: landscape shaped by crisis and recovery, by people who endured famine and chose to stay. The pass itself is a monument to human labour undertaken in desperate circumstances; its construction employed hundreds during Ireland's greatest catastrophe.

The Ardgroom Stone Circle sits quietly above the trail: 11 Bronze Age stones predating the copper mines by thousands of years. They've stood here for four millennia, watching the landscape change around them — forests fall, people arrive and leave, economies rise and collapse. The eastern section returns towards civilisation through Eyeries, a brightly painted village home to Milleens Cheese dairy and Veronica Steele, who revived Irish farmhouse cheese in the 1970s.

Castletownbere is a real working town: Ireland's largest whitefish port, with €107 million in annual landings. The harbour in early morning fills with trawlers unloading catch, wholesalers working, the smell of fresh fish and diesel. Tourism is secondary; fishing is everything. The final stages loop back along the northern coast towards Glengarriff through West Cork wine country, completing a circuit that feels like stepping through multiple centuries.

### County Cork & the West Cork Food Scene

West Cork is one of Ireland's finest food-producing regions, rooted in artisan cheesemaking and fresh fish. Castletownbere's role as the largest whitefish port means brown crab and lobster land daily, served simply with butter and lemon. Milleens Cheese at Eyeries, made by Veronica Steele since 1976, sparked Ireland's cheesemaking revolution. Durrus Cheese and the Woodcock Smokery near Ardgroom represent the wider artisan food scene, woven genuinely into the landscape.

### The Literary Landscape

Daphne du Maurier's novel Hungry Hill (1943) drew inspiration from the copper mining history and the Puxley family of Allihies. The landscape becomes almost a character: wild, unforgiving, marked by industrial cycles and the sea's permanence. Walking through Allihies with engine houses in view, you understand what drew du Maurier to this story.

The peninsula carries the weight of famine history. Pre-famine population was approximately 39,000; today it stands at fewer than 6,000. The landscape itself is a memorial: empty valleys, ruined cottages, churches in isolation. Walking it, the sense of absence is palpable.

## Landscape and Scenery

The Beara is geologically one of southwest Ireland's most dramatic landscapes. Mountains reach only 685 metres but feel vast because they rise directly from the Atlantic with no gentle transition. The granite and quartzite Caha Mountains form a ragged spine with dramatic corries and waterfalls on the north side and steeper, exposed faces on the south.

The eastern section through Eyeries is pastoral and sheltered; the southern coast is raw and elemental. Rocky hillsides, ancient stone walls in disrepair, windswept bogland and constant Atlantic swells shape the terrain. Light changes with extraordinary speed: clear sunshine with Bantry Bay views can shift to enveloping mist within minutes.

Wildlife is abundant: gannets dive off southern cliffs; red grouse flush from heather; sea cliffs support razorbills, guillemots and choughs. Summer brings wildflowers; autumn rowan trees turn brilliant orange-red against grey granite. The sense of wildness emerges from genuine remoteness, absence of development, constant Atlantic presence, and deep time.

## Walking Difficulty

The Beara Way suits experienced walkers with strong fitness and mountain terrain confidence. Daily distances run 14 to 26km with ascents of 400 to 800m on mountain sections involving sustained height gain over rough, undefined terrain where paths exist only as faint moorland tracks.

The trail is less comprehensively waymarked than popular routes. Open mountain sections, particularly around Hungry Hill, present navigation challenges in poor visibility. Waterproof footwear and full weather-appropriate gear are mandatory year-round. The southern coast crossing and Hungry Hill approach are most demanding, with exposed terrain. Early Glengarriff stages are gentler, letting you settle rhythm before terrain becomes serious.

## Who Is This For?

The Beara Way is for experienced walkers wanting genuine wildness and comfortable with navigation and remote, exposed conditions. If the Kerry Way feels too busy and you want solitude, challenging terrain and discovery, the Beara is your trail.

It suits confident hillwalkers understanding weather management, comfortable navigating with map and compass, and prepared for landscape offering limited shelter. It's particularly rewarding for those interested in industrial heritage and famine history. Not ideal for first-time long-distance walkers or those uncomfortable with poor visibility navigation.

---

## Best Time to Visit

May, June and September are optimal. May brings long daylight, moorland wildflowers and relatively stable westerly winds. June offers longest days, making remote southern sections more manageable. September combines golden light, noticeably fewer visitors and cooler temperatures comfortable for mountain walking.

July and August can be busy; accommodation books up and exposed sections can feel uncomfortably hot. October brings beautiful hillside colour and is walkable in cooler conditions. Winter above 400m requires serious navigation, appropriate cold-weather gear and careful Atlantic weather timing.

## Culture and Cuisine Highlights

The Beara carries deep history: copper mining, famine catastrophe, persistent fishing communities. Allihies' mines (1812-1884) employed over 1,600 workers; the Copper Mine Museum tells this story with genuine depth. Famine reshaped population from 39,000 to fewer than 6,000. Empty cottages and landscape silence mark this catastrophe. Castletownbere harbour remains the economic heart, a working port where commercial fishing dominates.

Milleens Cheese, made by Veronica Steele since 1976, represents post-famine recovery and sparked Irish farmhouse cheesemaking revolution. Daphne du Maurier's 1943 novel Hungry Hill drew inspiration from copper mining and the Puxley family.

### Local Food & Drink

- **Castletownbere fish** — Ireland's largest whitefish port; brown crab and lobster landed daily, served simply
- **Milleens Cheese** — unpasteurised, washed-rind farmhouse cheese with international recognition
- **Durrus Cheese & Woodcock Smokery** — artisan West Cork products using local ingredients
- **Hearty pub food** — village pubs serve generous stew, fresh fish and local meat

## Accommodation Types

Accommodation is simpler and more dispersed than popular Kerry trails; booking ahead is essential. Family-run bed and breakfasts and small guesthouses are standard, comfortable and genuinely local. Villages like Castletownbere, Eyeries, Allihies and Glengarriff have most options.

We pre-book quality accommodation with breakfast included and arrange luggage transfers. This is important on the Beara, where distances are substantial and rough terrain makes carrying full packs unpleasant.

## How to Get Here

Cork Airport (ORK) and Kerry Airport (KIR) are approximately 90 minutes from Glengarriff. Cork is larger with better international connections.

Bus Éireann and GoBus operate express services from Cork City and Killarney, taking approximately 1.5 hours. We provide full transfer guidance with every booking. Glengarriff's limited taxis require advance arrangement.

Irish Rail services Cork and Killarney but not Glengarriff directly; nearest rail heads are Mallow (90 minutes' drive) or Killarney (1.5 hours). During the walk, Castletownbere is the largest resupply point with supermarket, butcher and post office.
