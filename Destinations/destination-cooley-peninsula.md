## Overview

The Cooley Peninsula sits at the northeastern edge of Leinster, shaped by glaciers and steeped in myth. Just 100 kilometres north of Dublin, it's one of Ireland's most underrated walking destinations. The Cooley Mountains reach 589 metres, and from Slieve Foy's summit you can see three countries: Ulster, the Midlands and the Mournes across Carlingford Lough.

Carlingford Lough is a glacial fjord, one of Ireland's most beautiful bodies of water, ringed by mountains on three sides. Carlingford, the medieval town and gateway, is one of Europe's finest surviving medieval towns with King John's Castle, the Tholsel gate and partly visible town walls. The oysters are world-class and restaurants genuinely excellent.

## Full Description

### A Walk Through Myth and Mountain

We begin in Carlingford, exploring King John's Castle and medieval laneways before heading to the mountains. The Cooley Way is a circular route; most walkers start north toward Omeath, a small lakeside village where the ferry to Warrenpoint departs daily.

The trail climbs through woodland and moorland onto the Cooley ridge. This is the mythological heartland of the Táin Bó Cúailnge, the Cattle Raid of Cooley, one of the greatest epics in early Irish literature. Every valley and stream is named in this 2,000-year-old story; walking these paths means treading the landscape of myth.

The ridge walk is one of Ireland's finest, with views constantly shifting as you move west. Lough Carlingford deepens below on your left; moorland rolls toward the Midlands on your right. Waymarking is excellent throughout.

Slieve Foy at 589 metres is the walk's emotional centre. From here, the Mournes rise across the lough like a dark wall. The Midlands stretch to the horizon. On clear days, you can see the Dublin Mountains to the south. This modest elevation delivers extraordinary vantage points due to position and exposure rather than height.

From the ridge, the trail descends through the Carlingford Lough Greenway, a gentle walking path built along a closed railway line, before looping back toward Carlingford. Final sections are considerably easier, giving your legs rest before finishing in the medieval town.

### The Cooley Peninsula as a Destination

**Carlingford** is one of Ireland's finest surviving medieval towns. King John's Castle, built around 1190 by Norman baron Hugh de Lacy, dominates the harbour. The Tholsel gate still stands on the main street. Town walls remain partly visible and Dominican friary ruins sit just outside. Walking Carlingford's narrow laneways means following footsteps from the 13th, 14th and 15th centuries.

**[King John's Castle (Carlingford)](https://heritageireland.ie/places-to-visit/carlingford-castle/)** is managed by Heritage Ireland and free to enter. Spend at least an hour here; the castle offers views back across the lough and connection to the Norman conquest of Ireland.

**Slieve Foy** anchors the Cooley Way. The ridge walk to the summit is 8–10 kilometres, taking 3–4 hours with photo stops. The 360-degree vista is one of Ireland's finest for minimal effort.

**Carlingford Lough** is a classic glacial fjord, 11 kilometres long and up to 4 kilometres wide, the second-largest sea lough in Ireland. The exceptionally clean water is why oysters are so famous. On calm mornings in late spring and early summer, the water is mirror-still and mountain reflections are complete.

**[Proleek Dolmen](https://www.archaeology.ie/)** is a Neolithic passage tomb near Dundalk, about 20 kilometres south; a significant detour for those interested in prehistoric Ireland. The massive capstone dates to around 3000 BCE.

**The Táin Trail** is an excellent heritage cycling or walking route following the mythological Cattle Raid across the peninsula. Every place name gains significance once you know the story.

**Omeath**, a small village on Carlingford Lough shore, marks the turning point of the circular route. Several small hotels serve the area, and the Carlingford Ferry operates daily to Warrenpoint, making it easy to extend into the Mourne Mountains.

## Landscape and Scenery

The Cooley Mountains reach 589 metres; their position and exposure give them disproportionate drama. They rise above Carlingford Lough, a glacial fjord, which is 11 kilometres long, up to 4 kilometres wide. The underlying geology is sandstone, creating rounded grass-covered slopes. In late summer, heather blooms purple across moorland. Spring brings golden gorse. Ravensdale Forest to the south is mixed woodland of oak, ash and hazel, ancient and rich in birdlife.

On windless mornings in late spring and early summer, the lough is mirror-still with complete mountain reflections. Red grouse inhabit higher moorland. Peregrine falcons nest above Slieve Foy. Harbour porpoises, seals and fish populate the lough itself.

## Walking Difficulty

The Cooley Way is moderate for those with reasonable fitness. Our 2–3 day itinerary covers 12–20 kilometres per day with daily ascents typically 300–500 metres. The route is well-waymarked and navigation straightforward even in poor visibility.

The most demanding sections are open ridge walks, particularly to Slieve Foy and exposed moorland where wind is strong and weather changes quickly. The descent from Slieve Foy is steep but brief. Final stages approaching Omeath are gentler.

The Carlingford Lough Greenway is entirely flat and easy, suitable for all fitness levels including families with children.

## Who Is This For?

The Cooley Peninsula is perfect for shorter walking holidays—a long weekend or 4–5 days—without sacrificing scenery or genuine mountain walking. The compact size gives satisfaction without committing to a full week.

It suits walkers interested in Irish mythology and early medieval history. Once you know the Táin Bó Cúailnge, every place name gains significance. Walking becomes a form of reading landscape as text.

It's excellent for combining two destinations. The ferry from Omeath to Warrenpoint links to the Mourne Mountains, making 3 days on the Cooley Way and 3–4 days on the Mournes possible in a week-long itinerary for experienced walkers.

## Best Time to Visit

April through October offers best walking conditions. April and May bring clear skies, bright light and quiet trails; gorse flowers and easy accommodation booking. June and early July offer longest daylight hours. August brings the Carlingford Oyster Festival, a culinary highlight.

September is finest: bracken and heather turn gold and rust, light is clear and low, trails are quieter than summer, and accommodation books easily. October brings autumn colour to woodlands and sharp, clear days for photography.

July and August are busiest; book accommodation 2–3 months in advance. Winter requires proper cold-weather gear and mountain experience.

## Culture and Cuisine Highlights

The Cooley Peninsula's identity stems from early medieval Christianity, Norman-English medieval urbanisation and Irish epic literature. The Táin Bó Cúailnge, the Cattle Raid of Cooley, composed in the 7th and 8th centuries, tells of Queen Medb's war against Ulster to obtain the stud bull from Cooley. Walking the Cooley Way with knowledge of the Táin adds extraordinary depth; every stream, ridge and valley carries narrative weight.

Carlingford is a physical text of medieval history. King John's Castle, built around 1190, controlled the lough. The Tholsel gate is one of Europe's finest surviving medieval town gates. Medieval street plans remain fundamentally unchanged from the 1400s.

### Local Food & Drink

- **Carlingford oysters** — farmed in clean lough water, among Ireland's finest; raw or gratinéed
- **Fresh seafood** — mussels, crab and lobster caught by local fishermen, featured on every restaurant menu
- **Dundalk Bay prawns** — regional speciality served simply grilled or in seafood pasta
- **Carlingford Brewing Company** — craft ales and stouts brewed locally

## Accommodation Types

Carlingford is the accommodation hub and perfectly suited to walkers. The town has small boutique hotels, quality guesthouses and family-run B&Bs, many in converted medieval buildings overlooking the lough. Quality is impressively consistent.

Omeath, the second overnight stop, has smaller options with charming village setting. We pre-book quality accommodation with breakfast included, selecting properties that understand walker needs. Luggage transfers each morning; you carry only a daypack.

## How to Get Here

Dublin Airport (DUB) is the primary international arrival point with direct services from North America, the Middle East and Europe. Carlingford is 100 kilometres north of Dublin, approximately 1.5 to 2 hours via the M1 motorway. Bus Éireann operates coach services from Dublin's Busáras station to Dundalk (1.5 hours), with connections to Carlingford. Irish Rail services Dublin Heuston to Dundalk (20 kilometres south); from Dundalk, Bus Éireann connects to Carlingford or local taxis can be arranged. Parking is available in town and at accommodation properties.
