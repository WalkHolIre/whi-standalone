## Overview

The Causeway Coast and Glens of Antrim are two contrasting landscapes united in a single walk. The western coast is raw and volcanic: black basalt cliffs rise 80 metres sheer from the Atlantic with geometric columns of rock, castles perch impossibly above the waves. The eastern glens shift the mood entirely—cliffs lower, land opens into glacial valleys, rivers carve through ancient woodlands, and walking becomes quieter and more intimate. We join these sections into a unified 7–9 day tour: the Causeway Coastal Route in the west and the Antrim Glens network in the east, over 100km of the most diverse coastal landscape in the British Isles. The contrast makes them stronger together.

## Full Description

### A Coast That Changes Everything

Our tour begins in the west, either at Portstewart or near Giant's Causeway, where the Atlantic has sculpted something unforgettable. Giant's Causeway is not a single viewpoint but an entire landscape: 40,000 hexagonal basalt columns left behind when volcanic lava cooled unevenly, creating natural architecture that looks designed rather than formed. Walking across the warm black stone, some columns thick as table legs, others sharp-edged and fractured, you feel small beneath the sea and sky.

From here, the western section climbs above basalt cliffs. We pass Dunluce Castle, a medieval fortress on an 80-metre promontory overlooking the rocks below; broken but spectacular. Beyond lies Bushmills, home to the world's oldest licensed whiskey distillery operating since 1608. The air carries the warm smell of grain and distillation, and the distillery tour and single malt tasting are not to be missed.

The walking continues east toward Ballycastle. We reach Carrick-a-Rede, a rope bridge suspended above a sea chasm; thrilling for the committed, though the approach itself offers some of the finest coastal views on the trail. By Ballycastle, having walked the western section, we experience the Causeway Coast fully: geological drama, medieval history, whiskey heritage and solitude above a wild sea.

### The Glens Section — A Different World

The eastern section begins as we leave Ballycastle and head inland. The coast softens but drama reshapes itself. The path enters Glenshesk, the first of the Nine Glens, a glacier-carved valley now filled with alder and birch woodland, fern banks and running water. Each glen has its own character. Glenarriff is the most visited and the forest park contains waterfall walks and gorge trails worth exploring. Glenardale, Glencloy and Glencorp offer quieter walking through pastoral farmland and moorland above the tree line.

The contrast to the western section is immediate. Instead of cliffs we have valleys; instead of exposed ridgelines, wooded river corridors; instead of basalt columns, rushing streams lined with smooth stones and moss-covered banks. Light filters through trees and reflects off wet rocks, creating the soft greyish-green quality that feels entirely Irish.

The eastern section climaxes at Cushendun, a small coastal village with sea caves carved by wave action into Jurassic rocks, known as a Game of Thrones filming location. The beach is one of the finest in Northern Ireland. From here, the route continues south toward Larne or Cushendall, bringing us back to the coast after days inland.

### The Places That Define This Walk

Giant's Causeway deserves the quietest possible approach—early morning or late afternoon when day-trippers have gone. The hexagonal columns tell the geological story most powerfully. The National Trust visitor centre has good displays, but the stone beneath your feet speaks loudest.

Dunluce Castle sits on a promontory above a sea chasm, looking like something from a fever dream. The ruins date from the 13th century and were continuously occupied until the early 1600s, when portions fell into the sea. The location is simultaneously beautiful and defensible, explaining its regional power.

Glenariff Forest Park deserves a dedicated rest day. The park contains waterfalls, gorge walks and a visitor centre. The Rainbow Trail, a 2.4km loop through the lower section, is steep in places but manageable and beautiful.

Fair Head offers some of the highest cliffs in Northern Ireland; basalt columns again but from a different angle, with clear-day views to the Scottish coast and Rathlin Island. The walking here is exposed and wild.

Cushendun Caves are smaller than their filming fame suggests, but they sit in a genuinely scenic cove with a crescent beach and show how different the eastern coastal geology is from the columnar basalt of the west.

## Landscape and Scenery

The western Causeway Coast is built from basalt lava that flowed 60 million years ago, fracturing into characteristic hexagonal columns. The cliffs are dark and sheer, rising 80 metres, shaped by Atlantic storms and constant wave action.

The Nine Glens were carved by glaciers during the last ice age; each valley is U-shaped between high moorland plateaus. Glacial action left hanging valleys, waterfalls and constantly present water. Vegetation is green and lush: native oak and alder woodland in lower sections, moorland heath above. Razorbills, guillemots and herring gulls nest on cliff faces. The eastern glens host red grouse, red squirrels and otters. Rathlin Island offshore hosts over 9,000 seabirds including puffins, razorbills, guillemots and kittiwakes.

## Walking Difficulty

The tour is moderate for walkers with reasonable fitness. Our 7–9 day itinerary covers 14–22km per day with daily ascents typically 200–400 metres. The route is well-marked and easier to navigate than more remote trails.

The western Causeway Coast is most exposed; wind is constant and paths wet and slippery after rain. The eastern glens are more sheltered, easier underfoot, with less elevation gain. Ankle-supporting waterproof footwear is essential throughout.

## Best Time to Visit

May, June and September are optimal. May brings longer evenings, lighter trail traffic and wildflowers in glen woodlands. June offers longest daylight hours. September is finest; bracken and heather are gold and rust, light is clear and low-angled, trails are quieter and accommodation easier to book.

April and early May offer fewer visitors. July and August are busiest; Giant's Causeway and Carrick-a-Rede receive heavy numbers and need advance booking. October brings autumn colour and fewer crowds. The trail is walkable year-round but winter brings short daylight and Atlantic exposure. Rathlin Island birdwatching is best April–July.

## Culture and Cuisine Highlights

The region has been continuously inhabited from early Irish settlers to Scottish Gaels. The MacDonnells made Dunluce Castle their power base in the medieval period. Built in the 13th century, portions fell into the sea in 1639, leaving dramatic ruins.

Bushmills Distillery is Ireland's oldest licensed distillery, operating since 1608. Tours walk through water sources, malting, distillation and aging. Single malt tasting is proper cultural immersion.

The Ould Lammas Fair in Ballycastle (late August) is one of Ireland's oldest fairs. The traditional snack of dulse and Yellow Man is sold only here.

### Local Food & Drink

- **Bushmills single malt whiskey** — the definitive Causeway Coast drink with distillery tours available
- **Fresh Antrim coast seafood** — chowder, crab, smoked salmon and langoustines
- **Dulse and Yellow Man** — iconic Ballycastle snack combination
- **Ulster fry** — Northern Ireland's signature cooked breakfast, ideal before a full day's walking
