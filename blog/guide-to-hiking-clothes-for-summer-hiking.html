Summer Hiking Clothes: What to Wear for Comfort on the Trail

My name is Cliff, and I run Walking Holiday Ireland. Choosing the right summer hiking clothes makes a bigger difference to your comfort than almost any other preparation. Get it right and you stay cool when it's warm, dry when it rains, and comfortable all day. Get it wrong and you end up chafing, overheating, or shivering in a surprise downpour — any of which can turn a great day's walking into a miserable one.

In Ireland, summer weather is mild but changeable. You might start a morning in sunshine, walk through a rain shower at lunchtime, and finish in warm evening light. That's why knowing what to wear hiking in summer here requires a layered approach rather than just shorts and a t-shirt. This guide covers everything from base layers to rain gear, with specific advice for walking in Ireland's climate.

The Layering System

The foundation of any good hiking outfit summer or winter is the layering system. Instead of one thick garment, you wear multiple thin layers that you can add or remove as conditions change. For summer hiking, you typically need three layers: a base layer, a mid layer, and a waterproof shell.

The base layer sits against your skin and its job is to move sweat away from your body. In summer, this means a lightweight, breathable fabric — synthetic materials like polyester or nylon dry fast and wick moisture well. Merino wool is another excellent choice. It regulates temperature naturally, resists odour even after several days of wear, and feels soft against the skin. Avoid cotton — it absorbs sweat, stays wet, and chills you when the wind picks up.

The mid layer provides warmth when the temperature drops. In Irish summers, a lightweight fleece or a thin softshell jacket is usually enough. On warmer days, you might carry this in your daypack rather than wear it. On cooler days or at higher elevations, you'll be glad to have it.

The outer layer is your waterproof jacket. In Ireland, this is non-negotiable regardless of season. A breathable waterproof with taped seams keeps rain out while allowing sweat vapour to escape. Look for a jacket with a hood, adjustable cuffs, and pit zips for ventilation. A good waterproof jacket is the single most important piece of summer hiking gear for Ireland.

Tops and Shirts

For the upper body, choose tops made from synthetic or merino fabric. A short-sleeved hiking shirt or technical t-shirt works as a base layer on warm days. Long-sleeved options give you sun protection without sunscreen and are worth considering for exposed ridges and coastal walks where the sun reflects off the water.

Avoid heavy fabrics and thick seams that can cause chafing under pack straps. A flat-seam construction is more comfortable over long distances. Bright or light colours reflect more sun and keep you slightly cooler than dark fabrics, though this matters less in Ireland's moderate temperatures.

For women choosing a summer hiking outfit, sports bras designed for medium-impact activity work well under hiking tops. Look for moisture-wicking fabric and a comfortable fit that won't ride up or rub during a full day of walking. Many outdoor brands now make women-specific hiking shirts with a better cut through the shoulders and torso, which makes a real difference in comfort over a full day.

Trousers and Shorts

Hiking trousers are better than jeans in every way — they're lighter, they dry faster, they stretch with your movement, and they don't absorb water. Good hiking trousers are made from nylon or a nylon-elastane blend that gives you freedom of movement on steep ground. Convertible trousers with zip-off legs give you the option of switching to shorts when conditions allow.

Shorts are tempting in warm weather, but in Ireland they're best saved for genuinely hot days. The combination of midges, thistles, gorse, and the possibility of rain means trousers are usually the better choice. If you do wear shorts, lightweight hiking shorts with a gusseted crotch are more comfortable than casual shorts.

Waterproof trousers are worth carrying even in summer. When rain arrives — and in Ireland it will — full-length waterproof over-trousers keep your legs dry and warm. Lightweight options pack down small and weigh very little.

Footwear

Good footwear is the most important piece of hiking equipment full stop. For summer hiking in Ireland, you have two main options: hiking boots and trail shoes.

Hiking boots with ankle support are the traditional choice and still the best option for mountain walking, rough terrain, and multi-day trails. They protect your ankles on uneven ground, grip well on wet rock and mud, and keep your feet dry in boggy sections. Our guide to choosing the right hiking boots covers this in detail.

Trail shoes or approach shoes are lighter and more breathable, making them comfortable for easier trails, flat paths, and warm weather. They dry faster than boots if they get wet. However, they offer less ankle support and protection on rough ground.

Whatever footwear you choose, break it in before your trip. New boots on the first day of a walking holiday are a recipe for blisters. Wear them on several practice walks beforehand — at least three or four outings of increasing distance.

Good hiking socks matter as much as good boots. Merino wool or synthetic hiking socks wick moisture, reduce friction, and cushion your feet. Bring at least two pairs so you always have a dry pair available. Avoid cotton socks — they hold moisture against the skin and increase the risk of blisters. Some walkers prefer a thin liner sock under a thicker hiking sock for extra protection on longer days.

Gaiters are another useful accessory, particularly in Ireland where boggy sections can splash mud and water onto your lower legs. Lightweight ankle gaiters weigh next to nothing and keep debris out of your boots on rough trails.

Accessories and Extras

A sun hat or cap protects your head and face on exposed sections. In Ireland, a hat with a brim also keeps light rain off your face.

Sunglasses are essential on bright days, particularly for coastal walks where the combination of sea glare and sky can be intense. Polarised lenses reduce glare from water and wet surfaces.

Lightweight gloves are worth packing even in summer if you're walking at altitude. Mountain summits can be cold and windy even on a warm day at sea level.

A buff or neck gaiter is one of the most versatile pieces of warm weather hiking clothes. Wear it as a neck warmer, headband, sun shade, or dust mask. It weighs almost nothing and takes up no space.

Sunscreen is essential even in Ireland's moderate climate. At altitude and on exposed coastal sections, UV can be stronger than you expect, especially when reflected off water. Apply SPF 30 or higher before you set out and reapply through the day. Lip balm with SPF protection stops sunburnt lips, which are surprisingly common among walkers who forget about them.

A lightweight daypack in the 20 to 30 litre range carries all your summer hiking gear comfortably. Look for a pack with padded hip straps to distribute weight, a mesh back panel for ventilation, and external pockets for water bottles. Pack your waterproof layers at the top where you can reach them quickly when weather changes.

What to Pack for Ireland Specifically

Ireland's weather in summer is mild — typical temperatures range from 15 to 20 degrees Celsius, occasionally reaching 25 on hot days. Rain can arrive at any time, and mountain weather changes quickly. Here's what I recommend for a walking holiday here:

Wear a moisture-wicking base layer, hiking trousers, and comfortable broken-in boots. Carry a mid layer fleece, a waterproof jacket, waterproof trousers, a hat, sunglasses, and sunscreen. Pack spare socks and a dry base layer in your daypack in case you get caught in heavy rain. Our full packing list covers everything you need.

The best time to visit Ireland for hiking is May to September. June and July are the warmest and driest months, though rain is possible any time. Common Mistakes to Avoid

The biggest mistake I see walkers make is wearing cotton. Cotton t-shirts, cotton socks, and denim jeans all absorb moisture and take hours to dry. On a warm morning you might get away with it, but the moment rain arrives or you start sweating on a climb, cotton becomes cold and heavy. Synthetic or merino alternatives cost a little more but last longer and perform far better.

Another common error is not carrying rain gear because the forecast looks good. In Ireland, forecasts are guidelines rather than guarantees. A waterproof jacket and trousers packed in your daypack weigh under a kilogram and can save your entire day.

Finally, don't underestimate the importance of fit. Clothing that's too tight restricts movement and causes chafing. Clothing that's too loose flaps in the wind and catches on vegetation. Try everything on before your trip, ideally with your pack on, and make sure you can move freely in every direction.

Getting your summer hiking clothes right is simple: wear layers, choose moisture-wicking fabrics, always carry a waterproof, and invest in good footwear. Do that, and you'll be comfortable in whatever Ireland's weather throws at you.
