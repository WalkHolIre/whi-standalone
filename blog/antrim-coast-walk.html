Antrim Coast Walk: Northern Ireland's Dramatic Shoreline

There's a moment on the Causeway Coast Way—usually somewhere between Ballintoy and Giant's Causeway, with the basalt cliffs dropping sheer to the Atlantic below and nothing between you and Iceland— where the scale of what you're looking at becomes genuinely difficult to process.

The Antrim coast walk is not subtle. It doesn't ease you in gently or reveal itself slowly. It hits you with drama from the first section and sustains it for the full 53 km between Ballycastle and Portstewart. This is one of the finest coastal walking routes in Europe, and the fact that it's still relatively little known among international walkers is, honestly, their loss and your opportunity.

I've walked this coast in sun, in driving rain, and in the peculiar horizontal mist that Northern Ireland does better than anywhere else. All three versions have been memorable. The one I think about most was a November morning when the basalt columns at the Giant's Causeway were slick with spray and there was nobody else there at all.

The Causeway Coast Way: What You're Walking

The Causeway Coast Way runs 53 km along Northern Ireland's north Antrim coastline, from Ballycastle in the east to Portstewart in the west. Most walkers complete it over 3 to 5 days, covering 12 to 18 km per day depending on the stage and how much time they spend at the landmarks along the way.

It is not a technically demanding walk. The trail is well-marked, the terrain is manageable for walkers of moderate fitness, and the villages along the route are well-served with accommodation and food. What makes it demanding is the exposure — the clifftop sections are genuinely open to North Atlantic weather, and conditions can change quickly. Good waterproofs and solid footwear are essential regardless of the season.

The route divides naturally into three sections.

Ballycastle to Ballintoy — the eastern section — is the wildest and least visited. Clifftop paths, hidden coves, and the dramatic ruins of Kinbane Castle perched on a white limestone headland. This is the section to walk if you want the Antrim coast to yourself.

Ballintoy to the Giant's Causeway – the central section – is where the iconic landmarks concentrate. Carrick-a-Rede, White Park Bay, Dunseverick Castle, and then the basalt columns themselves. This is the section most walkers talk about for years afterwards.

The Giant's Causeway to Portstewart — the western section — passes Dunluce Castle and the towns of Portrush and Portstewart, where the walk ends with the energy of proper seaside towns rather than clifftop solitude.

The Highlights: What to Expect on the Trail

Giant's Causeway

The Giant's Causeway is simultaneously Ireland's most visited natural attraction and one of the most disappointing if you only see it from the visitor centre car park. On foot, from the coastal path, it's a completely different experience.

Walking down into the geometric columns, you're not observing geology — you're inside it. The hexagonal basalt stones, formed 60 million years ago when ancient lava flows cooled and contracted with extraordinary mathematical precision, are as extraordinary underfoot as they are in photographs. The scale is something photographs don't convey.

Our Giant's Causeway guide covers the geological and mythological story of the site in detail. In the context of the Antrim coast walk, allow a minimum of two hours here — three if the weather is good and the light is right.

Carrick-a-Rede Rope Bridge

The rope bridge at Carrick-a-Rede has been crossing the 20-metre gap between the mainland and a small island 30 metres above the Atlantic since the 17th century, when salmon fishermen strung the original across to reach their nets. The modern version is considerably more robust — but crossing it is still an exercise in managing your relationship with exposed heights.

For walkers comfortable with exposure, it's one of those brief experiences — five minutes across, five minutes back — that stays with you disproportionately. For walkers who'd rather not, the coastal path on the mainland side offers similar views without the bridge. Both options are legitimate.

Book your crossing in advance, particularly in summer. Numbers are limited, and it sells out.

Dunluce Castle

Dunluce Castle sits on a basalt headland between Portballintrae and Portrush, separated from the mainland by a narrow chasm that required a drawbridge. The ruined towers and walls of this mediaeval fortress — partially collapsed into the sea during a storm in 1639 — are among the most dramatic ruins in Ireland.

Walking up to Dunluce Castle from the coastal path, with the castle above you and the sea crashing against the headland below, the drama of the location is impossible to miss. This was a real seat of power — the MacDonnell clan's stronghold for two centuries — and the landscape makes that feel entirely believable.

The Dunluce Castle and broader Causeway Coast area are covered in our Antrim Coast guide with context on the history of the area.

White Park Bay

White Park Bay is a long, curving strand of white sand backed by dunes and limestone cliffs — one of the finest beaches on the Irish coast. The path arrives at the eastern end and crosses the bay at low tide on the sand or climbs into the grassland above at high tide. Either way, it's the kind of walking that makes you stop mid-stride and simply look.

At the western end of the bay, the village of Portbraddan — just a handful of cottages — contains Ireland's smallest church, dedicated to Saint Gobban. Worth the short detour.

Dunseverick Castle

Dunseverick Castle doesn't get the attention it deserves. These 6th-century ruins sit on a headland between Ballintoy and the Giant's Causeway, surrounded by sea on three sides and connected to the mainland by a path so narrow that the strategic logic of the location is immediately obvious. One of the oldest fortified sites in Ireland — said to be where one of the routes of the Táin Bó Cúailnge reached the sea.

The ruins are modest by castle standards. The location is not.

Weather and when to go

The Antrim coast is walkable year-round, but the exposed clifftop sections demand respect in poor conditions. Wind can be ferocious; visibility can drop quickly in mist and driving rain.

May, June, and September offer the best combination of stable weather, good light, and manageable crowds at the major landmarks. July and August bring more visitors — particularly to the Giant's Causeway and Carrick-a-Rede — but the walking itself is excellent.

Winter walking is possible for experienced, well-equipped walkers. The coast is dramatically beautiful in low season, and the landmarks are quieter. But the exposed sections demand caution in high winds.

Our guide to managing weather on Irish trails covers the practical safety considerations in detail.

Accommodation along the route

Ballycastle — a proper town at the eastern start point, good for an opening night. Excellent fish and chips.

Ballintoy — small, quiet, and beautiful. A handful of guesthouses and B&Bs. Book early.

Bushmills — the most practical overnight stop near the Giant's Causeway, with the added advantage of being home to the world's oldest licensed distillery. An evening tour of Bushmills Distillery is a very reasonable thing to do after a day's walking.

Portrush — a lively seaside town with a wide range of accommodation, restaurants, and pubs. A livelier end-of-day option than the smaller stops.

Portstewart — the western end of the trail. Quieter than Portrush, equally well-served with accommodation.

We book all accommodation on our Causeway Coast tours — including luggage transfers so your bags move between each stop while you walk.

Combining the Causeway Coast with the Antrim Glens

The Glens of Antrim lie immediately south of the Causeway Coast — nine distinct glacial valleys cutting down from the Antrim plateau to the coast. Walking a combination of the Causeway Coast Way and the Glens gives you two very different versions of Northern Ireland's landscape in a single trip.

Our Antrim Glens and Causeway Coast 8-day tour combines both areas with full logistical support.

Entry requirements for Northern Ireland

Northern Ireland is part of the United Kingdom. If you're travelling from the Republic of Ireland, no border formalities apply — you cross freely. If you're travelling from outside the UK or Ireland, check our Northern Ireland ETA guide for current entry requirements.

Why Walk the Antrim Coast?

The Causeway Coast Way offers something that's increasingly rare: a walking trail of genuine international significance that isn't yet overcrowded. You can walk alongside day-trippers at the Giant's Causeway at noon, then round a headland and find yourself completely alone on a clifftop path with nothing but the Atlantic and your own breathing.

The best walks in Northern Ireland include sections of this coast, but walking the full route from Ballycastle to Portstewart is a different experience from day-walking individual sections. The narrative builds. Each day's walking gives context to the next. By the time you reach the Giant's Causeway on Day 2, you've already walked through enough of the coast's geology and history to understand what you're looking at.

That understanding is what walking does that driving doesn't.
