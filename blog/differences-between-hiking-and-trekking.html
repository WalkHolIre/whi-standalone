Hiking vs Trekking: What's the Difference and Which Is Right for You?

My name is Cliff, and I run Walking Holiday Ireland. People often use "hiking" and "trekking" as if they mean the same thing. They don't — and understanding the difference between hiking and trekking helps you choose the right trip, pack the right gear, and know what to expect on the trail. Whether you're planning a day in the Wicklow Mountains or a week on the Kerry Way, knowing where your walk sits on the hiking vs trekking spectrum makes all the difference.

The short answer: hiking is typically a day walk on established trails, while trekking is a multi-day journey through more remote terrain. But the real picture is more nuanced than that. Here's the full breakdown.

What Is Hiking?

Hiking means walking in the countryside or mountains on paths and trails, usually for a single day. You start in the morning, walk for several hours, and return to your starting point or a nearby village by evening. The trails are generally marked, the terrain is manageable, and you carry a daypack with water, food, and rain gear.

In Ireland, most of what we do is hiking. A walk up Diamond Hill in Connemara, the Spinc Trail at Glendalough, or the cliff path at the Cliffs of Moher — these are all hikes. You're on established paths, the route is measured in hours rather than days, and you sleep in a bed at the end of it.

Hiking covers a wide range of difficulty. A gentle stroll along a riverside path is hiking. So is a steep climb up Carrauntoohil, Ireland's highest mountain at 1,039 metres. What they share is the day-walk format — you head out, walk, and come back.

In Britain and Ireland, you'll also hear the words "rambling," "hill walking," and simply "walking" used alongside hiking. They all describe roughly the same thing — heading out for the day on foot. Hill walking tends to mean higher ground, while rambling suggests a more relaxed pace through countryside. In practice, the terms overlap and locals use whichever feels right.

What Is Trekking?

The trekking meaning goes further. Trekking is a multi-day walking journey, usually through remote or mountainous terrain, where the walk itself is the purpose of the trip. You travel from point to point over several days, often covering significant distances and elevation changes.

Trekking typically involves carrying more gear than a day hike — either in your own backpack or with the help of support services that transfer your luggage between overnight stops. The routes are longer, the terrain can be rougher, and you need a higher level of fitness and preparation.

Walking the Wicklow Way over 5 to 8 days is trekking. So is the Kerry Way or the Dingle Way. You walk 15 to 25 km each day, sleep in different villages, and the journey has a rhythm — wake, walk, arrive, rest, repeat. The experience is fundamentally different from a day hike because you live on the trail.

Internationally, trekking often refers to more challenging routes — the Annapurna Circuit in Nepal, the Camino de Santiago in Spain, or the Tour du Mont Blanc in the Alps. Ireland's treks are generally less extreme in altitude but no less rewarding in terms of scenery and experience.

The word "trekking" itself has an interesting history. It comes from the Afrikaans word "trek," meaning a long, arduous journey — originally used by South African settlers. Today, trekking vs hiking is a distinction walkers make worldwide, and while the exact definitions vary by country, the core idea holds: a trek is longer, harder, and more immersive than a hike.

The Key Differences

The difference between hiking and trekking comes down to several factors.

Duration is the most obvious. Hiking is a day activity — a few hours to a full day. Trekking spans multiple days, sometimes weeks. A hike fits into a holiday; a trek is the holiday.

Distance follows from duration. A day hike might cover 5 to 20 km. A trek covers that distance each day for several days running. The Kerry Way is 215 km — that's not a hike, that's a trek.

Terrain varies too. Hiking trails tend to be well-marked and well-maintained. Trekking routes can include rough ground, river crossings, mountain passes, and sections where navigation skills are needed. In Ireland, most waymarked trails are well maintained, but the mountain sections of routes like the Wicklow Way include boggy ground and exposed ridges.

Gear needs increase with trekking. A day hike needs a daypack, water bottle, waterproof jacket, and good hiking boots. Trekking requires more — a larger pack or luggage transfer service, changes of clothing, and enough supplies for days on the trail. Our packing list covers what you need for multi-day walks in Ireland.

Fitness requirements are higher for trekking. Walking 20 km a day for five or six consecutive days demands a different level of endurance than a single day walk. Your body needs to recover overnight and be ready to go again in the morning. Check our tour grading to find the right level for your fitness.

Hiking and Trekking in Ireland

Ireland offers both in abundance, and one of the things that makes this country special for walkers is that you can combine them easily.

For day hiking, Ireland has hundreds of waymarked trails. The national parks — Wicklow, Connemara, Killarney, the Burren — all have networks of marked trails from easy to challenging. The coastal paths along the Wild Atlantic Way give you some of the finest cliff walking in Europe. And every county has local walks that show you a different side of the landscape.

For trekking, Ireland's long-distance waymarked trails are the draw. The Wicklow Way, Kerry Way, Dingle Way, Beara Way, and Western Way all offer multi-day journeys through beautiful country. These routes are well-marked and pass through villages with accommodation, pubs, and shops — so you get the trekking experience without the wilderness isolation of high-altitude routes.

This is where our self-guided walking holidays fit in. We book your accommodation at each stop, transfer your luggage between villages, and provide detailed route notes. You walk at your own pace with a light daypack, knowing your bag will be waiting at the next guesthouse. It's trekking with the hard parts taken care of.

For walkers who want company and local knowledge, our guided walking holidays pair you with guides who know every path and have stories about every hill.

Gear for Hiking vs Trekking

The gear question is where the hiking vs trekking difference becomes very practical.

For a day hike, you need a daypack of 20 to 30 litres, a water bottle, a packed lunch, a waterproof jacket, and a warm layer. Add a map, a phone with the route downloaded, and sun protection. That's about it. You travel light because you're back at base by evening.

For a trek, the gear list grows. If you're carrying everything yourself, you need a pack of 50 to 65 litres, a sleeping bag (if camping), multiple changes of clothing, food and cooking supplies, and first aid equipment. The total weight can reach 12 to 15 kg — a completely different experience from walking with a light daypack.

This is where self-guided walking holidays in Ireland have a real advantage. Because we transfer your luggage between overnight stops, you walk each day with just a daypack. You get the trekking experience — the multi-day journey, the changing landscape, the sense of distance covered — without the physical burden of a heavy pack. It's the best of both worlds.

Which Is Right for You?

If you're new to walking or short on time, start with hiking. Pick a day walk that matches your fitness — the Green Road at Glendalough for an easy start, or the Spinc Trail for something more demanding. Day hikes let you test your stamina, break in your boots, and get a feel for the landscape.

If you want a deeper experience — the rhythm of walking day after day, the satisfaction of covering ground on foot, the way a trail changes your sense of time — try trekking. A self-guided week on the Dingle Way or the Wicklow Way gives you something a day hike can't match. You arrive in each village tired, hungry, and happy, and the pint of Guinness that evening tastes better than any you've had in your life.

The best trips combine both. Spend a few days hiking different trails from a single base, then set off on a multi-day trek. Ireland is small enough that you can mix and match easily. Many of our guests start with two or three day hikes from a base in Killarney or Kenmare to build confidence, then walk the Kerry Way or Dingle Way for the second half of their holiday.

The best time to visit Ireland for both hiking and trekking is April to October. The Irish weather is changeable year-round, so pack waterproofs regardless of season.

Get Walking

The hiking vs trekking question matters less than the decision to get out and walk. Both give you fresh air, exercise, scenery, and the kind of quiet that's hard to find elsewhere. Ireland is one of the best places in Europe for both — the trails are there, the scenery is spectacular, and the welcome at the end of the day is warm. Get in touch with Walking Holiday Ireland and we'll help you plan a trip that matches exactly what you're looking for — whether that's a day on the hills or a week on the trail.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the main difference between hiking and trekking?

Hiking is typically a day walk on established trails where you return to your starting point by evening. Trekking is a multi-day journey through more remote terrain, covering 15 to 25 km per day over several days or weeks. Trekking requires more fitness, gear, and planning.

Is trekking harder than hiking?

Generally yes. Trekking demands more endurance because you walk for multiple consecutive days, often over rougher terrain with greater elevation changes. However, difficulty depends on the specific route — a challenging day hike up a mountain can be harder than a gentle multi-day trek on flat paths.

Can beginners go trekking in Ireland?

Yes. Ireland's long-distance trails like the Wicklow Way and Dingle Way are well-marked and pass through villages with accommodation and services. Self-guided walking holidays with luggage transfer mean you walk with just a daypack, making multi-day treks accessible to anyone with reasonable fitness.

What gear do I need for a multi-day trek in Ireland?

With a luggage transfer service, you only need a daypack with water, snacks, a waterproof jacket, warm layer, and your phone or map. Your main luggage travels between accommodations. If carrying everything yourself, you need a 50 to 65 litre pack with clothing, sleeping gear, food, and first aid supplies.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a self-guided and a guided walking tour?
On a self-guided tour, you walk independently using our detailed maps, route notes, and GPS app, with your luggage transferred each day and 24/7 support available. A guided tour provides a local expert guide who walks with you, shares local knowledge, stories and history, and ensures you stay on track and safe. Guided tours also include built-in companionship with fellow walkers.
What is the most popular route?
The Dingle Way is our most popular route, closely followed by the Wicklow Way. The Dingle Way offers dramatic Wild Atlantic coastline, ancient history at Slea Head, and charming villages like Annascaul and Dingle town.
Are the trails well-marked?
Ireland's waymarked long-distance trails are generally well-signed. However, some mountain areas have less consistent waymarking, so it is important to carry a paper map and compass as backup. Our route notes highlight any sections that require extra attention.
How much rain should I expect?
Ireland has a famously changeable Atlantic climate - rain is possible at any time of year. The saying goes: If you don't like the weather, wait five minutes. Pack full waterproofs regardless of the season. Many walkers experience some wet weather during their trip, but this is part of the authentic Irish walking experience and rarely spoils the adventure.
What kind of boots should I wear?
Well-fitted, waterproof hiking boots are essential. Begin breaking them in 8-10 weeks before your trip, gradually increasing your walking distances in them. By departure, they should feel familiar and comfortable. Test them in wet and uneven conditions similar to Irish terrain. Many experienced walkers also carry blister treatment just in case.
