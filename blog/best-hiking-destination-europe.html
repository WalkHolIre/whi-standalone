Ireland's Best Hiking Destination in Europe | Why Choose Ireland

Photo: Walking Holiday Ireland

Why Ireland Is Europe's Best Hiking Destination: A Comparison

European hikers have excellent options. The Alps deliver scale and drama that nothing in Ireland matches. The Camino de Santiago offers one of the world's great long-distance walking experiences. The Amalfi Coast has scenery that genuinely belongs in a different category of visual impact. The Dolomites are extraordinary. Scotland is magnificent and wild.

I've walked trails across much of Europe, and I keep returning to Ireland. Not because it's the most famous, or the most photographed. I return because Ireland offers something that most of the continent's famous hiking destinations don't: a combination of waymarked infrastructure, practical logistics, genuine cultural warmth, and consistently walkable terrain that makes it the finest place in Europe for the specific format of walking holiday that most people actually want.

This article makes the case for Ireland directly — by comparing it against the alternatives, honestly.

The Comparison Framework

Most people planning a walking holiday in Europe want roughly the same things:

  • Routes that are clearly marked and don't require expert navigation

  • Accommodation at the end of each day's walking, within reasonable distance

  • Luggage that gets from A to B without the walker carrying it

  • Terrain that's challenging but not technical or dangerous

  • An evening worth having after the day's effort

  • Safety and accessibility as a solo walker or couple

  • The ability to book and plan without specialist knowledge

Against these criteria — not the criteria of a seasoned mountaineer, but the criteria of a serious walker who wants a rewarding multi-day experience — Ireland consistently outperforms every other European destination.

Ireland vs The Alps

The Alps produce some of the most dramatic mountain scenery in the world, and on clear summer days the experience is genuinely unrivalled for scale. But as a walking holidays europe destination, the Alps have significant practical limitations for most walkers.

Weather: Alpine weather is genuinely dangerous. Afternoon thunderstorms in July and August are the norm, not the exception. Mountain rescue operations in the Alps run year-round for a reason. The window of safe walking is concentrated in July and August and requires constant monitoring.

Altitude: Many of the Alps' signature routes require acclimatisation. Walkers unused to altitude above 2,500 metres can find the experience unpleasant. Ireland has no walking above 1,041 metres (Carrauntoohil), and most trails stay well below 600 metres.

Navigation: Alpine trails require competence with maps and weather reading in ways that Irish trails don't. Getting caught on an exposed ridge in deteriorating conditions in the Alps is life-threatening. Getting caught on Croagh Patrick in the same conditions is deeply unpleasant but rarely fatal.

Logistics: Mountain huts (refugios) in the Alps require reservations months in advance for popular routes in high season. The hut system is magnificent but inflexible. Irish B&Bs and guesthouses offer comparable comfort (often considerably more) with far greater flexibility.

What the Alps do better: Scale, dramatic scenery, technical challenge for experienced mountain walkers. If you want to feel genuinely small in a landscape, the Alps deliver.

What Ireland does better: Year-round accessibility, practical infrastructure, safety margin, cultural experience, flexibility.

Ireland vs The Camino de Santiago

The Camino de Santiago is one of the world's great walking experiences. The Frances route (St Jean Pied de Port to Santiago de Compostela, approximately 800 km) is an extraordinary journey and deserves its reputation. But as a walking holiday format, the Camino is a specific kind of experience with specific limitations.

Crowds: The Camino Frances carries hundreds of thousands of walkers annually. In summer, the main albergues (pilgrim hostels) are full by early afternoon. The path itself is visually dominated by other walkers for much of its length — inspiring in its sense of collective purpose, but not remotely solitary.

Accommodation: The albergue system was designed for pilgrims on a budget and provides dormitory sleeping for €15 to 25 per night. For walkers who want a private room, a hot shower, and breakfast — the standard expectation of a walking holiday — the Camino requires booking ahead and often paying significantly more than the base rate.

Single route: The Camino is, essentially, one route. Ireland has more than 40 waymarked long-distance trails covering multiple landscapes and characters.

Season dependency: The Frances in summer is genuinely crowded. The Meseta (the central plateau of Spain) in July is hot, exposed, and monotonous. The autumn Camino is considerably finer, but the window is narrow.

What the Camino does better: The sense of communal pilgrimage, the historical and spiritual depth, the social experience of walking with people from across the world, the infrastructure for walkers in small towns along the route.

What Ireland does better: Privacy, scenic variety, quality of evening experience, flexibility, the self-guided format at a level of comfort that the Camino rarely provides at base prices.

Ireland vs Scotland

Scotland and Ireland are the most natural comparison — geographically adjacent, climatically similar, both offering genuine wild walking in Atlantic landscapes. But for the best hiking holidays europe format — particularly the inn-to-inn, self-guided structure — Ireland has meaningful advantages.

Waymarking: Ireland's network of waymarked trails is more extensive and more consistently maintained than Scotland's. The Kerry Way, Wicklow Way, Dingle Way, Barrow Way, and Causeway Coast are all clearly signed throughout. Scotland's long-distance paths (West Highland Way, Great Glen Way, Cape Wrath Trail) vary considerably in waymarking quality, and some require genuine navigation skills.

Accommodation density: The concentration of B&Bs, guesthouses, and small hotels along Ireland's walking routes means that stages can be walked in the 16 to 22 km range with reliable accommodation at the end. Scotland's remoter routes (Cape Wrath, for example) require wild camping or very long stages between facilities.

Safety: Both are relatively safe walking environments. Scotland's Munro-bagging culture involves genuine mountain risk; Ireland's popular trails stay below the threshold where weather creates life-threatening situations for normally equipped walkers.

Evening experience: This is Ireland's clearest advantage. Scotland's rural pub culture, while excellent, is less consistent and less warm than Ireland's. In Kerry, Dingle, or Wicklow, the evening after a day's walking is reliably excellent. In Scotland, depending on location, it can be very isolated.

What Scotland does better: Wilder, more dramatic, more genuinely remote landscape. The Highlands and islands offer a quality of solitude that Ireland's denser population cannot quite match.

Ireland vs The Amalfi Coast

The Amalfi Coast is genuinely spectacular. The Sentiero degli Dei (Path of the Gods) between Positano and Praiano offers Mediterranean views that have no equivalent anywhere in Ireland or Britain. But as a practical walking holiday destination, the Amalfi has significant limitations.

Heat: The Amalfi coast in July and August is hot and dry at altitudes that most walkers find exhausting. The quality walking season is May and September to October.

Terrain: Many Amalfi routes involve significant stone stair climbing — effective exercise but hard on knees, ankles, and walking boots. The paths are short compared to Ireland's long-distance trails; there's no equivalent of a multi-day coast-to-coast.

Crowds: The Amalfi Coast is one of the most visited stretches of coastline in Europe. Path junctions are often congested. The contrast with the solitude available on Ireland's western trails is stark.

Cost: Accommodation on the Amalfi coast in season is among the most expensive in Europe. Ireland, particularly in the rural west, offers comparable quality at significantly lower cost.

What the Amalfi does better: Scenery, food, Mediterranean warmth, dramatic coastal views, the village-to-village culture.

What Makes Ireland Different: The Specific Advantages

Infrastructure built for walkers

Ireland's network of long-distance waymarked trails was built specifically for self-guided walking holidays. The Kerry Way, Wicklow Way, Dingle Way, Barrow Way, Causeway Coast — all are marked with consistent yellow walking man symbols from start to finish. Luggage transfer operators service every major route, moving bags between overnight stops while walkers carry only day packs.

This infrastructure is why the self-guided format works so well in Ireland. You arrive with your rucksack, your route map, and your accommodation list, and the system carries you.

Year-round accessibility

Ireland has no walking season in the way that Alpine or Mediterranean destinations do. Every WHI trail is walkable from March to November with appropriate gear. The shoulder seasons — April to June and September to October — deliver the finest conditions: long light, lower visitor numbers, and the specific quality that comes from an Irish autumn or Irish spring that is unlike anything the Continent produces.

Safety for solo walkers

Ireland is among the safest solo walking environments in the world. The trails pass through inhabited countryside; there is almost always a farmhouse, a village, or a road within reasonable distance. Phone coverage is generally good. The walking public — other walkers, farmers, local people — is reliably friendly and helpful.

For women walking alone, Ireland is an outlier in European terms. Our women's guide to solo hiking in Ireland covers the practical experience in detail.

The evening

This bears repeating: nowhere in Europe does the end of a walking day as well as Ireland. A good B&B or guesthouse, excellent food, a pub with a fire, and the possibility of trad music — this combination is either absent or considerably less reliable in the Alps, the Camino, the Amalfi, or Scotland.

The Honest Caveat: Weather

Ireland's weather is the one argument that opponents of this case cite, and they're not wrong. It rains in Ireland. Persistently, unpredictably, and sometimes heavily. The Atlantic climate means that clear forecasts can turn and sunshine can follow grey starts within the same day.

The counterarguments are: the right waterproofs make rain non-fatal; soft days (warm light rain) are often beautiful; and the trails empty of other walkers in wet conditions, giving you the landscape to yourself. Our rain walking guide covers both how to walk in it and what to do when you really don't want to.

Weather is not a reason not to walk Ireland. It's a reason to pack properly.
