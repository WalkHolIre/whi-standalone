How to Plan a Walking Holiday in Ireland: The Complete Guide

My name is Cliff, and I run Walking Holiday Ireland. After twenty years of organising walking holidays for visitors from around the world, I know that the planning stage is where most people feel unsure. Which trail should I choose? When should I go? What do I need to pack? How fit do I need to be? This guide answers all those questions and walks you through every step of planning a walking holiday ireland that matches your interests, fitness, and budget.

Ireland is one of the best places in Europe for a walking holiday. The landscape is varied, the trails are well maintained, the distances between villages are manageable, and the welcome you receive in local B&Bs and pubs is genuine. Whether you want a week-long coastal trek or a few days in the mountains, this guide covers everything you need to know before you book.

Choose Your Trail

Ireland has dozens of waymarked trails, but a few stand out for walking holidays. The Kerry Way is the most popular long-distance route, circling the Iveragh Peninsula through mountains, coastline, and traditional villages over five to eight days. The Wicklow Way runs through the mountains south of Dublin and is an excellent choice if you want to combine walking with time in the capital. The Dingle Way offers spectacular coastal scenery on one of Europe's most beautiful peninsulas.

The Burren Way crosses the extraordinary limestone landscape of County Clare, while the Beara Way follows the wild Cork-Kerry border through scenery that rivals the more famous routes with far fewer walkers. Each trail has its own character, and our tour grading helps you match your fitness level to the right route.

Decide on Guided or Self-Guided

This is one of the first decisions to make when planning a hiking vacation ireland. A guided walking holiday means you walk with a local guide who knows every trail, every shortcut, every story about the landscape. You don't need to navigate, and you benefit from local knowledge that no guidebook can match. Guided trips are social — you'll walk with a small group and share meals and evenings together.

A self-guided walking holiday gives you complete freedom. We book your accommodation, transfer your luggage between stops, and provide detailed route notes and maps. You walk at your own pace, start when you want, stop when you want, and have your evenings to yourself. Self-guided is our most popular option because it combines the independence of walking alone with the comfort of having all the logistics handled.

Pick the Right Time of Year

The best time to visit Ireland for walking is April to October. Each month has its advantages. April and May bring longer days, wildflowers, and quieter trails. June and July are the warmest months, with daylight lasting until after ten in the evening — perfect for long days on the trail. August is busy but still excellent. September and early October offer warm colours, fewer visitors, and often the most settled weather of the year.

The Irish weather is mild but changeable. Summer temperatures range from 15 to 20 degrees Celsius, occasionally warmer. Rain can arrive at any time, but it rarely lasts all day. The key is to be prepared for all conditions, which brings us to packing.

Pack Smart

Good gear makes a walking holiday comfortable. Poor gear makes it miserable. The single most important item is a pair of well-fitted, broken-in hiking boots with ankle support and a waterproof membrane. Break them in on several practice walks before your trip — new boots on day one are a recipe for blisters.

A breathable waterproof jacket is essential. In Ireland, you will need it at some point regardless of the forecast. Pack layers rather than heavy clothing — a moisture-wicking base layer, a lightweight fleece, and your waterproof shell will cover most conditions. Our packing list covers everything in detail, from socks to sunscreen.

On a self-guided holiday, your main luggage is transferred between accommodations, so you only carry a daypack with water, lunch, rain gear, and a spare layer. This makes the walking much more enjoyable than carrying everything on your back.

Get Your Fitness Ready

You don't need to be an athlete to enjoy a walking holiday in ireland, but some preparation makes the experience much more enjoyable. Most of our trails involve walking 15 to 25 km per day over mixed terrain — paths, roads, tracks, and sometimes rough mountain ground. A reasonable level of fitness means you'll enjoy the scenery instead of just surviving the distance.

The best preparation is walking. In the weeks before your trip, build up your distance gradually. Start with shorter walks of 8 to 10 km and increase to 15 to 20 km over three to four weeks. Walk on hills if you can, and wear the boots and daypack you'll be using on your holiday. This gets your body used to the specific demands of trail walking and helps you identify any gear problems before you arrive.

If you're unsure which grade suits you, our tour grading system rates every trail from easy to challenging with clear descriptions of daily distances, elevation, and terrain. We're always happy to advise by email or phone if you're not sure which route is right for you.

Book Accommodation

On our walking holidays, we handle accommodation for you. We book you into quality B&Bs, guesthouses, and small hotels along each route. Irish B&Bs are one of the highlights of any trip — the hospitality is warm and personal, the breakfasts are legendary, and your hosts are a wealth of local knowledge about the area.

If you're planning independently, book accommodation well in advance for the summer months, particularly in popular areas like Killarney, Dingle, and Doolin. Many B&Bs along walking trails fill up early. We recommend booking at least three to four months ahead for June to August travel.

Understand the Costs

A walking holiday ireland is good value compared to many European destinations. Our self-guided packages typically include accommodation with breakfast, luggage transfers, route notes, maps, and local support. Dinner, lunch, and transport to and from the trailhead are usually extra. Budget around 30 to 50 euros per day for meals on top of your package price.

Getting to Ireland is straightforward. Dublin, Cork, Shannon, and Kerry airports all receive international flights. Irish Rail and Bus Eireann connect major towns, and we can arrange transfers from airports to your starting point. Car hire is another option, though on a walking holiday your vehicle stays at your accommodation while you walk — it's worth considering whether you actually need one.

Plan Your Daily Walking

On a typical day of a walking holiday, you'll walk between four and seven hours depending on the route and your pace. Most walkers start between eight and nine in the morning after a full Irish breakfast. You carry lunch in your daypack — sandwiches from your B&B or supplies picked up at a village shop. The afternoon is spent walking, with the day usually finishing between three and five in the evening at your next accommodation.

Evenings are yours to enjoy. Many of our routes pass through villages with excellent pubs, restaurants, and traditional music sessions. A pint of Guinness and a plate of seafood chowder after a day's walking is one of life's simple pleasures, and it's one of the things that makes hiking in ireland different from walking in more remote destinations.

What to Expect on the Trail

Irish walking trails are generally well waymarked with yellow arrows and wooden posts. The terrain varies from smooth paths and quiet country roads to rough mountain tracks and boggy ground. Stiles, gates, and stepping stones are common. The walking is rarely technical, but it does require attention to footing, especially on wet days.

You'll share the trails with other walkers, local farmers, and sheep. The Irish countryside is largely working farmland, so you'll pass through fields, farmyards, and along field boundaries. Gates should be left as you find them — open if open, closed if closed. Dogs should be kept on leads near livestock.

Mobile phone coverage is good in most areas but can be patchy in mountain valleys and remote coastal sections. Our route notes include emergency contact numbers and the locations of the nearest services along each section. On a self-guided holiday, you also have a local contact number for assistance if you need it.

Book Early and Look Forward to It

The best walking holidays book out months in advance, particularly for the peak season from June to August. If you have specific dates in mind, booking three to six months ahead gives you the best choice of accommodation and route options. Shoulder season months — April, May, September, and October — offer excellent walking with more availability and often lower prices.

If you're not sure where to start, get in touch — after twenty years of planning walking holidays, I can match you with the perfect trail based on your experience, fitness, and what you want from your time in Ireland.
