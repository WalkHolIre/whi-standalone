Ireland Hiking Trails: A Region-by-Region Guide to the Best Routes

My name is Cliff, and I run Walking Holiday Ireland. One of the things I love most about this country is that you're never far from a great walk. Ireland has over 40 waymarked long-distance trails and hundreds of shorter routes, spread across landscapes that change dramatically from one county to the next. Coastal cliffs in Kerry look nothing like the limestone plateau of the Burren, and the granite mountains of Wicklow are a world away from the green river valleys of the southeast. This guide breaks down the ireland hiking trails region by region, so you can find the route that matches where you want to go and what you want to see.

Whether you're planning a multi-day walking holiday or looking for day walks to build into a broader trip around Ireland, this guide covers the best options in every part of the country. I've walked all of these trails myself, most of them many times over, and I know what makes each one worth your time.

Southwest Ireland: Kerry and Cork

The southwest is where most people start when exploring ireland hiking trails, and for good reason. This is where Ireland's most famous long-distance routes are concentrated, along the wild peninsulas that reach into the Atlantic.

The Kerry Way is the flagship — 215 km circling the Iveragh Peninsula through Killarney National Park, the Black Valley, Cahersiveen, Waterville, Sneem, and Kenmare. It's the most popular walking trail in Ireland and the one I recommend most often to first-time visitors. The variety is extraordinary — ancient oakwoods, mountain passes, remote Atlantic coastline, and traditional villages with music in the pubs at night.

The Dingle Way circles the neighbouring Dingle Peninsula over 179 km. The scenery is more dramatic and compact, with the Connor Pass crossing, the Slea Head cliffs, and views to the Blasket Islands. The peninsula is an Irish-speaking area with a strong cultural identity that gives the walking a distinctive character.

The Beara Way follows the Cork-Kerry border around the Beara Peninsula — 196 km of wild, unspoiled coastal landscape with far fewer walkers than the more famous routes. The Healy Pass crossing and the stretch around Dursey Island are among the finest walking anywhere in Ireland. If you want solitude with your scenery, the Beara is where to find it.

The West: Clare, Galway, and Connemara

The west coast offers a completely different character. The Burren Way crosses the extraordinary limestone karst of County Clare — a landscape unlike anything else in Europe. Ancient dolmens, ring forts, and the Cliffs of Moher are all part of the route. In May and June, the grey limestone comes alive with wildflowers, including rare species found almost nowhere else on the continent.

Connemara in County Galway is one of Ireland's wildest regions. The Twelve Bens mountain range and the coastal paths along the Sky Road offer dramatic hiking for experienced walkers. Diamond Hill in Connemara National Park is an excellent shorter option — a well-maintained trail with panoramic views over bog, mountain, and Atlantic coastline that takes about two to three hours.

The Western Way runs through Galway and Mayo, connecting Connemara to Westport through some of the most remote and beautiful countryside in Ireland. It's less well known internationally than the Kerry or Wicklow trails, which means quieter paths and a genuine sense of discovery.

East Coast: Wicklow and Dublin

The Wicklow Way is Ireland's oldest waymarked trail, established in 1980, and it starts right on the edge of Dublin. The 127 km route runs south through the Wicklow Mountains, past Glendalough's monastic ruins, through glacial valleys and heather-covered ridges. It's the most convenient trail for anyone flying into Dublin, and the contrast between the city and the mountain wilderness just an hour away is remarkable.

For day walks near Dublin, the Howth Cliff Walk offers a spectacular coastal circuit within the city limits, and the walks around Glendalough in the Wicklow Mountains give you mountain scenery, ancient history, and lakeside paths all in one visit. These are among the most accessible hiking trails ireland has to offer for visitors with limited time.

The Southeast: River Valleys and Quiet Trails

The southeast is Ireland's best-kept walking secret. The Barrow Way follows the River Barrow through Counties Kildare, Laois, and Carlow along old towpaths — flat, peaceful walking through farmland, riverside villages, and ancient woodland. It's perfect for walkers who prefer gentle terrain and quiet surroundings to mountain drama.

The South Leinster Way connects Kildare to Carrick-on-Suir through the Blackstairs Mountains, offering a mix of mountain and lowland walking. The area around Borris and Graiguenamanagh is particularly beautiful, where the river flows through a wooded valley overlooked by a 12th-century abbey. These are trails where you'll meet more locals than tourists.

The Midlands and Northwest: Quiet Country and Wild Coasts

Ireland's midlands are often overlooked by walkers, but they have a charm of their own. The Slieve Bloom Way in Counties Laois and Offaly circles a mountain range that rises from the flat central plain — a 77 km loop through blanket bog, conifer forest, and open ridges with views across half the country on a clear day. You won't meet another walker for hours.

Further north, County Donegal has some of the most spectacular and least-visited coastline in Ireland. The Slieve League cliffs are among the highest sea cliffs in Europe, and the walking trails along the northern coast of the peninsula feel genuinely wild and remote. Donegal is where the ireland hiking trails feel most like an adventure.

Northern Ireland: Mountains and Coastline

The Mourne Mountains in County Down are among the most dramatic walking landscapes on the island. Slieve Donard, at 850 metres, is the highest peak in Northern Ireland, and the range offers everything from gentle valley walks to challenging ridge traverses. The Mourne Wall trek — following the dry stone wall across the summits — is one of the finest mountain walks in Ireland.

The Causeway Coast Way runs 53 km along the Antrim coast, past the Giant's Causeway, Carrick-a-Rede rope bridge, and some of the most striking sea cliffs in the British Isles. The nine Glens of Antrim that descend toward the coast add waterfall walks, ancient woodland, and a sense of remoteness that surprises visitors expecting a small country.

Choosing the Right Trail for You

With so many ireland hiking trails to choose from, the right route depends on your fitness, your interests, and how much time you have.

For first-time visitors wanting a multi-day walk, I usually recommend the Kerry Way or the Wicklow Way. Both are well waymarked, have excellent accommodation along the route, and deliver consistently outstanding scenery. The Kerry Way gives you the full west-of-Ireland experience — mountains, coast, and traditional culture. The Wicklow Way is ideal if you're combining walking with time in Dublin.

For experienced walkers seeking a challenge, the Beara Way and the Dingle Way both offer tougher terrain with more elevation gain. The Mourne Mountains are excellent for anyone who wants mountain walking without the commitment of a multi-day route.

For gentle walking and quieter trails, the Barrow Way and the southeast routes offer flat or rolling terrain through peaceful countryside. These are underrated gems that deserve more attention. The Slieve Bloom Way is another excellent option for walkers who want solitude and open mountain scenery without extreme elevation.

Our tour grading rates every trail from easy to challenging, so you can match the route to your fitness level with confidence. If you're not sure, just ask — helping people choose the right trail is something I do every day.

Planning Your Walking Holiday

The best time to visit Ireland for walking is April to October. May and September are my favourite months — warm enough for comfortable hiking, with longer days and fewer visitors than peak summer. The Irish weather is mild but changeable year-round, so a waterproof jacket belongs in your daypack regardless of the forecast.

Good hiking boots are the single most important piece of gear. Break them in before you arrive. Our packing list covers everything else you need.

On our self-guided walking holidays, we handle all the logistics — accommodation, luggage transfers between stops, detailed route notes, and local support. You walk at your own pace with complete freedom, knowing that everything is taken care of. If you prefer company on the trail, our guided walking holidays pair you with local guides who know every path, every viewpoint, and every story along the way.

One of the things that surprises visitors most about walking trails ireland is how varied the landscape is for such a small country. You can walk coastal cliffs one day, mountain ridges the next, and riverside paths the day after that — all within a few hours' drive. That variety is what keeps people coming back year after year, and it's what makes Ireland one of the finest walking destinations in Europe. Pick a region, pick a route, and come see for yourself.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are the trails well-marked?
Ireland's waymarked long-distance trails are generally well-signed. However, some mountain areas have less consistent waymarking, so it is important to carry a paper map and compass as backup. Our route notes highlight any sections that require extra attention.
How much rain should I expect?
Ireland has a famously changeable Atlantic climate - rain is possible at any time of year. The saying goes: If you don't like the weather, wait five minutes. Pack full waterproofs regardless of the season. Many walkers experience some wet weather during their trip, but this is part of the authentic Irish walking experience and rarely spoils the adventure.
What kind of boots should I wear?
Well-fitted, waterproof hiking boots are essential. Begin breaking them in 8-10 weeks before your trip, gradually increasing your walking distances in them. By departure, they should feel familiar and comfortable. Test them in wet and uneven conditions similar to Irish terrain. Many experienced walkers also carry blister treatment just in case.
What are the most essential items to pack for a walking holiday in Ireland?
The most important items are: a quality waterproof jacket and trousers (essential in Irish weather), well-fitted and broken-in hiking boots, merino wool or synthetic base layers (avoid cotton), a comfortable daypack, paper maps and compass, a GPS device or smartphone with offline maps, sun protection, and a fully charged power bank. Trekking poles are optional but helpful for longer descents.
What is the most popular route?
The Dingle Way is our most popular route, closely followed by the Wicklow Way. The Dingle Way offers dramatic Wild Atlantic coastline, ancient history at Slea Head, and charming villages like Annascaul and Dingle town.
