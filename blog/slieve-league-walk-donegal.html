Slieve League Cliffs Walk: Europe's Highest Sea Cliffs

Slieve League Walk: A Guide to Europe's Highest Sea Cliffs

There's a moment about an hour into the Slieve League walk when you round a bend and the cliffs appear before you in their full, impossible scale. They're not rising gradually — they're just there, a wall of stone dropping straight into the Atlantic Ocean.

At 601 metres at their highest point, the Slieve League Cliffs are the tallest sea cliffs in Europe. Taller than Croagh Patrick. Three times the height of the Cliffs of Moher. And somehow known by only a fraction of the walkers who visit Ireland each year.

This is one of the country's great under-visited places. The Cliffs of Moher have the visitor centre and the tour buses; Slieve League has the walk, the solitude, and a scale that makes Moher look modest. If you're choosing between them — come here.

The Cliffs: A Quick Orientation

Sliabh Liag — the Irish name for Slieve League — translates roughly as "Mountain of Slabs." The cliffs form the southern face of a mountain in southwest Donegal, County Donegal, on Ireland's Wild Atlantic Way. They were formed over hundreds of millions of years, the rock strata clearly visible in the cliff faces: ancient Silurian sandstone, quartzite, and shale laid down in horizontal bands and then tilted by geological forces into near-vertical positions.

The cliffs are not just high — they're continuous. Unlike most dramatic cliff scenery, there's no sense of the ground levelling off. From the clifftop, the drop goes straight down 601 metres to the water. On a still day, you can hear the Atlantic far below. On a rough day, you can feel it through the rock.

The statistics tell one story. Standing on the summit ridge tells another.

The Routes: What You're Choosing Between

There are two main approaches to the Slieve League Cliffs walk, and the choice between them determines whether you have a short, spectacular experience or a full mountain day.

Route 1: The Lower Car Park to Bunglass (Easy viewpoint access)

Distance: 2 km return | Time: 45 to 60 minutes | Difficulty: Easy

From the visitor centre and lower car park near Teelin village, a short walk leads to the Bunglass viewpoint — arguably the finest single viewpoint of the Slieve League Cliffs and one of the most dramatic in Ireland. This route is accessible to all walkers and gives a true sense of the cliffs' scale without requiring mountain fitness.

The path climbs steadily, past the ruins of an old signal tower, to a clifftop platform from which the full face of Slieve League is visible. This is where photographs are taken. This is where people fall silent.

Many walkers do this section and consider it complete. It is — as a viewpoint experience.

Route 2: The Full Ridge Walk via One Man's Pass (Mountain route)

Distance: 9 to 12 km (depending on start and return route) | Time: 4 to 6 hours | Difficulty: Moderate to Challenging

This is the walk that people talk about for years. From Bunglass, the route continues along the clifftop ridge — one of the finest ridge walks in Ireland — eventually crossing the notorious One Man's Pass and ascending to the summit of Slieve League itself.

The ridge walking above the cliffs is extraordinary. The path follows the cliff edge with the Atlantic far below on one side and the rolling Donegal uplands on the other. The exposure is real, and the views compound at every turn — west to the Atlantic horizon, east across Donegal bay, north to the Donegal highlands.

The return can be made by the same route, or experienced walkers can continue over the summit and descend by alternative routes back to Teelin.

One Man's Pass: The Section People Ask About

One Man's Pass is a narrow ridge section of the Slieve League walk that gets its name from its width — at its narrowest, the path is genuinely tight, with significant drops on both sides. It's the section that people research before they come and the section they talk about afterwards.

The honest assessment: One Man's Pass is not technically difficult, but it demands composure. The path is secure underfoot in dry conditions. The challenge is psychological rather than technical — the exposure is real, and if you're uncomfortable with heights, the experience will be stressful rather than exhilarating.

In wet or windy conditions, One Man's Pass becomes genuinely serious. The rock is slippery when wet, the wind at this elevation can be significant, and the consequences of a slip are severe. Check the weather carefully before attempting the full ridge route.

For the majority of regular walkers in good conditions: One Man's Pass is manageable and the reward is one of the great walking experiences in Ireland.

For walkers who are uncomfortable with exposure or heights: The Bunglass viewpoint delivers most of the visual drama without the exposed ridge. There is no shame in stopping there — it is magnificent.

The Cliffs of Moher are easier, more accessible, and better known. Slieve League is more dramatic, more remote, and more rewarding for walkers. If you can do both — do both. If you have to choose and you're a walker — choose Slieve League.

Practical Information

Getting there

Slieve League is in southwest Donegal, accessed via Carrick village (approximately 25 km from Donegal Town). From Carrick, follow signs for Bunglass. The road narrows significantly — drive slowly and pull in to passing places for oncoming vehicles.

The Slieve League Cliffs Visitor Centre at the lower car park provides parking, toilets, and basic facilities. A shuttle bus service operates from the lower car park to the upper viewpoint area during peak season — useful for those who want the viewpoint experience without the uphill walk.

There is no public transport to the cliffs — a car is essential.

When to go

May to September gives the best walking conditions. The long daylight hours in June and July allow for unhurried exploration; the summit ridge walk needs at least 5 hours with comfortable margins.

Avoid the ridge route in strong winds — the Donegal coast is exposed to Atlantic weather systems and wind speeds at cliff-edge elevation can be significant even on days that feel calm in the valleys. Check the Met Éireann forecast specifically for the Donegal coast and be prepared to adjust plans.

Winter visits are possible for the Bunglass viewpoint but the full ridge route should only be attempted by experienced mountain walkers with appropriate gear and navigation skills. Short daylight and unpredictable weather make planning margins tight.

What to wear and bring

Good waterproofs are essential — Donegal weather is famously changeable. Our layering guide for hiking covers the right approach.

For the full ridge route: waterproof jacket and trousers, walking boots with ankle support and grip (not trail runners), at least 2 litres of water, food, a map, and a charged phone. How to prepare for a hike covers the essentials in detail.

For the Bunglass viewpoint only: comfortable walking shoes and a windproof layer are sufficient, though full waterproofs are always sensible in Donegal.

Fitness requirements

The Bunglass viewpoint walk is suitable for anyone with basic mobility. The full ridge route requires solid walking fitness — the equivalent of being comfortable with a full day's walking at moderate pace. Our tour grading guide explains the fitness levels in practical terms.

Slieve League as Part of a Donegal Walking Holiday

The cliffs don't exist in isolation. Southwest Donegal is one of Ireland's most extraordinary walking landscapes, and Slieve League is the centrepiece rather than the whole picture.

Hiking in Donegal covers the broader walking options across the county — from the Blue Stack Mountains to the Inishowen Peninsula. The Donegal hiking experience article gives the fuller picture of what makes this county so compelling.

Slieve League sits on the Wild Atlantic Way, Ireland's long-distance coastal route. Walkers who combine Slieve League with the Antrim Coast to the north — completing both ends of Ireland's Atlantic walking — experience something very complete.

Frequently Asked Questions

How high are the Slieve League Cliffs? 601 metres at their highest point — the tallest sea cliffs in Europe, nearly three times the height of the Cliffs of Moher.

Are the Slieve League Cliffs dangerous? The Bunglass viewpoint is accessible and safe for most walkers. The full ridge route, particularly One Man's Pass, involves genuine exposure. In wet or windy conditions, the exposed sections become serious. Check weather carefully and turn back if conditions deteriorate.

Is One Man's Pass really that scary? It's exposed but not technically difficult in good conditions. Most regular walkers find it manageable. If you're uncomfortable with heights, it will feel stressful. The Bunglass viewpoint is magnificent without it.

How long does the Slieve League walk take? The Bunglass viewpoint: 45 to 60 minutes return. The full ridge route via One Man's Pass: 4 to 6 hours. Build in extra time for stops — you will stop.

Can I visit without doing the full walk? Absolutely. The Bunglass viewpoint is one of the finest coastal views in Ireland and requires only a gentle 2 km walk. Many visitors do just this and leave very satisfied.
