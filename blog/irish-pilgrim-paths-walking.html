Irish Pilgrim Paths: A Walker's Guide to Sacred Routes

Photo: Walking Holiday Ireland

There’s something about walking the same ground that pilgrims have walked for 1,500 years that changes how you see a landscape. Ireland’s pilgrim paths are among Europe’s oldest continuous walking routes, worn into being by countless feet seeking spiritual connection, healing, or simply the transformative power of a long walk. What draws me to these trails — and what I think draws so many of you — isn’t necessarily about faith. It’s about walking with history. It’s about following ancient footsteps through some of Ireland’s most stunning terrain, feeling the weight of centuries beneath your boots, and discovering why these paths have endured.

The thing is, Irish pilgrim paths walking isn’t about quaint heritage strolls. These are proper walks. Some are challenging mountain ascents. Others traverse remote bog and forest. All of them carry something intangible — a sense of purpose, of following a thread that connects you to countless others who’ve walked this way before.

Croagh Patrick: Ireland’s Holy Mountain

Croagh Patrick dominates the Mayo landscape like nothing else I’ve encountered in Ireland. This 764-metre mountain rising from Clew Bay isn’t just any pilgrim destination — it’s Ireland’s most sacred site, and on Reek Sunday (the last Sunday in July), roughly 30,000 people ascend it in a single day. It’s extraordinary to witness, though I’d recommend visiting outside that peak if you want a more contemplative experience.

The path begins at Murrisk and gains considerable height over less than 4 kilometres. The lower sections wind through moorland, but things get serious quickly. The upper reaches involve scree scrambling — loose stone that requires real focus. The final stretch to the white-washed church at the summit is steep and exposed. Most walkers take two to three hours to reach the top.

What makes it worthwhile is the view. From the summit on a clear day, Clew Bay spreads beneath you studded with islands, and you can see across three counties. The spiritual dimension is palpable — pilgrims leave stones, photographs, prayers. You don’t need to be religious to be moved by it.

The descent is harder on the legs than the ascent, so allow time and consider trekking poles. Weather changes rapidly here, so go prepared with waterproofs even if it looks settled.

Cosán na Naomh: The Saints’ Road

If Croagh Patrick is Ireland’s most famous pilgrim mountain, Cosán na Naomh — the Saints’ Road — is the country’s most significant long-distance pilgrimage trail. This ancient route runs roughly 35 kilometres from the Dingle Peninsula across to Mount Brandon, following a path that pilgrims have walked for over a thousand years. It’s a serious undertaking, best walked over two or three days.

The landscape along Cosán na Naomh is extraordinary. You’re moving through Kerry geology alongside archaeological sites that mark the route’s antiquity. Early Christian hermitages dot the landscape. Ogham stones — ancient carved markers — stand as signposts from another era.

The path demands fitness and good hill-walking experience. It rises steeply in sections and crosses high moorland exposed to Atlantic weather. But this remoteness is precisely why it matters. You’re walking through genuine wilderness, not manicured tourist routes. The solitude is profound. There are sections where you might not see another soul all day, and that silence — that sense of walking between the physical and spiritual worlds — is what pilgrims have always sought.

Mount Brandon itself, at 952 metres, is Ireland’s second highest peak. The views from the summit extend across Dingle Bay to the Skellig Islands and beyond. This is a walk for people who want challenge and meaning in equal measure.

St Kevin’s Way: Monastic Wicklow

St Kevin’s Way is a brilliant introduction to Irish pilgrim paths if you’re newer to serious walking. This 30-kilometre route runs from the village of Hollywood in County Wicklow south to Glendalough, following ancient tracks that medieval pilgrims used to reach St Kevin’s monastic settlement.

Glendalough itself is extraordinary. Two lakes nestle in a glacial valley surrounded by cliff-faced mountains. The monastic city — the round tower, churches and settlement remains — sits between them, hidden until you’re almost upon it. Walking toward it along St Kevin’s Way, you understand why early monks chose this place. It feels removed from the world.

The walk itself is accessible compared to the mountain pilgrimage trails. It’s waymarked throughout, mostly following forest tracks, country roads and open hillside. The terrain is moderate — real hills but nothing approaching Croagh Patrick’s scree. Most walkers cover it comfortably in two days, staying overnight in Glendalough itself.

What I love about St Kevin’s Way is how it brings you into the Wicklow landscape gradually. You emerge from forest and moorland to find yourself looking down into the valley where nothing seems to have changed in a thousand years. The round tower catches the light. The water reflects mountains. It’s a powerful arrival, earned through proper walking.

St Finbarr’s Pilgrim Path: Cork’s Forest Walk

Cork’s St Finbarr’s Pilgrim Path is less well-known than the northern and western routes, which is partly why I recommend it. This roughly 9-kilometre path runs from Drimoleague westward to Gougane Barra, a remote mountain lake where St Finbarr founded a monastery on an island in the 6th century.

The walk is beautiful — forest trails, mountain streams, and proper Munster scenery. It’s less demanding than the longer pilgrim routes, making it accessible for a wider range of walkers, though the terrain can be muddy and exposed in sections.

What matters about Gougane Barra is the arrival. The lake sits in a corrie, surrounded by steep mountainsides, with the island church visible across the water. It’s a place of genuine power. The sense that this valley has been a focus of spiritual practice for 1,400 years settles into you.

Slí Cholmcille: The Columban Way

For those seeking serious distance and real remoteness, Slí Cholmcille in Donegal is unmatched. This 65-kilometre trail traces the path of St Columba from Glenveagh in the Donegal highlands toward the coast, moving through some of Ireland’s wildest landscape.

The Donegal landscape is brutal and beautiful in equal measure. You’re walking through bog, moorland and mountain terrain that feels genuinely untamed. The route is less developed than southern pilgrim paths — waymarking is intermittent and navigation requires attention. Weather changes rapidly and exposure is real. Most walkers tackle this as a four to five day undertaking.

If you walk Slí Cholmcille, you understand why St Columba sought solitude. The landscape itself is a kind of spiritual practice — demanding, unforgiving, utterly honest.

Planning Your Pilgrimage

These walks demand fitness appropriate to mountain or moorland walking. They require proper gear — waterproof jacket and trousers, decent boots, navigation tools. Irish weather is changeable, and these trails often sit exposed to Atlantic systems.

Most major routes are waymarked, though standards vary. Some sections of less-travelled routes require map and compass skills. The pilgrim season runs May through September, with peak conditions from June through August. For Reek Sunday on Croagh Patrick, arrive in late July.

Each route has different demands. Croagh Patrick is a day walk. St Kevin’s Way works as an overnight trip. Cosán na Naomh requires two to three days and serious fitness. Slí Cholmcille is a four to five day expedition for experienced walkers.

Why These Paths Still Call

You don’t need to be religious. You don’t need to believe in miracles. What you do need is openness to the possibility that walking can be transformative. That landscape carries memory. That following ancient footsteps connects you to something larger than yourself.

Whether you’re drawn to the spiritual dimension or simply to the landscape and solitude, Irish pilgrim paths offer something you won’t find on most hiking trails. They’ve been waiting for you for a very long time.

Frequently Asked Questions

What fitness level do I need for a walking holiday in Ireland?
It depends on the type of tour. Walking holidays on gentle routes (10-16 km per day) suit anyone who is reasonably active. Hiking holidays (14-20 km per day with hills) need moderate fitness — regular walking or light exercise is enough preparation. Trekking-level routes (20+ km per day over challenging terrain) require good cardiovascular fitness and strong legs. All our self-guided tours include detailed route notes so you can walk at your own pace.
Are the trails well-marked?
Ireland's waymarked long-distance trails are generally well-signed. However, some mountain areas have less consistent waymarking, so it is important to carry a paper map and compass as backup. Our route notes highlight any sections that require extra attention.
What are the most essential items to pack for a walking holiday in Ireland?
The most important items are: a quality waterproof jacket and trousers (essential in Irish weather), well-fitted and broken-in hiking boots, merino wool or synthetic base layers (avoid cotton), a comfortable daypack, paper maps and compass, a GPS device or smartphone with offline maps, sun protection, and a fully charged power bank. Trekking poles are optional but helpful for longer descents.
