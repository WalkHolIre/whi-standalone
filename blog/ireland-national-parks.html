Ireland National Parks: 6 Wild Places to Walk

Ireland has six national parks. That's it — just six. But each one packs a punch that bigger countries would envy. Mountains, lakes, bogs, coastlines, ancient oak forests, and red deer herds— all packed into protected land you can walk through at your own pace.

My name is Cliff, and I run Walking Holiday Ireland. I've walked every one of these parks more times than I can count. Some days it's a gentle loop through oak forest. Other days it's a mountain climb in sideways rain with a view that makes it all worth it. That's what the national parks in Ireland give you — range, beauty, and the kind of quiet that's difficult to find in the modern world.

Here's my guide to all six Irish national parks, with the walks that make each one special.

Killarney National Park — Kerry

Killarney National Park is Ireland's oldest and most famous, and it earns every bit of its reputation. Set in County Kerry, it covers 26,000 acres of mountains, lakes, and the last native red deer herd in Ireland. If you visit only one national park in Ireland, most people would say it should be this one.

The park holds three big lakes — Lough Leane, Muckross Lake, and Upper Lake — ringed by oak and yew woodland that's been growing here since the last ice age. Muckross House sits at the heart of it, with gardens that are worth the visit on their own.

For walkers, Killarney is impossible to beat. The trails around Muckross Lake are flat and easy — perfect for families or anyone who wants a gentle day out. For more of a test, head up to Torc Waterfall and keep climbing to the top for views over the whole park. The Kerry Way passes right through, and it's one of our most popular routes.

The park is also a dark sky reserve, which means clear nights bring some of the best stargazing in Europe. Come for the walk, stay for the stars.

Wicklow Mountains National Park — Wicklow

Just an hour south of Dublin, the Wicklow Mountains feel a million miles from the city. Rolling heather-covered peaks, blanket bogs, and deep glacial valleys make up Ireland's second-largest national park.

The centrepiece is Glendalough—a glacial valley with two lakes and the ruins of a 6th-century monastery founded by St. Kevin. It's one of the most photographed spots in Ireland, and for good reason.

The Wicklow Way — Ireland's first long-distance walking trail — runs through the park. It takes you over mountain passes and through pine forests, with views that open up at every turn. We run self-guided trips on the Wicklow Way that suit beginners and experienced walkers alike.

Beyond the Way, the park has shorter loops for day walks. The Spinc trail at Glendalough is a classic — a boardwalk climb that takes you high above the Upper Lake with views you won't forget.

Connemara National Park — Galway

Connemara is wild Ireland at its finest. Among all the Irish national parks, this one feels the most untamed. The park sits in the west of County Galway, backed by the Twelve Bens mountain range and looking out over bog, lake, and Atlantic coastline.

Diamond Hill is the main walking draw — a 7 km loop that climbs to 442 metres with panoramic views over the coast, the islands, and the Twelve Bens. On a clear day, you can see all the way to the Aran Islands. We've written a full guide to the Diamond Hill walk.

The park is also the gateway to some of the best hikes in Connemara — the Maumturk range, the Twelve Bens ridge, and bog walks that take you through land that hasn't changed in centuries. Kylemore Abbey is just down the road, adding a bit of history and beauty to any visit.

Connemara's nature is as raw as it gets in Ireland. Blanket bogs, heath, and grassland— all shaped by Atlantic wind and rain. If you want to feel what wild Ireland is really like, this is the place to be.

Burren National Park — Clare

The Burren is like nowhere else in Ireland — or anywhere, for that matter. This national park in County Clare protects a limestone landscape where Arctic, Alpine, and Mediterranean wildflowers grow side by side. It shouldn't work, but it does.

The rock looks barren at first glance. Grey limestone stretches to the horizon in smooth, cracked pavements. But look closer and you'll find orchids in the cracks, hazel scrub in the gullies, and butterflies you won't see anywhere else in the country.

Walking here is special. Trails cross open limestone with views down to Galway Bay. The Burren Way runs through the wider area, and our tours take in the best of it. The terrain is unlike any other park — firm underfoot, with the kind of natural beauty that grows on you with every step.

For something different, the Aillwee Cave sits nearby, and the Cliffs of Moher are a short drive away. Pair a Burren walk with a coastal visit and you've got one of the best days out in Ireland.

Glenveagh National Park — Donegal

Glenveagh is Ireland's second-largest national park, and it's one of the least visited. That's part of its charm. Set in the mountains of northwest Donegal, it covers 40,000 acres of mountains, bogs, lakes, and native oak woodland.

The centrepiece is Glenveagh Castle—a 19th-century castle on the shores of Lough Veagh, surrounded by gardens that bloom even in late autumn. The walk from the visitor center to the castle follows the lakeshore through quiet woodland—about 3 kilometres each way.

For hikers, the Derryveagh Mountains offer serious climbing. The park includes some of the most remote ground in Ireland. Golden eagles have been reintroduced here, and if you're lucky, you might spot one circling above the ridges.

Hiking in Donegal is an experience you won't get anywhere else in Ireland. The light, the emptiness, and the scale of the land—it's closer to Scotland or Norway than anything you'd expect this far south.

Ballycroy National Park — Mayo

Ballycroy is Ireland's youngest national park and its wildest. It protects one of the last intact blanket bog systems in Europe — a vast, open, treeless landscape in northwest County Mayo.

This isn't a park with paved paths and visitor centers around every corner. It's big, remote, and quiet. The Wild Nephin Wilderness area — Ireland's first dark sky park — sits within its borders. On a clear night, the Milky Way is visible in full.

Walking here is about space and silence. The Bangor Trail crosses the park from north to south — a challenging route over open bog and mountain that's been used for centuries. It's not for beginners, but for experienced walkers, it's one of the outstanding wild walks in Ireland.

The park sits near Croagh Patrick — Ireland's holy mountain — so you can combine a bog walk with a mountain pilgrimage if the legs are up to it.

Planning Your Ireland National Parks Walk

You can visit Ireland's national parks any time of year. The best time to visit Ireland for walking is April to October, when the days are long and the trails dry out. But each season has its own appeal—autumn colours in Killarney, spring wildflowers in the Burren, and winter solitude in Glenveagh.

Good boots are a must — most park trails cross uneven ground, and rain is always a possibility. Our guide to picking hiking boots and the packing list will get you sorted.

If you're new to hiking, try a guided tour in one of the parks. You'll have a local guide who knows the trails, the wildlife, and the stories that bring the landscape to life. For more experienced walkers, our self-guided trips give you full freedom with all the support you need.

Several of our tours pass through national parks — the Kerry Way through Killarney, the Wicklow Way through the Wicklow Mountains, and Connemara routes through the park at Diamond Hill. Check our tour grading to find the right level for you.

Six Parks, One Country, Endless Walking

Ireland's national parks are the best of what this country has to offer walkers. Mountains, coast, bog, forest, history, and wildlife—all protected, all free to visit, and all waiting for you to lace up your boots and start walking.

What makes the national parks in Ireland special is how different each one feels. Killarney gives you lakes and oak forests. Wicklow provides you glacial valleys an hour from Dublin. Connemara gives you Atlantic wildness. The Burren gives you a moonscape full of flowers. Glenveagh gives you solitude. Ballycroy gives you the stars.

Six parks. Six different landscapes. And every one of them is worth the trip.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many national parks are there in Ireland?

Ireland has six national parks: Killarney National Park (Kerry), Wicklow Mountains National Park (Wicklow), Connemara National Park (Galway), Burren National Park (Clare), Glenveagh National Park (Donegal), and Ballycroy National Park (Mayo). All six are free to visit and open year-round.

Which Ireland national park is best for walking?

Killarney National Park offers the widest range of walks, from flat lakeside paths to mountain climbs. For long-distance trails, Wicklow Mountains National Park has the Wicklow Way. For dramatic coastal scenery, Connemara National Park and the Diamond Hill loop are difficult to beat. The best choice depends on your fitness level and what kind of landscape you enjoy.

Are Ireland's national parks free to visit?

Yes, all six national parks in Ireland are free to enter. There is no admission charge for walking the trails. Some visitor centres and specific attractions within or near the parks (like Muckross House in Killarney or Glenveagh Castle) may charge a separate entry fee.

What is the best time of year to visit Ireland's national parks?

April to October is the best time for walking in Ireland's national parks. Days are longest from May to August, giving you more daylight on the trails. Spring brings wildflowers to the Burren; summer has the warmest weather; and autumn colours are stunning in Killarney. Winter visits offer solitude but shorter days and wetter conditions.

Can beginners walk in Ireland's national parks?

Absolutely. Every national park has trails suitable for beginners. Killarney has flat lakeside walks around Muckross Lake. Wicklow has the lower Glendalough Valley trails. Connemara has the lower Diamond Hill loop. The Burren has gentle limestone paths. To get started, all you need are good walking shoes, waterproof layers, and a daypack.
