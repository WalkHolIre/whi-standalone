My name is Cliff, and I run Walking Holiday Ireland. After twenty years of guiding walkers through every corner of this country, people always ask me the same question: what are the best walks in ireland? It's a hard question because Ireland has so many outstanding trails, each with its own character. A coastal cliff path in Kerry feels nothing like a mountain ridge in Wicklow or a limestone pavement in the Burren. But that variety is exactly what makes walking here so rewarding.

Ireland packs a huge range of landscape into a small island. Mountains rise from the Atlantic. Ancient woodlands fill deep valleys. Blanket bogs stretch for miles. And the coastline is so wild and varied that it draws walkers from all over the world. The trails listed here represent the finest walking this country has to offer — from multi-day long-distance routes to shorter day walks that deliver views you won't forget. Whether you're planning your first trip or your tenth, these are the ireland hiking trails that belong on your list.

The Kerry Way

The Kerry Way is Ireland's longest and most popular waymarked trail, circling the Iveragh Peninsula in County Kerry over approximately 215 km. The route passes through Killarney National Park, the Black Valley, Cahersiveen, Waterville, Sneem, and Kenmare before returning to Killarney. You walk through ancient oakwoods, over mountain passes, along remote Atlantic coastline, and through small villages where traditional music fills the pubs at night.

What makes the Kerry Way one of the best hikes in ireland is the sheer variety of terrain. In a single day you might cross bogland, climb to a mountain saddle with views across the Atlantic, descend through farmland to a fishing village, and finish with a pint overlooking the sea. Most walkers take between five and eight days, and our self-guided walking holidays handle all the logistics — accommodation, luggage transfers, and detailed route notes.

The Wicklow Way

The Wicklow Way was Ireland's first waymarked trail, established in 1980, and it remains one of the finest. Running 127 km from the southern suburbs of Dublin through the Wicklow Mountains to Clonegal in County Carlow, it takes walkers through glacial valleys, heather-covered ridges, pine forests, and past the monastic ruins of Glendalough.

The early stages above Dublin offer panoramic views over the city and coast, while the middle sections around Glendalough and Glenmalure are among the most beautiful mountain walking anywhere in Ireland. The final stages descend through gentle farmland and riverside paths. The Wicklow Way is an excellent choice for walkers who want to combine mountain scenery with the convenience of starting close to Dublin.

The Dingle Way

The Dingle Way circles the Dingle Peninsula in County Kerry over 179 km of coastal and mountain paths. The peninsula is one of the most spectacular landforms in Ireland — a finger of mountain and moorland reaching into the Atlantic, with beaches, sea cliffs, and archaeological sites at every turn.

Highlights include the Connor Pass crossing, the walk along Slea Head with views to the Blasket Islands, and the stretch from Dunquin to Dingle town through some of the finest coastal scenery in Europe. The Dingle Peninsula is also an Irish-speaking area with a strong musical tradition, so your evenings are as memorable as your days on the trail.

The Burren Way

The Burren Way takes you through one of the most unusual landscapes in Europe — the Burren in County Clare. This 114 km trail crosses exposed limestone karst, passes ancient dolmens and ring forts, and runs along the Cliffs of Moher. The Burren looks barren from a distance, but up close it's alive with wildflowers — Arctic, Alpine, and Mediterranean species growing side by side in a botanical puzzle that scientists still can't fully explain.

May and June are the best months for flowers, when spring gentians, orchids, and bloody cranesbill paint the grey limestone with colour. The walking is generally moderate, with well-marked trails across open terrain. The nearby village of Doolin is one of the great traditional music centres of Ireland, making the Burren Way a perfect combination of natural wonder and cultural experience.

The Beara Way

The Beara Way is the hidden gem among Ireland's long-distance trails. Circling the Beara Peninsula on the Cork-Kerry border over 196 km, it passes through some of the wildest and least-visited coastal landscape in the country. The route follows old roads and mountain paths between fishing villages, with views across Bantry Bay and Kenmare Bay that rival anything on the more famous Ring of Kerry.

The Beara Peninsula has a raw, unspoiled quality that sets it apart. The Healy Pass crossing, the walk around Dursey Island connected by Ireland's only cable car, and the stretch along the Caha Mountains are among the most memorable sections. Because fewer walkers choose the Beara, you'll often have the trails to yourself — something increasingly rare on Ireland's better-known routes.

Cliffs of Moher Coastal Walk

The Cliffs of Moher in County Clare are one of Ireland's most famous natural landmarks, and walking along the cliff edge is an experience that takes your breath away. The cliffs rise up to 214 metres above the Atlantic, with seabirds wheeling below you and views stretching to the Aran Islands and the mountains of Connemara.

The official cliff walk runs about 18 km from Doolin south to Hag's Head, following the cliff top along grassy paths with the ocean far below. The northern section near Doolin is the most dramatic, with the cliffs at their highest and most vertical. On a clear day, the combination of crashing waves, soaring cliffs, and vast Atlantic views makes this one of the most memorable short walks anywhere in Ireland.

Glendalough Valley Walk

Glendalough — meaning "valley of the two lakes" — is a glacial valley in the Wicklow Mountains that combines stunning natural beauty with over a thousand years of human history. The monastic settlement founded by St Kevin in the 6th century sits between two dark lakes surrounded by steep, forested hillsides.

Several walking routes explore the valley, from easy lakeside loops to steeper climbs up the valley sides. The Spinc Walk takes you up to a viewpoint 300 metres above the Upper Lake, offering one of the most photographed views in Ireland. Glendalough is only an hour from Dublin, making it one of the most accessible of the best hikes in ireland for visitors with limited time.

Torc Mountain, Killarney

Torc Mountain in Killarney National Park is a compact but rewarding climb that gives you views across all three of Killarney's famous lakes, the MacGillycuddy's Reeks mountain range, and the oak and yew woodlands of the valley below. At 535 metres, it's not the highest peak in the area, but the panorama from the summit is one of the finest in Kerry.

The walk starts at the Torc Waterfall car park, where you can visit the 20-metre cascade before beginning the climb. The ascent takes about 1.5 hours and follows a combination of forest paths and open mountain. It's a perfect day walk for anyone staying in Killarney who wants mountain views without committing to a full-day expedition.

The Sheep's Head Way

The Sheep's Head Way is a 93 km loop around the smallest of the five great peninsulas of southwest Ireland. It's a quieter, more intimate alternative to the Kerry Way or Dingle Way, with gentle terrain, superb coastal views, and a wonderfully remote atmosphere. The peninsula is only a few kilometres wide in places, so you're never far from the sea on either side.

The walk to the Sheep's Head lighthouse at the tip of the peninsula is a highlight — a path across open headland with the Atlantic stretching away in every direction. The Sheep's Head has won awards for its walking trails and community tourism, and the small villages along the route offer warm hospitality without the crowds of more popular destinations.

Causeway Coast Way

The Causeway Coast Way in Northern Ireland runs 53 km along one of the most striking coastlines in the British Isles. The route passes the Giant's Causeway — a UNESCO World Heritage Site made up of 40,000 basalt columns formed by ancient lava flows. You'll also see Carrick-a-Rede rope bridge, ruined castles, and golden sand beaches.

The coastal scenery is extraordinary, with towering cliffs, hidden coves, and rock formations that look like they belong on another planet. The section between the Giant's Causeway and the rope bridge is the finest stretch, with constant views along the Antrim coast and across to Scotland on clear days.

Planning Your Walking Holiday

The best time to visit Ireland for walking is April to October. May and September are particularly good — warm enough for comfortable walking, with fewer visitors than peak summer. The Irish weather is mild but changeable, so a waterproof jacket is essential regardless of season.

Good hiking boots are the most important piece of equipment you'll bring. Check our packing list for everything else you need, and use our tour grading to match trails to your fitness level.

Our self-guided walking holidays include accommodation, luggage transfers, route notes, and local support for all the major trails listed here. If you prefer expert company on the trail, our guided walking holidays pair you with local guides who know every path, viewpoint, and story along the way. Whatever route you choose, walking in ireland is one of the finest ways to experience this country — at your own pace, in your own time, with the landscape unfolding around you with every step.

Frequently Asked Questions About Walking in Ireland

What is the difficulty level of the best walks in Ireland?

Ireland's best walks range from easy day walks to challenging multi-day routes. Easy walks include the Glendalough Valley Walk and sections of the Cliffs of Moher Coastal Walk, which can be completed by walkers of all fitness levels. Moderate to challenging options include the Kerry Way, Wicklow Way, Dingle Way, Burren Way, and Beara Way, which require good fitness and typically span 5-8 days. For detailed information about your fitness level and trail suitability, check our tour grading system.

What is the best time of year to walk in Ireland?

The best time to visit Ireland for walking is April to October. May and September offer ideal conditions — warm enough for comfortable walking with fewer visitors than peak summer. June is excellent for wildflower viewing on the Burren Way. October provides autumn colours and clear skies. Spring (March-April) and autumn (September-October) are generally less crowded than July and August, making them ideal for a more peaceful walking experience.

What gear and equipment do I need for walking in Ireland?

The most essential item is a good pair of hiking boots with ankle support and waterproof protection. Irish weather is mild but changeable, so always bring a waterproof jacket regardless of season. Other key items include weather-appropriate layers, a warm hat and gloves, socks designed for hiking, a daypack (20-30 litres), a water bottle, sun protection, and a map or GPS device. For longer trails, add blister treatment, energy snacks, and a basic first aid kit. See our complete packing list for more details.

Should I do a guided walking holiday or self-guided walking?

The choice depends on your preference and experience. Self-guided walks offer flexibility, independence, and the freedom to walk at your own pace. We handle logistics including accommodation, luggage transfers, and detailed route notes. Guided walking holidays provide expert company on the trail, local knowledge, historical and cultural insights, and the social experience of walking with others. Many walkers choose guided walks for their first visit to build confidence and knowledge, then return for self-guided options. Both approaches include full support and are excellent ways to experience Ireland's walking trails.

Do I need to be very fit to walk these trails?

Walking fitness is different from general fitness — consistency matters more than intensity. If you regularly walk 10-15 km on flat ground without difficulty, you're ready for most of Ireland's trails. Shorter walks like the Glendalough Valley Walk and Torc Mountain require moderate fitness. Long-distance trails (Kerry Way, Wicklow Way, etc.) require good fitness and the ability to walk 15-20 km daily over multiple days. Start with shorter trails to build confidence and strength, and use our grading system to match trails to your fitness level. Regular walking training for 6-8 weeks before your holiday is ideal.
