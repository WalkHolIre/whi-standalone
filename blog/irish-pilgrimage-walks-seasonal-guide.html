Irish Pilgrim Paths: Seasonal Guide for Walking in 2026

Photo: Walking Holiday Ireland

Best Times to Walk Ireland's Pilgrim Paths: A Seasonal Guide

There's a particular quality to pilgrimage walks that distinguishes them from standard hiking routes. It's not the distance or difficulty, though Ireland's sacred paths often involve both. It's the accumulated weight of walking where millions have walked before — footsteps following patterns established centuries ago, each walker joining a continuity that stretches back to early Christian Ireland and, before that, to the pre-Christian sacred sites those monasteries were often built upon.

Ireland's pilgrim paths are among the oldest organised walking routes in Europe. They connect early monastic sites, holy wells, mountain summits, and island hermitages in a network that shaped the physical landscape of the country. Walking them today is genuinely different from walking a long-distance trail — and the time of year you walk them shapes the experience considerably.

This guide covers Ireland's main pilgrim paths season by season: what conditions to expect, what events to plan around, and when each route is at its most rewarding.

Ireland's Main Pilgrim Paths: A Brief Overview

Croagh Patrick, County Mayo — Ireland's holiest mountain, 764 metres, climbed by an estimated one million pilgrims annually. St Patrick spent 40 days fasting on the summit in 441 AD. The route from Murrisk (8 km return, steep final ascent over loose quartzite scree) is one of the defining Irish pilgrimage experiences.

St Kevin's Way, County Wicklow — a 30 km pilgrim path from Hollywood to Glendalough, following the route that St Kevin walked to his monastic foundation. The path passes through mountain valleys and the eastern flanks of the Wicklow Mountains before descending to the lake valley. Our St Kevin of Glendalough guide covers the saint's story in depth.

Skellig Michael, County Kerry — a UNESCO World Heritage site off the Kerry coast, accessible by boat from May to October. The 6th-century monastery sits 600 steps above the sea. Not a walking route in the conventional sense but one of the great pilgrimage destinations of early Christianity. Our Ring of Kerry walks guide covers the coastal context.

St Finbarr's Way, County Cork — a 35 km walk from Drimoleague to Gougane Barra, following the route walked by St Finbarr when he founded his first oratory at the lake. One of Ireland's most beautiful pilgrim routes, combining mountain and forest with a lake site of extraordinary atmosphere. Our St Finbarr's Way guide covers the full route.

Cnoc na dTobar, County Kerry — the pilgrim path ascending Knocknaman Mountain to a holy well, traditionally associated with Reek Sunday. A shorter but genuinely moving route in the shadow of Croagh Patrick.

The Celtic Camino — a modern designation connecting pilgrim routes across western Ireland from Galway to Mayo, parallelling in spirit (and partly in geography) the famous Camino de Santiago. Draws walkers who have completed the Spanish Camino and want to experience the Irish equivalent. Our Irish pilgrim paths guide covers the full network.

Season by Season: When to Walk Ireland's Pilgrim Paths

Spring: March to May

Character: Lengthening days, variable weather, rapidly improving conditions from mid-April. The pilgrimage season opens.

Croagh Patrick: April and May are excellent months. The mountain emerges from winter, the scree is dry enough for confident footing, and the crowds that characterise July and August are entirely absent. Early morning departures from Murrisk in May — walking in clear light with Clew Bay spread below — represent Croagh Patrick at something close to its best.

St Kevin's Way: Perfect spring walking. The Wicklow landscape undergoes its most dramatic transformation from March onward: the gorse blazes on the hillsides, the valley floors green rapidly, and the mountain section between Hollywood and the plateau has a quality of seasonal opening that suits pilgrimage. Glendalough in April or May, before the summer crowds, is unusually peaceful.

Skellig Michael: Boats begin operating in May, weather permitting. Early season visits are often the most atmospheric — puffins arrive in late April and the island feels genuinely remote rather than managed. Book as early as possible; landing permits are strictly limited.

St Finbarr's Way: Excellent from April. Gougane Barra forest is at its most vivid in spring. The oratory at the lake reflects in still water on calm mornings, and the walk through the Shehy Mountains carries the season's specific freshness.

Pilgrimage calendar note: The feast of St Patrick (17 March) marks the traditional opening of the Croagh Patrick pilgrimage season. Ballintubber Abbey, at the start of the traditional Croagh Patrick pilgrimage route from the east (the Tóchar Phádraig), often hosts early season pilgrimages organised around this date.

Summer: June to August

Character: Long days, warmest temperatures, maximum crowds. The main pilgrimage season. Book everything in advance.

Reek Sunday — last Sunday in July — is the single most significant date in the Irish pilgrimage calendar. Up to 30,000 pilgrims ascend Croagh Patrick in a single day, many barefoot in the tradition of the penitential pilgrimage. The experience of Reek Sunday is extraordinary and completely unlike any other day on the mountain — the scale of human effort, the shared purpose, the community — but the path is severely crowded. For walkers who want the spiritual weight of the tradition without the physical challenge of sharing a narrow scree path with 30,000 people, the weeks either side of Reek Sunday offer a middle ground.

Skellig Michael: June to August are the primary access months but also the most competitive for landing permits. Book months in advance. The island in July carries more visitors than at any other time — the experience is different from the solitude that the monastic community sought but no less powerful at the summit.

St Kevin's Way: Summer is perfectly walkable but the Glendalough end of the route becomes significantly more crowded. Starting early — before 8 AM — gives you the valley at something approaching its natural quiet.

Practical note on summer conditions: Irish summer weather remains variable. The extended daylight (nearly 17 hours around the solstice) compensates for lost walking hours on poorer weather days. See our best time to visit Ireland hiking guide for full seasonal conditions.

Autumn: September to November

Character: Crowds thin, light changes, colours arrive. The finest pilgrimage season for experienced walkers.

September and October are the months that WHI consistently recommends for Ireland's pilgrim paths. The reasons compound: reduced crowds on every route; stable weather windows (September in particular often delivers settled conditions); the specific quality of autumn light on the Irish landscape; and the sense that a pilgrimage walk belongs to this season — the year winding down, a natural moment for reflection and deliberate movement.

Croagh Patrick in September is the mountain at its finest for walkers who are not attending Reek Sunday specifically. The path is quieter by 80% compared to July. The light on Clew Bay from the summit in September — warm, angled, the bay's 365 islands picked out clearly — is one of the specific visual experiences of Ireland.

Skellig Michael: Boats continue operating until late October, weather permitting. September visits combine good access probability with reduced visitor numbers. The puffins have left by this point (they depart in August) but the monastic settlement itself, in autumn light with the Atlantic visible in every direction, is arguably at its most affecting.

St Finbarr's Way in October: Gougane Barra's woodland turns in October and the route through the Shehy Mountains is at its most dramatically coloured. The oratory at the lake on an October morning — mist lifting off the water, the forest in full colour — is a particular experience.

Winter: December to February

Character: Short days, frequent storms, significant mountain hazards. Specialist territory.

The pilgrim paths are not closed in winter but several of them carry genuine risk in winter conditions.

Croagh Patrick: The quartzite scree on the upper section becomes treacherous when iced. Winter ascents are attempted by experienced mountain walkers only, with full winter equipment. The route is not suitable for pilgrimage walking in December, January, or February for most walkers.

St Kevin's Way: The valley sections remain walkable throughout winter. The mountain section becomes serious in snow or ice. The Glendalough valley in winter has a quality entirely its own — frost on the oak woodland, ice on the lakes, the valley almost empty of visitors — and for walkers who are comfortable with winter conditions, a winter visit to Glendalough has a spiritual weight appropriate to the season.

Skellig Michael: Boat access is suspended from late October to early May. The island is not accessible to visitors in winter.

St Finbarr's Way: The lower sections remain walkable. Gougane Barra in winter, particularly around the feast of St Finbarr (25 September, just before winter properly sets in), carries a particular stillness.

The Pilgrim Passport

The Irish pilgrim passport — modelled on the Compostela system of the Camino de Santiago — allows walkers to collect stamps at designated sites along Ireland's pilgrim paths. Completing a full route earns a certificate of completion. The passport is available from pilgrim path offices and tourism information centres.

For walkers who have completed the Camino de Santiago and want to experience the Irish tradition, the pilgrim paths offer a direct connection: much of Irish monasticism spread through the network of scholars who walked between Ireland and the continent in the 5th to 8th centuries, and the pilgrimage tradition that survived in Ireland is in many ways older and less mediated than the Camino as it now operates.

Combining Pilgrim Paths with a Walking Holiday

The Wicklow Way passes through Glendalough — St Kevin's Way's destination — making it the most natural combination of long-distance trail and pilgrim path. Walkers on the Wicklow Way can walk the St Kevin's Way approach from Hollywood as an alternative route to the standard trail, adding both distance and the specific quality of pilgrimage intent to the experience.

The Kerry Way puts walkers within reach of both Skellig Michael (by boat from the Kerry coast) and the Cnoc na dTobar route — a combination that makes the Kerry peninsula one of the richest areas in Ireland for pilgrimage walking.
