Travel to Ireland: A Practical Planning Guide for Walking Holidays

Getting to Ireland is straightforward. Getting everything organised before you go — flights, visas, documentation, getting around once you're here, and

What to expect from the weather—it takes a little more thought, particularly if you're planning a self-guided walking holiday rather than a city break.

This guide covers everything you need to know before you book. I've written it specifically for walkers: what is relevant for a multi-day hiking trip is sometimes different from a general Ireland travel guide, and I'd rather give you the specific information that's actually useful.

If you have any questions that aren't covered here, our FAQ page covers a lot of ground, or you can get in touch directly.

Getting to Ireland: Flights and Ferries

Flights

Dublin Airport (DUB) is Ireland's main international gateway and the best arrival point for most walking holidays. It has excellent connections from the UK, Europe, and North America. The airport is well-served by public transport into Dublin city centre and has car rental facilities at the terminal.

Cork Airport (ORK) is useful if your walking holiday is in the southwest — particularly for the Kerry Way, Dingle Way, or St Finbarr's Way. Connections are good from UK airports and some European hubs.

Shannon Airport (SNN) serves the west coast and is a viable option for the Burren Way or anyone planning to explore Connemara before or after their walking holiday.

Belfast International (BFS) covers Northern Ireland routes including the Causeway Coast and Antrim Glens. Note that Northern Ireland is part of the UK, so separate entry requirements may apply — see the section on visas below.

For flights to Ireland, booking 6 to 8 weeks in advance typically gives the best price balance. Aer Lingus, Ryanair, and British Airways are the primary carriers from UK airports. Transatlantic services from the US run year-round from Dublin; seasonal options include Cork.

Passports, Visas and Documentation

Do you need a passport for Ireland?

For visitors from the UK: You do not need a passport to travel to the Republic of Ireland — a photo ID is technically acceptable under the Common Travel Area arrangements. However, in practice, most airlines require a valid passport even for UK-Ireland routes, and I'd always recommend travelling with your passport regardless.

For all other nationalities: A valid passport is required. Check that your passport doesn't expire during your stay; most countries recommend at least 6 months' validity beyond your return date.

Visa requirements

The Republic of Ireland does not require a visa for citizens of EU/EEA countries, the USA, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and many other nationalities. If you're unsure whether your nationality requires a visa, check the Irish Naturalisation and Immigration Service at inis.gov.ie.

Northern Ireland is part of the UK and has separate entry requirements. An electronic travel authority (ETA) is now required for some nationalities travelling to the UK, including visitors who would otherwise not need a visa. Our Northern Ireland ETA guide explains exactly who needs one and how to apply.

If your walking holiday includes both the Republic and Northern Ireland—for example, combining the Causeway Coast with a southern route— check both sets of requirements before you travel.

Travel insurance

Comprehensive travel insurance is essential. Make sure your policy covers:

  • Medical expenses (Ireland's health system prioritises residents — visitors should have private cover)

  • Trip cancellation and interruption

  • Hiking and walking activities specifically — some standard policies exclude these; check the small print

  • Luggage and equipment, including walking boots and specialist gear

  • Emergency evacuation for remote trail sections

Most walking holiday insurers will cover the routes WHI offers. If you're unsure, ask your insurer to confirm cover for multi-day self-guided walking holidays in Ireland.

Getting Around Ireland

Car rental

For a self-guided walking holiday in Ireland, car rental gives you the most flexibility — particularly for accessing trailheads, moving between regions, or getting to and from airports.

Key points to know before you rent:

  • Drive on the left throughout Ireland (both Republic and Northern Ireland)

  • Rural roads are narrower than they appear on maps — allow more time than you'd expect

  • A full driving licence is required; an International Driving Permit is recommended for non-EU licence holders

  • Confirm your car insurance covers Ireland, including Northern Ireland if your route crosses the border

For most of our self-guided tours, you don't need a car during the walking days — we arrange luggage transfers and accommodation in walking villages. But rental is useful for the journey in and out.

Public transport

Irish Rail (irishrail.ie) connects Dublin to major cities including Cork, Galway, Limerick, and Waterford. Train travel to a trail starting point – Cork city for the Dingle Way or Kerry Way, for example – is entirely viable and avoids driving through city centres.

Bus Éireann and GoBus cover regional routes not served by rail. The Transport for Ireland journey planner is the most useful tool for planning connections.

For Northern Ireland, Translink (translink.co.uk) operates bus and rail services, including routes along the north Antrim coast near the Causeway Coast Way.

Practical Preparations

Currency

The euro (€) is the currency in the Republic of Ireland. Pound sterling (£) is used in Northern Ireland.

ATMs are readily available in towns and villages along all our walking routes. Credit and debit cards are widely accepted, including in most rural pubs and guesthouses. That said, it's worth carrying some cash — a handful of places in tiny villages still prefer it.

Notify your bank before travelling to avoid fraud blocks on your card.

Electricity and plugs

Ireland uses the Type G plug (the same three-pin rectangular plug used in the UK) at 230V. Visitors from mainland Europe will need an adapter. Visitors from the US and Canada will need both an adapter and a voltage converter for older devices, though most modern electronics handle 100–240V automatically.

Mobile phones and connectivity

Irish mobile providers include Vodafone, Three, and eir. Prepaid SIM cards are available at Dublin Airport and in most convenience stores. EU roaming rules mean European visitors typically pay domestic rates.

WiFi is standard in all our partner accommodation. Mobile signalling is reliable in towns and villages but can be patchy on mountain trails— particularly in the Wicklow Mountains and parts of Kerry and Donegal. Don't rely on the phone's signal for navigation on exposed routes.

Time zone

Ireland runs on GMT (Greenwich Mean Time) in the winter and IST (Irish Standard Time, UTC+1) in the summer. Ireland is 5 hours ahead of US Eastern Time in the winter and 6 hours ahead of the summer.

Irish Weather and When to Visit

Ireland's weather is famously variable. That's not pessimism — it's just true. Rain can occur any time of year, and the mountains create their own weather, regardless of the forecast.

The practical response is to pack properly and embrace it. Our layering guide covers the right approach to Irish hiking kits. Good waterproofs, sturdy boots, and a positive attitude towards grey days will serve you better than hoping for unbroken sunshine.

That said, the best months for walking in Ireland are:

May and June—long evenings, fresh greens, and wildflowers in bloom. These months often offer the best weather window of the year, free from the peak-season crowds of July and August.

September and October—stable weather, golden light, and autumn colours in the valleys. My personal preference for most routes.

July and August — reliable but busy. Popular trails and accommodations can be congested, so it is advisable to book well in advance.

For detailed month-by-month guidance, our best time to visit Ireland for hiking article covers every trail and region.

Planning Your Walking Holiday

The most useful advice I can give you about planning a walking holiday in Ireland is to arrange the walking before booking the flights. Accommodation in the most popular walking villages — Glendalough, Dingle, Kenmare, and Bushmills — fills up quickly in the summer months, and good availability on your preferred dates matters more than saving €30 on a flight booked three weeks earlier.

Here's how we work with our guests:

  1. We discuss your preferred route, dates, and fitness level

  2. We check accommodation availability and lock in your preferred stops

  3. We sort luggage transfers, route notes, and any additional logistics

  4. You book flights and ferry once the walking itinerary is confirmed

Our how it works page covers the full process. If you'd like to see what your holiday would look like day by day, our destinations page shows all the routes we cover, or you can browse self-guided walking holidays for the full tour range.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use my UK or EU driving licence in Ireland?

Yes. UK and EU driving licences are fully valid in Ireland. Non-EU licence holders should carry an International Driving Permit.

How much cash should I bring?

Most places accept cards. Bring a modest amount of cash (€50–100) for small village stops, tips, and the occasional place that prefers it.

What's the best way to get around for a walking holiday?

Car rental gives the most flexibility for reaching trailheads and airports. For the walking days themselves, luggage transfers mean you travel with just a daypack — your bags move between accommodation stops without you. Our tours handle all of the logistics.

How do I book a walking holiday with Walking Holiday Ireland?

You can book directly through our website by selecting your preferred tour and clicking Book Now, or use the Trip Enquiry form to contact us first. A deposit of 25% of the invoice is required at the time of booking to secure your place.

What should I do if something goes wrong on the trail?

All our self-guided guests have a 24-hour support contact number. For emergencies in Ireland, call 999 or 112. Mountain Rescue Ireland covers the major walking areas.

Is tap water safe to drink?

Yes. Irish tap water is among the safest in Europe. There's no need for bottled water.

Do I need a visa to travel to Ireland?

Citizens of EU/EEA countries, the USA, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand don't need a visa for the Republic of Ireland. Check inis.gov.ie for your specific nationality. Northern Ireland (UK) has separate entry requirements — see our Northern Ireland ETA guide.

Is travel insurance required?
Yes. Travel insurance is mandatory for all participants. Your policy must cover cancellations, personal accidents, illness, loss of possessions, and any medical costs that may arise during the tour - including emergency evacuation if needed. Walking Holiday Ireland does not review insurance policies for suitability, but reserves the right to request proof of insurance.
What should my travel insurance cover for a hiking holiday?
Your policy should cover: trip cancellation, personal accident and illness, emergency medical expenses (including helicopter rescue), loss or damage of personal belongings, and hiking/walking activities at the relevant difficulty level. Many standard travel policies exclude hillwalking - check the small print before purchasing.
