Dingle Way vs Kerry Way: Which Irish Walking Trail Is Right for You?

My name is Cliff, and I run Walking Holiday Ireland. The dingle way vs kerry way question is one I get asked more than almost anything else. Both trails are in County Kerry, both loop around stunning Atlantic peninsulas, and both rank among the finest long-distance walks in Europe. But they're different experiences, and the right choice depends on what you're looking for.

I've walked both trails many times and organised hundreds of holidays on each. This guide breaks down the key differences so you can decide which one suits you best — or whether you should walk them both.

The Dingle Way at a Glance

The Dingle Way is a 179 km loop around the Dingle Peninsula in the far southwest of Ireland. The trail starts and finishes in Tralee, passing through Dingle town, Camp, Annascaul, and along some of the most dramatic coastal scenery in the country. Most walkers complete it in 8 days, though we offer versions from 5 days to 10 days depending on your pace and fitness.

The Dingle Peninsula is compact and intimate. The mountains — including Mount Brandon, Ireland's second-highest peak at 952 metres — rise steeply from the sea. The coastline switches between sandy beaches, high cliffs, and rocky headlands. Ancient beehive huts, ogham stones, and early Christian sites dot the landscape. Much of the western peninsula is a Gaeltacht area where Irish is the daily language, giving the place a cultural depth that goes beyond scenery.

The trail itself mixes coastal paths, mountain passes, quiet country roads, and green lanes. The daily distances are manageable — typically 18 to 25 km — but there are some stiff climbs, particularly over the Connor Pass and the stretch from Cloghane to Brandon. The accommodation along the route is excellent, mostly small family-run B&Bs where the hosts know the trail and the area inside out.

The Kerry Way at a Glance

The Kerry Way is a 215 km circuit around the Iveragh Peninsula, starting and finishing in Killarney. It's Ireland's longest waymarked trail and one of the most popular walking routes in Europe. The trail passes through Killarney National Park, Black Valley, Glencar, Glenbeigh, Cahersiveen, Waterville, Sneem, Kenmare, and back to Killarney. Our tours run from 5 days to 8 days.

The Iveragh Peninsula is bigger and more varied than Dingle. The Kerry Way covers a wider range of terrain — lakeshores, mountain passes, river valleys, coastal cliffs, and woodland. Killarney National Park alone offers ancient oak forests, the Lakes of Killarney, and views of the MacGillycuddy's Reeks, Ireland's highest mountain range. The trail passes through larger villages with more services, pubs, and restaurants.

The daily stages average 20 to 28 km, with some longer days. The terrain is generally less mountainous than the Dingle Way, though the Black Valley and Windy Gap sections have real elevation. The accommodation ranges from guesthouses in Killarney and Kenmare to cosy B&Bs in quieter villages like Sneem and Glencar.

Distance and Duration

The Kerry Way is longer at 215 km compared to the Dingle Way's 179 km. On a standard itinerary, most walkers need 8 days for the full Kerry Way and 8 days for the full Dingle Way. However, we design tours at different lengths for both trails.

If you have 5 to 6 days, either trail works — we select the best sections and arrange transfers to skip the less scenic road walking. If you have a full 8 to 10 days and want to walk every kilometre, the Dingle Way is the more achievable complete circuit, while the Kerry Way demands more stamina for the full route.

Scenery Comparison

This is where the choice gets personal. The Dingle Way offers raw, concentrated coastal drama — cliff edges, crashing Atlantic waves, sandy coves, and Mount Brandon towering above. The scenery feels wild and ancient, and you're never far from the ocean. The Blasket Islands appear off the coast, adding to the sense of being at the edge of Europe.

The Kerry Way offers more variety. You get lakes and ancient oakwoods in Killarney National Park, high mountain passes with views of the Reeks, green valleys with tumbling rivers, and coastal stretches around Waterville and Cahersiveen. The scenery changes dramatically from day to day. If the Dingle Way is an intense coastal painting, the Kerry Way is a gallery with a different landscape in every room.

Both trails deliver some of the finest walking scenery in Ireland. You won't be disappointed by either. Many walkers tell me the scenery alone was worth the trip, regardless of which peninsula they chose.

Difficulty and Terrain

The Dingle Way is moderately challenging. The Connor Pass crossing and the Mount Brandon area involve steep, exposed climbs where you need to be comfortable on rough ground and prepared for changing weather. Some sections are remote, with limited shelter. Fitness level should be moderate to good.

The Kerry Way is moderate overall, with a few harder sections. The Black Valley to Glencar stage and the Windy Gap are the toughest stretches. Much of the trail follows old droving roads and quiet lanes, making it slightly easier underfoot than the Dingle Way. The longer daily distances on some stages mean you need endurance even if the terrain is less technical.

Check our tour grading for detailed difficulty ratings on each route. Good hiking boots are essential for both.

Villages and Culture

Dingle town is one of the most charming small towns in Ireland. Colourful shopfronts, traditional music sessions in the pubs every night, and some of the best seafood restaurants on the west coast. The Gaeltacht villages on the western peninsula — Ventry, Dunquin, Ballyferriter — have a quieter, more traditional character. You'll hear Irish spoken in the shops and on the street.

The Kerry Way passes through more and larger villages. Killarney is a bustling tourist town with every amenity. Kenmare is an elegant foodie destination. Sneem is a postcard-pretty village with painted houses around a green. Waterville has wild Atlantic beaches and a famous golf course. Each night brings a different village with its own character.

If you want deep cultural immersion in a compact area, the Dingle Way wins. If you prefer more variety in your evening stops and a wider range of dining and entertainment, the Kerry Way has the edge.

Accommodation and Logistics

Both trails are well served by accommodation, and we handpick every B&B and guesthouse on our self-guided walking holidays. Your luggage is transferred each day, you carry just a daypack, and we provide detailed route notes with maps.

The Dingle Way has slightly fewer accommodation options in the more remote western sections, which is why we book well in advance. The Kerry Way has more choice at each stop, particularly in Killarney, Kenmare, and Waterville.

Both trails are easy to reach. The Dingle Way starts in Tralee, which has a train station and regular bus services. The Kerry Way starts in Killarney, one of the best-connected towns in the southwest with direct trains from Dublin and Cork.

Which Trail Should You Choose?

Choose the Dingle Way if you love dramatic coastal scenery, want a more intimate and culturally rich experience, enjoy challenging terrain with mountain elements, and prefer smaller villages with traditional character. The Dingle Way is also a good choice if you want to complete an entire long-distance trail in a single trip.

Choose the Kerry Way if you prefer varied scenery that changes daily, want more village amenities and dining options, prefer gentler terrain for most of the walk, and like the idea of starting from Killarney with its national park. The Kerry Way suits walkers who want a longer adventure with more variety in the landscape and the overnight stops.

If you can't decide, consider doing a shorter version of each on the same trip — walk 4 to 5 days on the Dingle Way, transfer to Killarney, and walk 4 to 5 days on the Kerry Way. We can arrange the entire combination as a single holiday.

Planning Your Walk

The best time to visit Ireland for either trail is May to September. June offers the longest daylight hours, while September brings quieter trails and golden autumn colours. The Irish weather in Kerry is mild and wet — waterproofs are essential regardless of season. Check our packing list for the full gear rundown.

Whether you choose the Dingle Way, the Kerry Way, or both, you're walking through some of the most beautiful landscape in Europe. Both trails deliver that rare combination of world-class scenery, warm hospitality, and the satisfaction of covering ground on your own two feet.

What is the difference between the Dingle Way and the Kerry Way?

The Dingle Way is a 179 km loop around the Dingle Peninsula, known for dramatic coastal scenery and cultural immersion in a compact area. The Kerry Way is a 215 km circuit around the Iveragh Peninsula offering more varied terrain including Killarney National Park, lakeshores, and larger villages with more amenities.

How many days does it take to walk the Dingle Way or Kerry Way?

Most walkers complete the full Dingle Way in 8 days and the full Kerry Way in 8 days. Both trails offer shorter versions ranging from 5 to 10 days depending on your pace, fitness level, and available time.

Which trail is easier: the Dingle Way or the Kerry Way?

The Kerry Way is generally easier overall. The Dingle Way is moderately challenging with steep climbs at Connor Pass and Mount Brandon. The Kerry Way is moderate with a few harder sections like Black Valley and Windy Gap, but mostly follows old droving roads and quiet lanes making it slightly easier underfoot.

What is the best time of year to walk the Dingle Way or Kerry Way?

The best time to visit Ireland for either trail is May to September. June offers the longest daylight hours, while September brings quieter trails and golden autumn colours. Both trails require waterproofs as Irish weather is mild and wet year-round.

Can I do both the Dingle Way and the Kerry Way on the same trip?

Yes. You can do a shorter version of each on the same trip by walking 4 to 5 days on the Dingle Way, transferring to Killarney, and walking 4 to 5 days on the Kerry Way. Walking Holiday Ireland can arrange the entire combination as a single holiday.
