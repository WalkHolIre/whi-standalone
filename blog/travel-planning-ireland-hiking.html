Travel Planning for Ireland: A Walker's Complete Guide

Photo: Walking Holiday Ireland

Planning a walking holiday in Ireland is one of the best decisions you’ll make. But before those boots hit the trails, there’s a fair bit of groundwork to sort out. I’ve walked these hills myself for twenty years, and I’ve helped thousands of walkers prepare for their Irish adventures. The good news? Getting here and getting ready is far simpler than you might think. This guide covers everything you need to know about travel planning ireland hiking, so you can arrive confident, organised and ready to walk.

Travel Planning Ireland Hiking: Getting to Ireland and Choosing Your Gateway

The first decision is which airport makes sense for your walking holiday. Ireland has four main international gateways, and each one serves different trails beautifully.

Dublin Airport is the largest and most connected. If you’re walking the Wicklow Way, the Burren Way, or exploring County Kerry, Dublin is your natural arrival point. You’ll find direct flights from London, most European capitals, and major North American cities.

Shannon Airport on the west coast is my recommendation if you’re heading to Clare, Galway or the Aran Islands. It’s smaller and less busy than Dublin, which many walkers appreciate. The drive time to western trails is significantly shorter, so you’re not wasting your first day crossing the country.

Cork Airport works beautifully if you’re exploring the southwest — County Cork itself, Kerry, or the Sheep’s Head Peninsula. It’s intimate and quick to get through, and you’re on hiking ground within an hour.

Belfast International Airport serves walkers exploring Northern Ireland — the Mourne Mountains, the Antrim coast, and the Causeway. My honest tip? Choose based on where you want to start walking, not on flight prices alone. Saving a few pounds on a ticket isn’t worth an extra three hours driving.

Getting From Airport to Trail

Rental cars give you complete freedom. You can stop at villages you fancy, explore side routes and move between different trail sections at your own pace. Ireland drives on the left, which catches some visitors out, so take time to get comfortable. Roads are generally well-maintained, though rural lanes can be narrow.

Buses and trains are reliable and surprisingly affordable. Irish Bus and Irish Rail connect all major towns. The journey from Dublin to Galway takes about two-and-a-half hours by bus, and you can relax and watch the landscape unfold. If you’re walking established routes with fixed start and end points, public transport often works perfectly.

Private transfers are the luxury option — someone meets you at arrivals and drives you to your accommodation. It costs more, but if you’re tired after a long flight, it’s worth every penny.

When you’re focused on travel planning ireland hiking, matching your transport method to your personal style matters. If you love spontaneity and don’t mind driving on the left, rent a car. If you prefer letting someone else navigate, take the bus. Either way, you’re walking some of the world’s most beautiful terrain within hours of landing.

Currency, Cards and Cash

The Republic of Ireland uses the Euro, while Northern Ireland uses British Pounds Sterling. Make sure you have the right money in the right place.

Contactless cards are accepted almost everywhere — supermarkets, restaurants, pubs, petrol stations, even small tea shops. American and Canadian visitors should let their bank know they’re travelling, as some institutions temporarily block international transactions as a fraud precaution.

That said, small villages and mountain tea rooms sometimes operate cash-only. A modest amount of euros in your wallet covers those moments. ATMs are common in towns and airports, and the exchange rate at ATMs is usually better than at airport currency exchanges.

Power and Connectivity in the Hills

Irish electrical outlets use Type G three-pin rectangular plugs, and the voltage is 230V. If you’re coming from the US or Canada, you’ll need an adapter. UK visitors won’t. Grab one before you leave, or pick one up at any airport or supermarket when you arrive.

Mobile signal is excellent in towns and villages, though it gets patchy once you climb into the mountains. Most Irish guesthouses and hotels have WiFi, so you can check messages and share photos every evening. European Union visitors enjoy roaming rates within Ireland as though you were at home. American and Canadian travellers should check roaming costs — many walkers buy a cheap Irish SIM card for the week.

Travel Insurance and Health

I’m direct about this: travel insurance is essential. You might be a fit and experienced walker, but an awkward step or a wet rock can happen to anyone. Your insurance should cover hiking and mountain walking — not all policies do, so read the terms carefully.

If you’re a European Union citizen, bring your European Health Insurance Card (EHIC). It doesn’t replace travel insurance, but it provides access to state healthcare in Ireland. Your travel insurance should include emergency evacuation, medical expenses, lost baggage and trip cancellation. It’s cheap — often just fifteen to twenty pounds for a week — and it means you can walk with complete peace of mind.

What to Pack: The Essentials

Packing right transforms your walking experience. You don’t need fancy gear, but you do need the right foundation.

Start with layering. An Irish day can move from blue skies to rain and back again in two hours. Bring a moisture-wicking base layer, a warm mid-layer and a waterproof outer layer. Your waterproof jacket is non-negotiable — Ireland is wet, and quality gear keeps you comfortable when weather turns.

Proper walking boots matter genuinely. You want boots that are already broken in, not new ones that will give you blisters on day one. Bring two pairs of socks per day — merino wool or moisture-wicking synthetics, never cotton. A comfortable daypack of 20-30 litres carries your layers, water, snacks and a waterproof cover.

Don’t forget sun protection — Irish sun bounces off water and reflects off rock. Bring sunscreen, a hat and sunglasses. And pack a headtorch for those evenings when the walk runs longer than expected.

Timing Your Irish Walking Season

May through September is peak season, and there’s a reason. Daylight stretches from dawn around 5 am to dusk around 9 pm. Weather is most stable, though rain still visits regularly. Accommodation is available and towns feel alive.

April and October are shoulder seasons and brilliant value. You get reliable daylight, fewer people on the trails and refreshingly crisp mornings. Weather is a bit more unpredictable, but that’s part of the adventure.

November to March is tougher. Daylight is limited, weather is cold and wet, and some smaller guesthouses close seasonally. Winter walking has quiet beauty and solitude that appeals to experienced walkers, but it demands more preparation.

Ready to Walk?

Travel planning for Ireland hiking needn’t be complicated. You’ve got good flights into the country, reliable transport to the trailhead, excellent infrastructure once you’re here, and endless beautiful hills to explore. Pack properly, book your insurance, and arrive with good boots already broken in.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the best way to get around for a walking holiday?

Car rental gives the most flexibility for reaching trailheads and airports. For the walking days themselves, luggage transfers mean you travel with just a daypack — your bags move between accommodation stops without you. Our tours handle all of the logistics.

Is travel insurance required?
Yes. Travel insurance is mandatory for all participants. Your policy must cover cancellations, personal accidents, illness, loss of possessions, and any medical costs that may arise during the tour - including emergency evacuation if needed. Walking Holiday Ireland does not review insurance policies for suitability, but reserves the right to request proof of insurance.
What should my travel insurance cover for a hiking holiday?
Your policy should cover: trip cancellation, personal accident and illness, emergency medical expenses (including helicopter rescue), loss or damage of personal belongings, and hiking/walking activities at the relevant difficulty level. Many standard travel policies exclude hillwalking - check the small print before purchasing.
How far in advance should I book?
For summer departures (June-August), we recommend booking 9-11 months in advance, as popular trails fill quickly and accommodation books up fast. For spring and autumn departures, 3-6 months is generally sufficient.
Will I have mobile phone coverage on the trails?
Mobile coverage is generally good in populated areas but can be patchy in mountain and remote regions. We recommend downloading offline maps before you set out each day and carrying a fully charged power bank. Our route notes highlight areas with limited coverage.
