Ireland welcomes walkers from around the world. The island has excellent flight connections from major cities globally, efficient transfer options from airports to trailheads, and accommodation built specifically for walkers. The logistics of travelling to Ireland for a walking holiday are genuinely straightforward — once you know what to expect.

This guide covers everything from visas and flights to packing and jet lag, whether you are travelling from North America, Europe, Australia, or anywhere else globally.

Before you travel to Ireland, it's beneficial to research various trails and activities available during your visit.

Entry Requirements

Visas & Immigration

For those looking to travel to Ireland, remember that each region offers unique experiences, so plan your itinerary accordingly.

🇺🇸

USA, Canada & Australia

Visa-free entry for tourism up to 90 days. Your passport must be valid for the full duration of your stay.

🇪🇺

EU & UK Citizens

Simple entry with a valid full passport. UK visitors should note that a passport card is no longer sufficient.

📱

Ireland ETA (Coming Soon)

An Electronic Travel Authorisation (similar to ESTA) is being introduced. Likely a simple online process costing €7–15. Check dfa.ie before booking.

⚠ Check Before You Book

ETA requirements are still evolving. Always verify current entry rules at dfa.ie for your specific nationality before finalising flights.

As you travel to Ireland, ensure you have the necessary documents and travel insurance for a smooth experience.

Many travellers opt to travel to Ireland via air, as it offers the quickest route to explore the beautiful landscapes.

Getting Here by Air

Flight Options to Ireland

Ireland's three main international airports serve different regions. Choose the one closest to your trailhead to minimise ground travel time.

🛬

Dublin Airport

Ireland's largest hub. Most transatlantic and European routes land here. Perfect for the Wicklow Way.

🛬

Cork Airport

Ideal for the Kerry Way and Dingle Way. Fewer connections but no onward transfer needed.

🛬

Shannon Airport

Gateway to the west — good for the Burren and Wild Atlantic Way routes. Less crowded than Dublin.

Flight Booking Tips

When planning your trip, make sure to book early to secure the best deals for flights and accommodations to travel to Ireland.

  • Book 6–8 weeks ahead for the best balance of price and seat availability
  • Fly Tuesday or Wednesday — typically cheaper than weekend departures
  • Morning or afternoon arrivals give you time to settle before an early night
  • Late afternoon departures on your last day allow a final morning walk
  • Consider flight insurance — if injured on the trail, coverage protects your return flight

Alternative Routes

Ferry Options

If travelling from the UK or mainland Europe, ferries offer a relaxed alternative to flying — and many walkers enjoy the crossing as part of the adventure.

Whether embarking on a solo journey or with friends, knowing how to travel to Ireland opens the door to unforgettable experiences.

Getting to the Trail

Airport to Trailhead Transfers

Dublin Airport → Wicklow Way

Cork / Shannon → Kerry Way & Dingle Way

💡 Pro Tip

Most walkers spend one night in Dublin before heading to the trailhead. It eases jet lag, lets you pick up any forgotten gear, and means you start your walk refreshed rather than rushed.

Finally, remember that every step you take while you travel to Ireland will lead you to unforgettable memories.

On the Road

Driving in Ireland

  • Left-side driving: Ireland drives on the left like the UK and Australia. Allow significant adjustment time if you are unfamiliar.
  • Narrow country roads: Rural roads are considerably narrower than North American highways — reduce your speed accordingly.
  • International Driving Permit: US travellers can drive with a US licence, but an IDP (from AAA) provides useful backup.
  • Car insurance: Rental cars include basic cover; an excess waiver (~€10–20/day) is advisable.
  • Parking: Free at trailheads and in small villages. Can be expensive in Dublin and Cork city centres.

When to Go

Best Time to Visit Ireland for Walking

Most Popular

Peak Season

June – August

Most reliable weather and longest daylight, but highest prices and busiest trails. Book 2–3 months ahead.

Sweet Spot

Shoulder Seasons

May & September–October

Excellent weather, fewer crowds, better pricing. Mid-May to June 1 and all of September are particularly ideal. Book 6–8 weeks ahead.

Experienced Only

Autumn & Winter

October – February

Dramatic skies and autumn colour, very few other walkers. Weather less predictable. For experienced walkers only.

What to Bring

Packing & Preparation

Essential Walking Gear

One essential part of your adventure is to make sure you are prepared to travel to Ireland, ensuring you have everything you need.

  • 🧥 Waterproof jacket (non-negotiable)
  • 👢 Broken-in hiking boots
  • 🧦 Two pairs of walking socks
  • 🧣 Thermal layers
  • 🧢 Hat and gloves (even in summer)
  • 🌞 Sunscreen
  • 🩹 First aid basics
  • 🔌 Type G power adapter (UK plug)

The Two-Bag Approach

🎒

Day Backpack (15–20L)

Water, snacks, a waterproof layer, your camera. This is what you carry on the trail each day.

🧳

Main Luggage

Clothes and overnight kit. On a Walking Holiday Ireland tour, this is transferred between accommodations every day — you never carry it on the trail.

Get Trail-Ready

Physical Training Timeline

Ireland's long-distance trails involve 15–20 km per day with elevation gain. Moderate fitness is sufficient, but preparation makes all the difference — especially on day 3 and beyond.

Your 3-Month Training Plan

When you travel to Ireland, consider the best times to visit for optimal weather and fewer crowds.

Managing the Journey

Jet Lag Strategies

  • Arrive morning or afternoon if possible — gives you time to settle before Irish bedtime
  • Stay awake your first day — light activity and eating at local times resets your rhythm fastest
  • Walk on your first full day — physical activity is one of the most effective jet lag remedies
  • Get morning sunshine — daylight is the most powerful circadian reset available
  • Start walking after 1–2 rest days — arriving early means you begin the trail refreshed, not exhausted

Admin & Insurance

Documents & Travel Insurance

📄

Essential Documents

Valid passport · Travel insurance · Flight & accommodation confirmations · Walking tour booking · Credit cards & euros · Driver's licence if hiring a car

☁️

Digital Backups

Store copies of your passport, insurance, and all confirmations in a cloud service accessible offline in an emergency.

🛡️

Travel Insurance

Strongly recommended. Covers trip cancellation, emergency evacuation, lost luggage, and medical expenses. Typically €30–80 for a 1–2 week trip.

When You Land

Arrival Day Logistics

Resist the urge to start walking immediately. Two easy days before your first trail day transforms the entire experience.

Day 1

Arrival Day

  • Clear immigration & collect bags
  • Transfer to accommodation
  • Early dinner and rest
  • Early night — resist jet lag temptation

Day 2

Orientation Day

  • Light walk or local exploration
  • Finalise gear and layers
  • Good meal and early night
  • Review route maps and notes

Day 3

Trail Start 🥾

  • Begin your walking holiday
  • Full energy, adjusted rhythm
  • Enjoy every single step

Your Roadmap

Planning Steps: Start to Trail

  1. Choose your trail — Kerry Way, Dingle Way, Wicklow Way, Barrow Way, Causeway Coast, or a combination.
  2. Decide your dates — May–September is optimal. Book 6–8 weeks ahead at minimum.
  3. Book your flights — Secure airfares early; prices rise significantly within 4 weeks of departure.
  4. Book your walking tour — This locks in accommodation, luggage transfers, and route notes in one step.
  5. Arrange airport transfers — Co-ordinate with your tour operator or book independently.
  6. Sort your documentation — ETA if required, travel insurance, and digital backups of all confirmations.
  7. Begin your training — Follow the 3-month plan above. Your legs will thank you on day 3 of the Kerry Way.
  8. Pack strategically — Day pack for the trail, main luggage for transfers. Lighter is always better.
  9. Arrive, rest, and walk — Two easy days before the trail. Then enjoy every step of Ireland's finest landscapes.
