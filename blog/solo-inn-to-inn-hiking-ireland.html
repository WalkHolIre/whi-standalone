What Is Inn to Inn Hiking?

Inn to inn hiking means walking a multi-day trail where you stay at different accommodation each night. Instead of carrying a tent, sleeping bag, and cooking gear, you walk with a light daypack and arrive at a warm guesthouse or B&B each evening. Your main luggage is transferred between accommodations by our support vehicle.

This style of walking is popular across Europe — the Camino de Santiago in Spain, the Tour du Mont Blanc in the Alps, and inn to inn hiking europe routes through Italy and Austria all follow the same principle. Ireland's version has its own character. The distances are manageable, the trails pass through villages with pubs and shops, and the accommodation is locally owned and personally run. You're not staying in hostels or refuges — you're sleeping in comfortable rooms in family homes where the owners know the trails and have stories to tell.

A typical day on an inn to inn walking tour in Ireland looks like this: breakfast at your B&B, collect your packed lunch and route notes, walk 15 to 25 km along the trail, arrive at your next accommodation in the late afternoon, shower and change, then head to the local pub for dinner and a pint. Your bag is already in your room when you arrive. It's a rhythm that becomes addictive quickly.

Best Inn to Inn Routes in Ireland

Ireland has some of the best inn to inn hiking trails in Europe. Here are the routes we recommend most.

The Wicklow Way is Ireland's oldest waymarked trail and one of the finest introductions to inn to inn walking. The route runs 127 km through the Wicklow Mountains south of Dublin, passing through Glendalough, over mountain passes, and through quiet farming valleys. Our 5-day version covers the best sections with comfortable guesthouses at each stop.

The Kerry Way is a 215 km circuit around the Iveragh Peninsula in County Kerry. It's one of the most scenic long-distance walks in Europe, with mountain, coastal, and lakeside scenery changing with every stage. The villages along the route — Killarney, Kenmare, Sneem, Waterville, Cahersiveen — are full of character and great accommodation.

The Dingle Way circles the Dingle Peninsula through some of the most dramatic coastal scenery in Ireland. At 179 km over 8 days, it takes you past ancient beehive huts, along cliff-top paths, and through Irish-speaking villages. The accommodation along the Dingle Way is excellent — small family-run B&Bs where the welcome is genuine.

The Beara Way is the quieter alternative to the Kerry Way, running through the wilder and less-visited Beara Peninsula. The scenery is raw and beautiful — mountains dropping straight to the sea, quiet fishing villages, and some of the most remote walking in southern Ireland.

The Burren Way in County Clare takes you through one of the most unusual landscapes in Europe — a limestone karst region with rare wildflowers, ancient dolmens, and views across Galway Bay. At 4 to 5 days, it's a shorter option that packs in extraordinary scenery.

What a Typical Day Looks Like

To give you a concrete picture, here's what a day on the Wicklow Way might look like for a solo inn to inn walker.

You wake at your B&B in Roundwood, have a full Irish breakfast — eggs, bacon, toast, tea — and pick up the packed lunch your host has prepared. You lace up your boots, shoulder your daypack, and step out the door by 9 or 9:30am. The trail climbs through farmland and onto open hillside, with views across the Wicklow Mountains opening up as you gain height. By mid-morning you're walking along a ridge with nobody else in sight.

You stop for lunch at a stream crossing, eat your sandwiches on a rock, and watch the clouds move across the valley. The afternoon takes you through forest and down to Glendalough, where the monastic ruins and twin lakes sit in a glacial valley between the mountains. By 4pm you're at your next B&B, your bag is in your room, and a hot shower washes the day's walking away. That evening you walk to the village pub, order a bowl of stew and a pint, and find yourself talking to the couple at the next table who walked the same route from the other direction.

That's the magic of inn to inn hiking. Every day is different, but the rhythm stays the same.

Planning Your Trip

The best time to visit Ireland for inn to inn hiking is April to October. May, June, and September are the best months — long daylight hours, moderate temperatures, and quieter trails. July and August are warmer but busier on the most popular routes.

The Irish weather is changeable. Pack waterproofs and layers regardless of season. A good rain jacket and waterproof trousers are essential — you will get rained on at some point, and being prepared makes the difference between a memorable day and a miserable one.

For first-time inn to inn hikers, we recommend starting with the Wicklow Way or a shorter section of the Kerry Way. These routes have well-maintained trails, manageable daily distances, and frequent villages. As your confidence grows, the Beara Way and longer Kerry Way sections offer more challenge and remoteness.

What is inn to inn hiking?

Inn to inn hiking is a form of multi-day walking where you stay at different accommodation each night while walking marked trails. Unlike traditional backpacking, your luggage is transferred ahead by vehicle and you walk with only a light daypack. You wake up at a guesthouse or B&B each morning, walk 15-25 km during the day, and arrive at comfortable accommodation each evening. This style combines the challenge and freedom of long-distance walking with the comfort of sleeping in warm beds and enjoying warm meals at traditional Irish guesthouses.

Is Ireland safe for solo hiking?

Yes, Ireland is one of the safest countries in Europe for solo walking. The long-distance trails are well-waymarked, villages are welcoming, and you're never more than a few kilometres from a road or settlement. When walking with Walking Holiday Ireland, you receive detailed route notes with maps, distances, and emergency contacts. You carry your phone with our local support number, and assistance is always available if you need it. Ireland's reputation for hospitality extends to solo walkers, and both the trail community and local pub culture are naturally welcoming.

What do I need to carry on an inn to inn walk?

You only need to carry a daypack weighing 5-8 kg. This should contain water, snacks, a rain jacket, extra layers, waterproof trousers, sun protection, your phone and charger, personal medications, and any items you need during the day. Your main luggage, including clothes for the evening, toiletries, and spare shoes, is transferred between accommodations by our support vehicle and will be in your room when you arrive. Good hiking boots that are well broken in are essential, as are waterproofs given Ireland's changeable weather.

What is the best inn to inn route for beginners?

The Wicklow Way is the best route for first-time inn to inn hikers. It's Ireland's oldest long-distance trail, running 127 km through the Wicklow Mountains with manageable daily distances of 15-20 km. The trail is well-maintained and clearly waymarked, passes through villages with shops and pubs, and offers excellent family-run B&B accommodation at each stop. Alternatively, shorter sections of the Kerry Way are also ideal for beginners. Both routes have established accommodation networks and well-developed infrastructure for walking holidays, making them perfect for learning the rhythm of inn to inn hiking.
