Difference Between Hiking and Walking: A Guide from the Irish Trails

People ask me this all the time. They land on our website, browse the trips, and then send an email: "Cliff, is this walking or hiking? I'm not sure I'm fit enough for hiking."

Fair question. And the answer matters — because the difference between hiking and walking shapes what you wear, what you carry, and how hard your legs work at the end of the day.

My name is Cliff, and I run Walking Holiday Ireland. I've spent years guiding people along trails from the Kerry Way to the Wicklow Mountains, and I've seen first-hand how this simple question trips people up. So let me clear it up — from someone who does both, every single week, on Irish ground.

What Is Walking?

Walking is moving on foot at a normal pace, on easy ground. Think footpaths, park trails, paved roads, flat beaches. You can walk in your normal shoes. You don't need a map. You don't need a backpack full of gear.

A morning stroll through a village. A lap of the local park. A wander along a canal towpath. That's walking.

It's simple, it's low-impact, and it's something most people can do every day without thinking twice. Walking is the base. It's where everyone starts.

But don't let the simplicity fool you. Walking is real exercise. A brisk 30-minute walk burns calories, lowers blood pressure, lifts your mood, and keeps your joints moving. The benefits of hiking page on our site covers the health side in detail — and most of those gains start with walking.

What Is Hiking?

Hiking is walking's tougher cousin. It means heading out on trails in natural terrain — hills, mountains, forests, bogs, coastal cliffs — where the ground is rough, the elevation changes, and you need proper gear to do it safely.

A hike takes you off the pavement and into the wild. You climb. You descend. You scramble over rocks and wade through mud. You carry water, food, and layers because the weather can change fast — especially in Ireland, where the Irish weather has a mind of its own.

That's the hiking meaning in a nutshell: planned travel on foot through varied, natural terrain that tests your body and rewards you with views, fresh air, and a real sense of achievement.

Hiking vs Walking: The Key Differences

So what sets a hike apart from a walk? Here are the main factors:

Terrain. Walking happens on flat, even ground — pavements, park paths, boardwalks. Hiking happens on uneven, natural terrain — rocky trails, bog paths, mountain ridges. When you hike the Connemara trails, you're on a different surface with every step.

Elevation. Walks stay flat or follow gentle slopes. Hikes climb. Some of our routes gain 500 metres or more in a single day. Climbing Croagh Patrick is a hike. Walking through Galway city is a walk.

Distance and time. Most walks last 30 minutes to two hours. Hikes tend to run from three hours to a full day. A day on the Kerry Way might cover 20 km over six or seven hours.

Gear. Walking needs comfortable shoes and not much else. Hiking needs proper hiking boots with grip and ankle support, a daypack with water and food, waterproofs, and often a map or GPS. Our packing list covers everything you need.

Effort. Walking is gentle. You can chat, take photos, stop for coffee. Hiking pushes your heart rate up, works your legs harder, and burns far more calories. Both are good exercise — but hiking builds real strength and stamina.

Planning. You can walk out the door and go. Hiking takes planning — you need to know the route, check conditions, pack the right gear, and prepare properly. That planning is part of the fun.

Where It Gets Blurry

I'll be honest — in Ireland, the line between walking and hiking isn't always clear. We call our trips "walking holidays" because that's the tradition here. In Ireland and the UK, "walking" covers everything from a stroll to a mountain day. In North America, anything on a trail gets called a "hike."

Our self-guided walking holidays include both. Some days are gentle — flat coastal paths, easy forest trails, nature walks through green valleys. Other days are proper hikes — mountain passes, steep descents, ridge walks with the Atlantic wind in your face.

We grade every route so you know exactly what you're getting into. Our tour grading system rates each day from easy to tough, so there are no surprises.

What About Trekking?

People also mix up hiking and trekking — and they're different again. Trekking means multi-day travel on foot, usually with overnight stops. A single day on a mountain trail is a hike. A week-long route with inn-to-inn stays is a trek.

Most of our trips are treks by this meaning — you walk from place to place over several days, with your bags moved ahead. We've written about the differences between hiking and trekking if you want the full breakdown.

There's also rambling — a more casual, less planned form of countryside walking. We cover rambling vs hiking as well.

Which Is Better for You?

Neither is better. Both are great. The right choice depends on your goals, your fitness, and what kind of day you want.

Choose walking if you want daily exercise that fits into your routine, you're starting out, or you prefer easy, social outings with no special gear. Walking is the foundation — it's how you build the fitness that lets you hike later.

Choose hiking if you want a challenge, you love being in wild places, and you're ready to put in some effort for big rewards. Hiking takes you to places you can't reach by car — mountain tops, hidden valleys, cliff edges with views that make you catch your breath.

Or do both. Most of our guests walk daily at home and hike on holiday. That's a great balance. Walking keeps you fit between trips. Hiking gives you the adventure.

If you're over 50 and wondering if hiking is realistic, it absolutely is. We run walking holidays for over 50s that match routes to your level. Plenty of our guests are in their 60s and 70s, out on the trails every day.

Getting Started: From Walker to Hiker

The shift from walking to hiking is easier than you think. Here's what I tell people:

Start by adding hills to your regular walks. Walk faster. Go longer. Build up to five or six miles on mixed ground. Once that feels good, try a short, easy hike — somewhere with a marked trail and gentle climbs.

Get proper boots. This matters more than anything else. Cheap shoes on wet Irish rock is a recipe for a twisted ankle. Our guide to choosing hiking boots will help you pick the right pair.

Try a guided walking holiday for your first big trip. A guide handles the route, the pace, and the tricky bits — so you can focus on the walk and the views. Once you've got a trip or two under your belt, self-guided trips open up the whole country.

The Best of Both Worlds in Ireland

Ireland is one of the best places on earth to experience both walking and hiking. You can walk through a charming village in the morning and hike a mountain trail in the afternoon.

The Wicklow Way mixes forest walks with mountain hiking. The Beara Way takes you along quiet roads and over rugged passes. Hiking in Donegal ranges from gentle beach strolls to serious mountain days.

Ireland's six national parks all have trails for walkers and hikers alike. And the Wild Atlantic Way — 2,500 km of coastal scenery — is the backdrop for some of the finest trail walking and hiking you'll find anywhere.

The best time to visit Ireland for both walking and hiking is April through October. But we run trips year-round for those who don't mind a bit of rain.

Walk or Hike — Just Get Out There

The difference between hiking and walking comes down to terrain, effort, and gear. Walking is easy, flat, and needs nothing special. Hiking is tougher, wilder, and takes proper kit. Both are brilliant for your body and your head.

Don't worry about labels. Worry about getting outside. Whether you call it a walk or a hike, every step on a trail is time well spent.

Ready to find out which suits you? Browse our walking tours and get in touch. We'll match you with the right route — from gentle nature walks to full-day mountain hikes — wherever you are on your journey.

Frequently Asked Questions

What kind of boots should I wear for hiking in Ireland?

Well-fitted, waterproof hiking boots are essential. Start breaking them in 8–10 weeks before your trip, and test them in wet and uneven conditions like Irish terrain. Many experienced hikers also carry blister treatment just in case. For walking on flat paths, comfortable walking shoes are fine.

How fit do I need to be for a walking holiday in Ireland?

Most walkers can cover 15–20 km a day over moderate terrain. Routes like the Kerry Way and Dingle Way need good stamina and hill-walking experience. Gentler options include the Barrow Way and Burren Way. Be honest about your fitness and check the elevation profiles before you book.

What is the difference between a self-guided and a guided walking tour?

On a self-guided tour, you walk on your own using our maps, route notes, and GPS app, with luggage moved each day and 24/7 support on call. A guided tour gives you a local expert who walks with you, shares stories and local knowledge, and keeps you safe and on track.

Are trekking poles recommended for hiking?

Trekking poles are optional but helpful, especially on long descents where they take pressure off your knees. They also give extra balance on uneven or boggy ground. Try them on training walks first before bringing them on your trip.

What are the most essential items to pack for a walking holiday?

The must-haves are: a quality waterproof jacket and trousers, well-fitted hiking boots, merino wool or synthetic base layers (not cotton), a daypack, maps and compass or GPS, sun protection, and a fully charged power bank. Check our full packing list for everything you need.
