My name is Cliff, and I run Walking Holiday Ireland. Killarney National Park is the starting point and finishing point of the Kerry Way, and it's one of the finest places to walk in Ireland. Covering over 10,000 hectares of mountains, lakes, and ancient oakwoods in County Kerry, the park has been a protected landscape since 1932 — making it Ireland's first and oldest national park. It was also designated a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve in 1981.

The park sits at the foot of the MacGillycuddy's Reeks, Ireland's highest mountain range, and contains the famous Lakes of Killarney — Lough Leane, Muckross Lake, and Upper Lake. Whether you're looking for an easy lakeside stroll, a mountain hike with panoramic views, or a multi-day walking holiday through wild Kerry landscape, this park delivers. Here's everything you need to know about walking in this remarkable corner of Ireland.

Best Walks in Killarney National Park

The park has walks for every level of fitness and ambition. Here are the routes I recommend most.

Muckross Lake Loop

The Muckross Lake Loop is a 10 km circuit around Muckross Lake, passing through ancient oakwoods, along the lakeshore, and past Muckross House — a Victorian mansion set in beautiful gardens. The trail is mostly flat and well surfaced, making it one of the most accessible walks in the park. Allow 2.5 to 3 hours at a comfortable pace.

The old yew woodland along the shore is one of the rarest habitats in Europe. These gnarled, ancient trees grow right down to the water's edge, creating an atmosphere that feels prehistoric. It's a magical stretch of walking, particularly in early morning light.

Torc Waterfall and Torc Mountain

Torc Waterfall is one of the park's most popular attractions — a 20-metre cascade tumbling through oak and holly woodland just off the N71 road. The walk to the waterfall from the car park takes about 10 minutes and is suitable for everyone.

For a longer outing, continue up the Old Kenmare Road to the summit of Torc Mountain at 535 metres. The climb takes about 1.5 hours from the waterfall and rewards you with spectacular views over all three lakes and across to the Reeks. This is one of the most rewarding short mountain walks in Kerry.

Gap of Dunloe

The Gap of Dunloe is a narrow mountain pass between the MacGillycuddy's Reeks and the Purple Mountain group. The walk through the Gap follows a road that becomes increasingly wild as you climb, with steep mountainsides rising on either side and a chain of five lakes in the valley below. The full walk from Kate Kearney's Cottage to the Black Valley and Lord Brandon's Cottage is about 11 km one way.

This is one of the most dramatic walks in the park. The scenery is raw and magnificent, especially when cloud sits on the mountain tops and the light changes on the lakes. Many walkers combine the Gap with a boat trip back through the lakes to Ross Castle, making a full-day excursion.

Blue Pool Nature Trail

The Blue Pool trail near Muckross is a gentle 2 km loop through woodland to a stunning natural pool where the water appears deep blue due to the limestone bedrock. It's an easy family walk and a peaceful retreat from the busier parts of the park. The trail passes through mixed woodland with good bird-watching opportunities year-round.

Old Kenmare Road

The Old Kenmare Road is a historic route that once connected Killarney and Kenmare before the modern N71 was built. The full route runs about 20 km through mountain passes and river valleys, but you can walk shorter sections as day walks. The eastern stretch from Torc Waterfall climbs gradually through oak and holly woodland before opening onto open mountain with views south towards the Caha Mountains. The surface is rough in places — proper hiking boots are essential — but the sense of walking along a road that people have used for centuries gives this route a character that newer trails lack.

Kerry Way Through the Park

The Kerry Way long-distance trail passes through the heart of the park on its first and last stages. The section from Killarney through the Black Valley to the head of Upper Lake is one of the most beautiful stages of any long-distance walk in Ireland. Ancient oakwoods, mountain passes, lakeshores, and the old butter road combine to create a day of walking that stays with you for years.

Our self-guided walking holidays on the Kerry Way include this stretch, with luggage transfer and handpicked accommodation in Killarney and the Black Valley.

Wildlife in the Park

Killarney National Park is home to Ireland's only native herd of red deer — animals that have roamed these mountains continuously since the end of the last Ice Age. You'll often spot them grazing on hillsides, particularly in early morning and late afternoon. During the autumn rut in October, the sound of stags roaring across the valleys is unforgettable.

White-tailed eagles were reintroduced to the park in 2007 and the population is growing. With a wingspan of up to 2.4 metres, these are enormous and impressive birds. Keep your eyes on the sky above the lakes and you may be rewarded.

The ancient oak and yew woodlands support a rich community of bird life including treecreepers, jays, long-eared owls, and woodpeckers. The lakes hold otters, and the rivers are home to salmon and trout. The park's mild, wet climate supports an extraordinary variety of mosses, ferns, and lichens — walking through the oakwoods feels like entering a temperate rainforest.

The Killarney oakwoods are among the most important native woodlands left in Ireland. Sessile oaks, holly, and arbutus — the strawberry tree — form a canopy found almost nowhere else in northern Europe. The arbutus is a Mediterranean species that somehow thrives in Kerry's mild, wet conditions, and its orange-red bark and white bell-shaped flowers make it easy to spot along lakeside trails. In autumn, its red strawberry-like fruits add a splash of colour to the woodland floor.

Ross Castle and Muckross House

Two of the park's most visited landmarks are worth building a walking day around. Ross Castle is a 15th-century tower house on a peninsula jutting into Lough Leane. You can walk to the castle from Killarney town centre in about 40 minutes along a flat, tree-lined path that follows the lakeshore. The castle itself is open for guided tours in summer, and the surrounding area is a popular starting point for boat trips on the lake. The views across Lough Leane to the mountains beyond are among the finest in Kerry.

Muckross House is a Victorian mansion set in 60 acres of landscaped gardens near the shores of Muckross Lake. The house is now a museum of Kerry life, and the gardens are free to explore year-round. The walled garden, water garden, and rock garden are beautifully maintained, and the surrounding parkland connects to most of the park's walking trails. Many walkers use Muckross as a base for a full day, combining the gardens with the lake loop and the Blue Pool trail.

Practical Information

Killarney National Park is free to enter and open year-round. The main access points are Muckross House and Gardens, the Torc Waterfall car park, and Kate Kearney's Cottage at the Gap of Dunloe. Killarney town is the main base, with a train station served by direct trains from Dublin and Cork.

The best time to visit Ireland for walking in the park is April to October. May and September are particularly good — warm enough for comfortable walking, with fewer visitors than the peak summer months. The Irish weather in Kerry is mild and wet, so a waterproof jacket is essential any time of year.

For mountain walks like Torc Mountain and the Gap of Dunloe, wear proper hiking boots and carry waterproofs, water, and food. Our packing list covers everything you need. Check our tour grading to match the walk to your fitness level.

Walking Holidays from Killarney

Killarney is the perfect base for a walking holiday in Kerry. The town has a wide range of accommodation, excellent restaurants, and lively pubs with traditional music most nights. From here, you can walk the Kerry Way, explore the national park, or take day trips to the Dingle Peninsula, the Ring of Kerry, and the Skellig coast.

Our guided walking holidays from Killarney pair you with local guides who know every trail, every viewpoint, and every story behind the landscape. Our self-guided options give you the freedom to walk at your own pace with all the logistics handled — accommodation, luggage transfer, route notes, and local support.

Whether you spend a day or a week here, you'll understand why this corner of Ireland has been drawing visitors for over two centuries. The combination of mountain, lake, and forest scenery with one of the richest wildlife habitats in the country makes it a place that rewards every visit.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the best time to visit Killarney National Park?

The best time to visit Killarney National Park for walking is April to October, with May and September being particularly ideal. These months offer warm enough conditions for comfortable walking while avoiding the peak summer crowds. The park's mild, wet climate means waterproof jackets are essential year-round.

How long is the Muckross Lake Loop walk?

The Muckross Lake Loop is a 10 km circuit that takes approximately 2.5 to 3 hours at a comfortable pace. The trail is mostly flat and well surfaced, passing through ancient oakwoods, along the lakeshore, and past Muckross House, making it one of the most accessible walks in the park.

Where can I see red deer in Killarney National Park?

Killarney is home to Ireland's only native herd of red deer. You'll often spot them grazing on hillsides, particularly during early morning and late afternoon hours. October is an especially rewarding time to visit, as the autumn rut brings the sound of stags roaring across the valleys — an unforgettable experience.

Is Killarney National Park free to visit?

Yes, Killarney National Park is free to enter and is open year-round. The main access points are Muckross House and Gardens, the Torc Waterfall car park, and Kate Kearney's Cottage at the Gap of Dunloe. Killarney town, served by direct trains from Dublin and Cork, is the main base for visitors.

What should I bring for a mountain walk in Killarney?

For mountain walks like Torc Mountain and the Gap of Dunloe, bring proper hiking boots with good grip. Since Kerry's weather is mild and wet, a waterproof jacket is essential any time of year. Always carry water, food, and check weather conditions before setting out. Our packing list covers everything you need for a safe and comfortable walk.
