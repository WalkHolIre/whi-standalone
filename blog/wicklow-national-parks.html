Wicklow Mountains National Park: A Walker's Hiking Guide

An hour south of Dublin, the city ends and the mountains begin. Not gradually — there's a moment on the road south through Enniskerry or Blessington when the landscape simply changes register and the Wicklow uplands take over. Open granite moorland, heather ridges, wooded valleys, and the blue-grey shapes of the mountains ahead.

Wicklow Mountains National Park covers 20,000 hectares of this landscape, making it the largest national park in the Republic of Ireland. It contains Ireland's most walked long-distance trail, one of the country's most significant early Christian monastic sites, three major waterfalls, and walking trails that range from a gentle 45-minute valley circuit to full-day mountain ridge traverses.

I've been walking in Wicklow for years, running self-guided tours through the park's best sections. This guide covers what's actually worth your time — the trails, the key landmarks, what to expect on the Wicklow Way, and how to get the most from the park depending on your fitness level and how long you have.

Glendalough: The Park's Heart

You could spend a full day in Glendalough and leave wanting more. Most people spend two hours and leave thinking they've seen it. There's a difference.

Gleann Dá Loch — the Valley of Two Lakes — is a glacially carved valley in the eastern Wicklow Mountains, occupied since the 6th century when Saint Kevin of Glendalough established a monastic settlement here that would grow into one of Ireland's most important mediaeval centres of learning. The round tower — 33 metres tall, still standing, dating to the 10th century — is one of the most recognisable structures in Ireland. The cathedral ruins, the stone churches, the high crosses: all genuine, all mediaeval, all within a ten-minute walk of each other.

The valley's scenery would justify the visit on its own. Two glacial lakes — the Lower Lake with the monastic settlement on its shore, the Upper Lake larger and wilder and surrounded by mountain cliffs — connected by forest paths of extraordinary beauty.

The Glendalough walks

Our dedicated Glendalough walks guide covers all the routes in detail. The quick summary:

Valley floor circuit (4.5 km, 90 minutes, easy): Around both lakes, past the monastic ruins, through the oak woodland. Perfect for families and anyone wanting the full Glendalough experience without significant elevation.

The Spinc (9 km, 3 to 4 hours, moderate): The ridge above the Upper Lake, approached via the mining road and returning along the lakeshore. The best single walk in the park — the views down into the valley from the ridge are outstanding and the contrast of open moorland above and ancient oak woodland below is uniquely Wicklow.

Camaderry (12 km, 4 to 5 hours, challenging): The mountain above Glendalough to the north, with panoramic views across the park and east to the Irish Sea. A full mountain day.

The early morning advice is genuinely worth following. The valley gets busy by 11am in summer. Before 9am — particularly on a weekday — you may have the upper lake largely to yourself. That experience is categorically different from the midday version.

The Wicklow Way

The Wicklow Way runs 127 km from Marlay Park in Dublin south to Clonegal in County Carlow — Ireland's first and most walked long-distance trail, passing through the heart of the national park on its central sections.

Most people don't walk the whole thing. They walk sections of it, or they join a multi-day tour that covers the best parts. Either approach works well.

The park sections of the Wicklow Way are the finest. The ridge crossing above Glenmalure. The descent into Glendalough. The open moorland above Roundwood where the trail feels genuinely remote despite being less than an hour's drive from Dublin. These are sections that stay with you.

The Waterfalls

The original article mentions three waterfalls. Here's the honest version of each:

Powerscourt Waterfall

At 121 metres, Powerscourt Waterfall is Ireland's highest single drop. It sits within the Powerscourt Estate grounds, which charges a small admission fee but includes parking, a café, and a children's play area. The walk from the car park to the waterfall takes about 20 minutes through mature woodland.

The waterfall is most impressive in winter and spring when water levels are high. In a dry August, it can be underwhelming. In full flow, it's genuinely dramatic.

Powerscourt is not accessed from within the national park boundary — it sits on the Powerscourt Estate, a separate entity. Worth knowing before you plan a combined visit.

Glenmacnass Waterfall

Glenmacnass Waterfall is a 50-metre cascade dropping into a dark plunge pool on the Military Road — the road that bisects the park north to south. It's roadside visible with a small car park, but the approach via the Glenmacnass Valley walk is far better. The valley above the falls is one of the quieter, less-visited sections of the park: open moorland, the Glenmacnass River, and the sense of genuine upland remoteness.

Glenealo Valley

The Glenealo Valley behind the Upper Lake at Glendalough leads to a series of cascades — not a single waterfall but a wild mountain river working its way down through the valley. Access from the Glendalough upper car park. Quiet, dramatic, and almost entirely missed by the day-trip visitor.

Wildlife and Landscape

The park's moorland is the defining habitat — vast stretches of blanket bog and heather-covered upland, one of the largest continuous expanses of this habitat in Ireland. In late summer (August to September) the heather turns purple across the ridges, a transformation that can make even a grey day in Wicklow worthwhile.

Red grouse breed on the moorland — you're more likely to hear the explosive go-back, go-back call before you see one. Peregrine falcons hunt the cliff faces above Glendalough and along the Wicklow ridges. Sika deer are common across the park, particularly at dusk in the valley areas. Keep a quiet eye on the lakes and rivers for otter — sightings are more frequent than most visitors expect.

The oak woodland at Glendalough is one of the few surviving fragments of ancient native woodland in Ireland — the 200-year-old sessile oaks along the Upper Lake path are genuinely ancient and the ecology of the woodland is distinct from planted commercial forest. Notice the difference.

When to Visit

May and June — the heather and gorse in full bloom, long evenings, manageable crowds. My preferred months for Wicklow. The park shows its best green in May after the winter rains.

September and October — the second-best window. Stable weather, golden light, autumn colour beginning in the valley woodlands from mid-October. The Spinc walk in October morning light is exceptional.

July and August — reliable weather but the park is at its busiest. Glendalough visitor numbers peak in these months. Start early and go to the Upper Lake sections if you want space.

Winter — the mountains are quiet and can be strikingly beautiful. The lower valley walks are fine year-round. High moorland routes require proper preparation — ice, short days, and limited visibility on the summits are real considerations.

Getting There and Practical Notes

By car: Glendalough is 53 km from Dublin city centre — approximately one hour via the N11 and R755. Parking fills early in summer; the upper car park is often better than the main visitor centre car park for access to the Upper Lake walks.

By public transport: The 133 St Kevin's Bus service runs from Dublin city to Glendalough — approximately 90 minutes, two departures per day. Check current timetables as services change seasonally. This is genuinely viable for a day walk.

What to bring: The standard Irish hiking kit applies — good waterproofs, broken-in boots, snacks and water. The park's elevation means weather can be significantly colder and wetter than Dublin even on the same day. Don't underestimate this.

Dogs: Permitted in the park but must be kept on leads in sensitive habitat areas. Check signage at trailheads for current restrictions.

Wicklow as Part of a Longer Walking Holiday

Wicklow sits at the northern end of the Ancient East walking area — the cultural heartland of Ireland that runs south through the Barrow Valley to the southeast. Walkers who want to combine the Wicklow Mountains with a longer self-guided walking holiday often pair the Wicklow Way with the Barrow Way, making a two-week south-to-north journey from Clonegal or St Mullins up to Dublin.
