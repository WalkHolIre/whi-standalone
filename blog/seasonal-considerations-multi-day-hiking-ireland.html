p>But here's the question I hear more than any other: "When should I go?"

The honest answer? Ireland rewards hikers in every season — but each one asks something different of you. Whether you're planning the Wicklow Way, the Kerry Way, or the wild coastline of the Antrim Glens and Causeway Coast, understanding the Irish weather will significantly impact your trip.

This guide covers every season — conditions, daylight, crowds, and gear — so you can choose the right time for your adventure.

Spring: Multi-Day Hiking in Ireland (March to May)

Spring is one of the best times for hiking in Ireland. The trails wake up. Wildflowers push through the hedgerows. Lambs appear in the fields along routes like St Kevin's Way and the Wicklow Hills.

Temperatures stay mild — typically 8–14°C — and daylight stretches out to 15 hours by May. You get proper walking days without the summer crowds.

What to Expect on the Trails

  • Trail conditions: Soft underfoot after winter rain. Expect some muddy sections, particularly in March and April.

  • Weather: Cool and changeable. Rain is likely, but heavy downpours are less common than in winter.

  • Crowds: Low to medium — popular routes like the Wicklow Way are quiet compared to summer.

  • Accommodation: Good availability. You can often book a few weeks in advance.

Spring Gear Checklist

  • Waterproof jacket and trousers

  • Merino wool base layer

  • Mid-layer fleece or lightweight down

  • Waterproof hiking boots with ankle support

  • Trekking poles (useful on soft ground)

I'd especially recommend spring for first-time walkers on the Wicklow Way. The light is soft, the scenery is fresh, and you have the trail almost to yourself.

Summer: Multi-Day Hiking in Ireland (June to August)

Summer is Ireland's most popular season for multi-day hiking tours. And it's simple to see why. Daylight runs until 10 pm in June. Temperatures sit comfortably in the mid-teens. Coastal routes like the Dingle Way and the Causeway Coast look spectacular in the long evening light.

That said, summer brings its own challenges. Trails get busy. Accommodation fills up fast — especially on the Kerry Way and Wicklow Way. Book early.

What to Expect on the Trails

  • Trail conditions: generally dry and firm. Some routes can be dusty in prolonged dry spells.

  • Weather: Warm but unpredictable. Rain showers can appear even in July, so don't leave waterproofs at home.

  • Crowds: High on popular routes. The Wicklow Way and Kerry Way are busiest in July and August.

  • Accommodation: High demand — book at least 3–4 months in advance for peak dates.

Summer Gear Checklist

  • Lightweight waterproof jacket

  • Sun protection (hat, sunscreen, sunglasses)

  • Moisture-wicking base layers

  • Trail runners or lightweight hiking boots

  • Good hiking snacks — longer days mean more energy burnt.

Insider tip: If you're walking the Kerry Way in summer, book your accommodation in Caherdaniel and Waterville well ahead. These small villages fill up quickly, and the stretch between them is one of the finest days of coastal walking in all of Ireland.

Autumn: Multi-Day Hiking in Ireland (September to November)

Autumn is my personal favourite season for multi-day hiking in Ireland. The summer crowds thin out. The light turns golden. The hillsides along Wicklow National Park burn with copper and rust.

September and early October offer near-perfect hiking conditions — drier than spring, cooler than summer, and quieter than both. You get the best of everything.

What to Expect on the Trails

  • Trail conditions: dry and firm in September. Paths get muddier through October and November as rain increases.

  • Weather: Cool and crisp, with Atlantic winds picking up from October. Rain showers are more frequent than in summer but manageable.

  • Crowds: Low — October is one of the quietest periods on the trails.

  • Daylight: Days shorten noticeably from October. Plan your stages around available light.

Autumn Gear Checklist

  • Waterproof jacket and trousers

  • Warm mid-layer (fleece or softshell)

  • Waterproof hiking boots

  • Headtorch for early finishes

  • Layering system — temperatures can swing 8–10°C in a single day

Autumn is ideal for the Kerry Way 8-day tour. The light on the Iveragh Peninsula in September is genuinely special, and availability is far better than in summer.

Winter: Multi-Day Hiking in Ireland (December to February)

Winter hiking in Ireland is not for everyone. Short days, cold winds, icy paths, and limited services on remote routes make it a tougher experience. But for the right walker, it offers something no other season can: complete solitude and landscapes that feel truly wild.

The Antrim Coast in winter, with waves crashing below the cliffs and frost on the heather, is a scene you won't forget.

What to Expect on the Trails

  • Trail conditions: Can be muddy, icy, or waterlogged. Check conditions before you start each day.

  • Weather: Cold, wet, and windy. Snow is possible on higher ground. Temperatures drop to 4–9°C.

  • Crowds: Very low — you may walk for hours without seeing another person.

  • Daylight: Short days — sunrise around 8:30 am, sunset by 4:00–4:30 pm. Plan shorter stages.

Winter Gear Checklist

  • Heavyweight waterproof jacket and trousers

  • Thermal base layers

  • Insulated mid-layer

  • Waterproof hiking boots with warm lining

  • Gaiters (for muddy or snowy conditions)

  • Microspikes (for icy paths)

  • Headtorch — essential

Spring or autumn is a better option for most walkers contemplating a winter trip. If you're set on winter, stick to lower-level routes and prepare thoroughly.

Understanding Irish Weather: What You Actually Need to Know

Ireland's weather is famously changeable. You can have brilliant sunshine, a rain shower, and a rainbow within the same hour. That's not a cliché — it's Tuesday.

The key is preparation, not prediction. Here's what I always tell walkers planning their first multi-day hiking trip in Ireland:

  1. Check the forecast the night before — not just the morning of. Met Éireann (Ireland's national weather service) is the most reliable source for Irish conditions.

  2. Pack for all four seasons, every day. Even in the summer, a waterproof layer is non-negotiable.

  3. Wind matters more than rain. A 20 km/h headwind on the Dingle Peninsula makes a grey day feel much harder than it looks on paper.

  4. Learn to read the weather — cloud shapes, wind direction, and barometric pressure can all give you early warning of what's coming.

For a full guide to staying safe in changing conditions, read our article on weather safety for hikers in Ireland.

How to Prepare for Multi-Day Hiking in Ireland

Good preparation is the difference between a fantastic trip and a miserable one — regardless of which season you choose. Here's a practical starting point:

Before You Go

  • Build your fitness with regular training hikes in the months before your trip

  • Study the route and daily distances — know what you're committing to

  • Break in your hiking boots at least 4–6 weeks before departure

  • Pack your backpacking checklist carefully — weight adds up fast over multi-day routes

On the Trail

  • Start early each day to build in buffer time

  • Eat before you're hungry, drink before you're thirsty

  • Tell someone your daily itinerary — or let us do it as part of your booking

  • Carry a charged phone with offline maps downloaded

We walk every route ourselves before each season. Our GPS navigation app—loaded with your specific route—takes the guesswork out of navigation, no matter the weather.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best time of year to walk in Ireland?
Summer (June-August) is the most popular season with temperatures reaching 18-20C, daylight extending until 10pm, and less frequent rainfall. However, trails are busier and accommodation books up quickly. Autumn (September-October) is considered ideal by many experienced walkers with fewer crowds, stunning golden landscapes, and generally stable weather in September. Spring (April-May) offers fresh, uncrowded trails. Winter walking is possible but demands experience.
Are there any seasonal challenges or risks associated with multi-day hiking in Ireland?

Yes, during certain seasons such as winter or early spring, you may encounter cold temperatures, strong winds, rain showers, and muddy trails. Before setting out, it's crucial to prepare and check local weather forecasts.

Are there any specific gear or clothing recommendations for multi-day hiking in Ireland?

It is important to have waterproof and breathable clothing, sturdy hiking boots with excellent traction, a 20L-30L backpack with proper support, and layers of clothing to adapt to changing weather conditions on the trail.

Are insects a problem while multi-day hiking in Ireland?

Not really. Ireland's Atlantic climate keeps most biting insects at bay. Midges exist but are far less aggressive than in Scotland — coastal routes like the Dingle Way and Kerry Way rarely have issues thanks to the sea breeze. Ticks are present in long grass and heather, so a quick check after boggy sections is sensible. A small tube of insect repellent is worth packing for summer trips, but insects won't define your experience. Ireland's weather is a far bigger consideration than its wildlife.

Is winter hiking in Ireland suitable for beginners?
Winter is not recommended for inexperienced walkers. Daylight is very limited (sunset as early as 4pm in December), temperatures hover around 8C, trails can be muddy and slippery, and some remote sections are isolated. Winter hiking is best suited to experienced walkers who are comfortable with challenging conditions and shorter daylight hours.
Can I hike all year round in Ireland?

Yes. Ireland's mild climate means trails are open and walkable in every month.

Summer (June–August) brings the longest days and best visibility, but also the most crowds.

Spring and autumn are ideal — quieter trails, softer light, and temperatures that suit long days on your feet.

Winter hiking is absolutely possible on lower-level routes, but shorter daylight hours and wetter conditions require more planning.

Whatever the season, layers and waterproofs are essential. Irish weather can change quickly, and that's as true in July as it is in January.
